Supply Chain Attack

Shai-Hulud Themed Malware Found in PyTorch Lightning

Directly applies to you

Researchers at Semgrep have uncovered a Shai-Hulud-style worm hiding inside PyTorch Lightning, the AI training library used in countless model pipelines. The compromise plants a self-propagating payload that scrapes credentials and chains forward to other packages developers maintain.

This is the same family of worm we saw tear through npm last autumn, now adapted for the Python ecosystem and aimed squarely at AI workloads. PyTorch Lightning is depended on by everything from research notebooks to production training clusters. If your training environments pull from public PyPI without pinning or signature checks, assume exposure. The payload exfiltrates GitHub PATs, npm tokens, and cloud credentials it finds in the runner, then republishes its loader into other packages the developer owns.

What to do

Audit any dev or CI runners that touched pytorch-lightning in the last 14 days. Rotate PATs and cloud keys for engineers who train models. Pin AI dependencies to known-good SHAs and require provenance checks. Brief the AI/ML team today, not after the weekend.

Surveillance

How Mark Klein Told the EFF About Room 641A

A new MIT Press book excerpt revisits the AT&T technician who walked into the Electronic Frontier Foundation in 2006 with documents proving the NSA had spliced a fibre tap into the public internet. Twenty years on, the story still defines the privacy fight.

Klein's quiet act of documentation, room-by-room diagrams, splitter specifications, internal memos, became the spine of Hepting v. AT&T and the long civil liberties argument that followed. The piece is a reminder that the surveillance backbone he exposed never went away. The legal architecture around it merely shifted. For anyone designing data-handling controls in 2026, Room 641A is the worked example of why network-level interception assumptions belong in your threat model.

Connected Vehicles

Can I Disable All Data Collection From My Vehicle?

Rivian's own support article, surfacing on the front page after a viral thread, confirms what privacy researchers have argued for years: telemetry from a modern vehicle cannot be fully turned off. Some collection is structural to how the car functions.

The article walks through every consent toggle Rivian offers and ends, politely, in the same place every connected-car privacy review has ended this decade. Location, drive data, and diagnostic streams keep flowing because over-the-air updates, safety recalls, and warranty obligations rest on them. The honesty is welcome. The implication is not. Anyone managing executive protection or fleet privacy should treat connected vehicles as continuous-collection devices and plan accordingly.

Disclosure Follow-Up

Follow-Up to Carrot Disclosure: Forgejo

Directly applies to you

Security researcher dustri publishes the Forgejo half of the Carrot disclosure chain, detailing how the self-hosted Git forge handled coordination, patching, and the awkward downstream conversation with Gitea.

Forgejo is increasingly the choice for teams who want a Git server they can actually run themselves, and its disclosure handling here is being held up as a model for small-but-critical open source projects. The post walks through the timeline, the scope of the fix, and the version cut-off. If you self-host source code, this is the kind of post you read end-to-end. The pattern (researcher discloses, fork patches first, upstream catches up) matters for any CISO who relies on community-maintained infrastructure.

What to do

Confirm your Forgejo or Gitea instance is on the patched release. If you host code review on either platform, add the project's security feed to your watchlist.

Vulnerability Coordination

For Linux Kernel Vulnerabilities, There Is No Heads-Up to Distributions

Directly applies to you

An openwall thread crystallises a long-running tension in kernel security: distributions get no advance notice of fixes. Patches land in mainline, then it is a race between defenders, downstream packagers, and exploit writers reading the same commit log.

The kernel team's position is that early notification creates more risk than it prevents. Distribution maintainers argue that the current model means a window where embedded systems, long-tail kernels, and enterprise distros sit unpatched while the fix is already public. Both sides have reasonable arguments. Neither side is going to move first. For practitioners this means kernel CVE response stays expensive, and the time-to-patch metric on Linux fleets is not going to shorten through coordination, only through automation.

What to do

Push your Linux fleet onto live-patching where the kernel and licence allow it. Treat kernel upgrade SLAs as a tracked KPI rather than a best-effort activity.

Open Models

Granite 4.1: IBM's 8B Model Matching 32B MoE

IBM's Granite 4.1 release lands a dense 8B-parameter model that holds its own against larger mixture-of-experts architectures on coding and tool-use benchmarks, while being small enough to run on a single high-memory GPU.

The interesting story is not the leaderboard line. It is that an enterprise-licensed, openly weighted model from IBM is now in the size envelope where a security team can actually self-host inference for sensitive workflows. For organisations that have hit a wall on sending logs, code, or customer data to a third-party endpoint, Granite 4.1 quietly becomes one of the more practical options. Tooling around it is still rough compared to the frontier labs, but the licence and the size are right.

CVE-2026-41940

cPanel and WHM Authentication Bypass

Directly applies to you

watchTowr Labs publishes a deep technical writeup of CVE-2026-41940, an unauthenticated bypass affecting the cPanel and WHM control plane that powers a significant share of the world's small-business hosting.

The flaw lets an attacker reach administrative endpoints without credentials. The post walks through the root cause, the patched diff, and provides indicators of compromise. cPanel's exposure is unusually broad: hosting providers, agencies running customer sites, and a long tail of self-hosted SMBs. If you have any cPanel surface in your estate or in a vendor's, the next 48 hours matter.

What to do

Inventory cPanel and WHM instances across owned and vendor estates. Apply the vendor patch and rotate any keys or sessions that traversed the bypassed paths. Hunt for the IOCs published by watchTowr.

Web Standards

Mozilla's Opposition to Chrome's Prompt API

Mozilla has formally registered opposition to Google's proposed Prompt API, which would expose a browser-resident LLM to any web page through a simple JavaScript call. The issue thread is a clear-eyed governance argument.

Mozilla's position is that bolting non-deterministic, locale-and-version-dependent model output into a stable web API breaks the contract the platform is meant to provide. Sites would silently get different behaviour from different users, depending on what model their browser ships with. That is a developer experience problem, an accessibility problem, and a privacy attack surface in one. The technical points are fair. The political read is that the platform is fragmenting on AI features and standards bodies are catching up months behind.

AI and Memory

Opus 4.7 Knows the Real Kelsey

Directly applies to you

An Argument essay on what it means when a frontier model already has a settled, opinionated picture of who you are, before the conversation begins. The author keeps trying to talk to a clean slate, and finds there is no clean slate left.

The piece sits with a question that anyone integrating Claude or its peers into a working life will recognise. The model knows things about you it has gleaned from training corpora, from prior sessions, from the long shadow of being a public person on the internet. You can reset a chat. You cannot reset what the model has internalised about you across versions. For practitioners building agentic workflows on top of these systems, it is a sober prompt to think carefully about persona, identity, and the boundary between user input and the model's own priors.

What to do

Document, for any agentic workflow you are deploying, what the model is allowed to assume about identities it encounters and how that assumption is bounded. Treat model priors as input data, with provenance.
