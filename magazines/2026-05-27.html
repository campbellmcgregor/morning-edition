Digital Sovereignty
Directly applies to you

Netherlands blocks US takeover of vital digital supplier

The Dutch government has invoked its new investment-screening powers to halt the sale of a strategic digital infrastructure provider to a US buyer, marking the most assertive European intervention yet in tech supply-chain control.

This is not subtle. The Netherlands is signalling that any non-EU acquirer of an asset deemed critical to Dutch digital sovereignty will now face the same scrutiny that defence and energy deals get. The wider context is the EU's Foreign Direct Investment Screening Regulation finally being used the way Brussels intended: as a brake on the long American shopping spree across European cloud, hosting, and communications providers. Expect Germany and France to test the same lever within the next two quarters.

For UK organisations the read is sharper than it looks. Many Scottish and London firms route data through Dutch-owned infrastructure precisely because it sits inside the EU but outside the GDPR-as-political-weapon zone. If Dutch screening pulls a supplier out from under a contract, or imposes change-of-control conditions, every downstream customer feels it. Cross-check your top fifteen processors against their ultimate parent and whether they sit on the Dutch designated-critical list.

What to do

Pull the parent-company and beneficial-ownership data for every Tier 1 and Tier 2 cloud, hosting, and SaaS supplier this week. Flag any whose ultimate parent is incorporated in the Netherlands, Germany, or France for a follow-up question to procurement: what happens if a national screening intervention forces divestiture or restricts cross-border data flow. Add a clause to your next renewal cycle requiring the supplier to disclose any active foreign-investment review.

Infrastructure Transparency

Erin Brockovich made a map to track data centers around the country

Brockovich, the environmental advocate, has built a public map plotting every confirmed and rumoured US data centre against local water tables, power draw, and groundwater contamination filings. It is the closest thing to an FOIA for hyperscale.

The deliberate point of the map is that this data was always available, just deliberately fragmented. Utility filings, county zoning approvals, EPA discharge permits, and state economic-development press releases never get pulled into the same view because no single regulator has the mandate. Brockovich's team did the joining manually, and the resulting picture is uncomfortable: communities are hosting facilities the size of a small town's grid load with almost no idea what is being trained inside them.

This is a surveillance-of-the-surveillers story. For the privacy community it gives a rare lever — concrete coordinates and capacity numbers to weigh against the vague sustainability claims that hyperscalers publish in their ESG reports. Expect this template to be copied in Ireland, the Netherlands, and the Highlands within months, particularly as Scottish and Welsh planning authorities grapple with the next wave of speculative AI-data-centre applications.

Payments Fraud

Stripe is friendly to “friendly fraud”

A merchant publishes a forensic walkthrough showing how Stripe's chargeback flow now systematically favours the cardholder even when documentary evidence proves delivery and acceptance — and how the platform's own dashboard nudges the merchant to give up.

The pattern in the post is mechanical. A customer disputes a charge with their bank, Stripe places the funds on hold, the merchant uploads receipts and signed acceptance, and Stripe writes the response in a way that the issuer reads as weak. The merchant loses. Multiply this across a small SaaS book and a single bad month of disputes can wipe a quarter. The post also documents Stripe's tightened evidence templates, which silently shrank the space for free-text rebuttals over the last year.

For security and finance leads this is more than an accounting headache. Friendly-fraud rings recycle stolen cards and good-customer accounts in alternating waves to keep dispute rates below platform-action thresholds. If your fraud team is sized for the bank-issued fraud rate but not for the Stripe-mediated chargeback rate, the two are diverging fast. The author's data shows merchant win-rates on legitimate disputes have dropped to single digits on some categories.

Build Pipelines

I Bypassed Adobe and Microsoft to Build a Git-Tracked Book Production Pipeline

An author rebuilds a full book-production pipeline — manuscript, layout, proofing, print-ready PDF — in plain text under Git, replacing the Adobe and Microsoft toolchain with Pandoc, LaTeX, and a handful of CI workflows.

The pitch is dry but the implications are not. Production pipelines that produce a single binary artefact (a book, a regulatory filing, a board pack, a contract bundle) almost always live in proprietary desktop tools because that is where the templates and the tracked changes live. The author's piece is a careful demonstration that the entire workflow — including footnoted citations, ligature-aware typography, and ISBN-correct PDFs — can be reproduced from a Git history, with each commit a defensible audit point.

That last bit is what makes this interesting beyond hobby publishing. Any regulated document — a SOC 2 report, a legal opinion, an annual report — has the same shape: many authors, version drift, last-minute legal review, single sealed PDF. A Git-tracked pipeline gives you a real audit trail and makes the document reproducible from source. The toolchain is finally mature enough that the hardest part is convincing the lawyers, not making LaTeX behave.

AI Research

A sleep-like consolidation mechanism for LLMs

Researchers propose an offline consolidation phase for large language models, modelled on biological sleep, that lets a model replay and compress recent interactions into long-term weights without catastrophic forgetting.

The mechanism is more elegant than the marketing summary suggests. During an online session the model logs its activations on novel inputs to a small buffer. Between sessions, an offline pass replays the buffer through a slow-learning auxiliary network, then merges the resulting updates back into the base model through a low-rank adapter. The headline claim is that the consolidated model retains the new behaviour without measurable degradation on standard benchmarks, which is the table that catastrophic-forgetting papers normally die on.

If the result generalises beyond the paper's setup it changes the economics of continual fine-tuning. Today's frontier labs treat post-training as a quarterly batch process because the cost of retraining without forgetting is high. A sleep-like consolidation that runs nightly on a single host would let an organisation maintain a model that genuinely tracks its own data without exporting it to a vendor for retraining. The follow-up to watch is whether the consolidated weights are stable enough to ship to inference clusters without re-evaluation.

AI Economics
Directly applies to you

Outsourcing plus local AI will soon become more economical vs. frontier labs

An argument, with numbers, that the cost curve for a hybrid model — local inference for the bulk of tokens plus selective outsourcing of hard tasks to a frontier API — has already crossed under the cost of routing everything through OpenAI or Anthropic.

The piece does the unit-economics work that most local-AI advocacy skips. It separates inference cost by task class, models the latency tax of round-tripping to a frontier API, and prices in the engineering cost of running and updating a local fleet. The conclusion is narrow but defensible: for a workload dominated by routine generation and retrieval with occasional reasoning, a local 70B-class model handling eighty per cent of traffic and a frontier API handling the rest now costs roughly half of frontier-only, even after the engineering overhead.

For anyone building Claude- or Cowork-based workflows this is the crossover point to watch. It does not mean abandoning the frontier — the analysis explicitly relies on a frontier model for the hard twenty per cent — but it does mean the architecture decision is no longer trivially "call the best API." The next test is whether agent workflows, which are far more sensitive to model capability per step, hold up under the same partition.

What to do

Take your highest-volume Cowork or Claude Code workflow and instrument it for token mix and task class over a week. If the hard-reasoning tail is under a quarter of calls, you have a candidate for a hybrid setup. Spin up an Ollama or vLLM host with a 70B-class open model and run the routine traffic against it for a comparison weekend. Keep Anthropic on the reasoning calls and measure whether the agent behaviour holds before committing.

Knowledge Commons

Big tech's anti-labor playbook has come for Wikipedia

A former Wikimedia staffer argues that the same anti-labour tactics that broke unions inside the big platforms are now being applied to Wikipedia's volunteer editor community, with AI scraping as the lever.

The piece traces a careful pattern. As frontier AI labs began ingesting Wikipedia at scale, the Foundation's response shifted from defending the volunteer corps to monetising the relationship — first through enterprise data feeds, then through governance changes that made volunteer dissent harder to organise. Editors who pushed back on the speed of AI partnerships found themselves answering to new conduct procedures rather than to the community. The author frames this as a soft capital strike: the platform extracts value, the labour force is renegotiated downward, and the user-facing product stays free.

The substantive question is what an open knowledge commons looks like when the dominant downstream consumer is not a human reader but a training pipeline. Wikipedia's social contract was built on the premise that editors and readers were roughly the same people. If the readers are now models, the editors are doing free quality control for a corporate input. The governance changes the post documents are the leading edge of that renegotiation, and they will inform every other open-data project sooner than people expect.

Identity & Trust

Are we self-sovereign PKI yet?

A status check on the long project of moving public-key infrastructure off the centralised certificate-authority model, with a clear-eyed look at what self-sovereign approaches actually buy you and where they still leak trust.

The author resists the temptation to declare victory. The piece walks through the current state of decentralised identifiers, verifiable credentials, and the small but real production deployments of self-sovereign systems in domains like supply-chain provenance and academic credentials. The honest finding is that the cryptography is fine, the wallet and revocation story is improving, and the actual obstacle is the same as it has been for fifteen years: nobody wants to be their own root of trust at scale.

The interesting nuance is that hybrid models — where a self-sovereign credential is anchored to a traditional CA for fallback verification — are quietly winning. They give you the audit-friendly revocation that pure decentralised systems still struggle with, while letting the holder present claims without a third-party round trip. If you are looking at machine-to-machine identity for a fleet of agents, this is the architectural pattern to watch over the next year.

Energy & Infrastructure

Power bills more than 250 per cent higher near data centres

Residential power bills in postcodes adjacent to large data centres are running more than two and a half times the regional average, according to new utility-data analysis — and the gap is widening as AI-driven facilities come online.

The analysis pulls hourly utility data and matches it against the construction-completion dates of named hyperscale facilities. The headline number is striking but the underlying mechanic is the boring one: data centres lock in long-term, discounted industrial tariffs negotiated as part of their planning permission, and the cost of the new transmission capacity required to serve them is socialised across all residential customers on the same grid segment. Local residents pay for the wires; the operator pays the negotiated rate for the electrons.

The political consequence is already visible. Several US state legislatures are pushing for direct cost-allocation rules that would make operators pay for the marginal capacity they require. The same fight is coming to the UK and Ireland — National Grid's recent connection-queue reform was a first step. Watch whether the next planning decisions in Aberdeenshire and South Dublin treat data centres as anchor employers or as a regressive cost shift, because the framing in 2026 will set policy for a decade.

Agent Evaluation

DeepSWE: A contamination-free benchmark for long-horizon coding agents

A new benchmark for coding agents tackles the contamination problem by drawing its tasks from a continuously generated stream of fresh, private repositories — so models cannot have seen the answers during training.

The benchmark's structure is the interesting part. Existing coding-agent benchmarks lean on SWE-bench or HumanEval, both of which now appear in pre-training corpora to varying degrees. DeepSWE solves this by partnering with private codebases that grant time-limited access to specific bug fixes the agent must reproduce, then rotating the task pool every two weeks. The result is a leaderboard that should, in principle, reflect actual capability rather than memorisation.

The honest read is that this is one good idea bolted onto a hard governance problem. The contamination-free claim depends entirely on the integrity of the partner codebases and the rotation cadence. If the benchmark becomes commercially important, the incentive to leak a few tasks early will rise. Still, the team has published their selection process and rotation schedule openly, which is more than the dominant agent evaluations do. For anyone choosing between Claude Code, Cursor, and Cline this quarter, this is the benchmark to track instead of the marketing tables.
