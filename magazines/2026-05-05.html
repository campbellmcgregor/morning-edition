Critical Vulnerability
Directly applies to you

Microsoft Edge stores all passwords in memory in clear text, even when unused

Browser memory dumps reveal Edge keeping plaintext credentials resident long after autofill events, a finding that recasts every Edge install on a managed Windows estate as a credential-theft vector.

The proof-of-concept is unsettling in its simplicity. Sign in to a site, close the tab, and Edge still holds the password as a UTF-16 string in heap memory hours later. Any process running with the same user privileges, including signed-but-malicious add-ins or post-exploitation tooling like Mimikatz variants, can scrape the lot without ever touching the encrypted password store. Microsoft's Trust Boundary documentation has long argued that local user privilege already grants access to credentials, but that defence does not survive contact with modern endpoint reality where browser extensions and standard-user applications are explicitly assumed hostile.

For CISOs, this is a shape that should look familiar after the LSASS years: a process holding cleartext secrets that any peer can read once attacker code reaches user-mode. Edge ships with default Windows builds and is the standard browser in most managed enterprises, including UK public sector and NHS estates. Until Microsoft confirms either remediation or a Defender ASR rule, treat Edge memory as compromised at the same trust level as the password manager itself, and prioritise endpoint EDR coverage that detects memory-region scraping on browser processes.

What to do

Audit which user populations rely on Edge's built-in password manager, particularly any group with privileged access. Push them to a dedicated password manager (1Password, Bitwarden) and disable Edge autofill via Group Policy. Add a detection for cross-process VirtualQueryEx/ReadProcessMemory against msedge.exe in your EDR ruleset and watch Microsoft's MSRC advisories for a CVE before patch Tuesday.

Surveillance & Privacy

Pulitzer Prize Winner in International Reporting

Five reporters won the Pulitzer for International Reporting on the global trade in location data and surveillance tooling, the strongest signal yet that data-broker accountability has crossed from niche concern to mainstream civic risk.

Dake Kang, Garance Burke, Byron Tau, Aniruddha Ghosal and Yael Grauer earned the prize for sustained reporting that traced how commercially harvested location data flows from consumer apps to intelligence agencies, military buyers and authoritarian regimes. The work draws a continuous line between a free flashlight app on a teenager's phone and the targeting packages used in conflict zones, and it lands at a moment when European DPAs are sharpening rules against the same supply chain.

The award matters beyond journalism. Pulitzer recognition reframes the data-broker industry from a privacy-team headache into a governance issue that boards, regulators and procurement teams will be asked about. Expect harder questions about what your own marketing tech, mobile SDKs and analytics partners are doing with telemetry, and expect those questions to come not just from your DPO but from the audit committee. The reporting also gives privacy advocates citable, peer-validated source material for legislative work in the UK Data Use and Access regime.

Data Protection Failure
Directly applies to you

US healthcare marketplaces shared citizenship and race data with ad tech giants

Healthcare.gov and the state-run ACA marketplaces transmitted citizenship status, race, sexual orientation and household income to ad tech intermediaries including Google, Meta and LinkedIn, exposing tens of millions of Americans through tracking pixels embedded in the enrolment funnel.

TechCrunch's investigation, working with the Markup-style methodology of replaying the consumer flow under instrumentation, found that the federal exchange and at least eleven state marketplaces leaked answers from the SEP eligibility questionnaire directly into third-party tag managers. The fields included whether a user identified as LGBTQ, whether they were a recently incarcerated person seeking coverage, and whether they had immigration status questions. HHS confirmed the pixels and said it was investigating; civil rights groups have already filed FTC complaints arguing the disclosures violate Section 5 and HIPAA's contemporaneous-marketing prohibitions.

The pattern is depressingly familiar. Marketing teams add a tag for attribution. Nobody flags that the form fields the tag captures include protected categories. Vendor risk reviews focus on contracts rather than runtime behaviour. UK and EU CISOs should not assume distance protects them; the same Tealium, Tag Manager and Pendo configurations sit on NHS Digital, DWP and devolved administration services. The regulatory exposure here under the UK GDPR Article 9 special-category rules would be larger, not smaller, than the US picture.

What to do

Run a tag and pixel inventory across every public-facing service that collects health, immigration, sexuality or financial-vulnerability data. Replay the actual consumer journey under a network proxy rather than trusting the consent management report. Confirm that special-category fields are masked or stripped before any third-party beacon fires. If you cannot confirm this in 24 hours, disable the relevant tags and reinstate after audit.

AI Tooling

Agent Skills

Addy Osmani's tour of agent skills as a primitive lands at the moment Anthropic, OpenAI and the open-source ecosystem are converging on the same mental model, and it doubles as a useful map for anyone running Cowork or Claude Code in production.

Osmani argues that skills are the unit of reusable capability for an agent, sitting between brittle prompt templates and full autonomous tool use. A skill bundles a description, a triggering rubric and the supporting files an agent needs to do one thing well, then gets surfaced only when context demands it. The piece walks through how this changes the design pressure on developer tooling: the bottleneck stops being context window and starts being skill curation, discovery and trust. He is generous about implementations across Anthropic's Claude skills, OpenAI's evolving function libraries and the emerging marketplace pattern.

For practitioners building on Cowork or Claude Code today, the practical takeaway is that skill libraries are about to be governed the way npm packages are. Provenance, signing, version pinning and isolation become first-class concerns. The piece does not get into the security model in depth but the implication is clear: a skill that runs with your tool permissions is a privilege boundary, not a configuration file. Anyone shipping skills internally should be modelling them as code with code-review, not as documentation with a YAML header.

What to do

Treat your installed skills as a software bill of materials. Inventory which skills are active in your Cowork and Claude Code setups, pin versions where the marketplace allows it, and prefer first-party Anthropic skills for anything that touches credentials, files or the shell. Add skill names to your weekly prompt-injection threat review.

Research & Analysis

What I'm Hearing About Cognitive Debt (So Far)

Margaret Storey's follow-up gathers the working hypotheses, dissents and emerging definitions around the cognitive debt that AI-augmented software development appears to be accruing inside teams.

The original cognitive debt frame argued that delegating reasoning to copilots leaves engineers with shallower mental models of their own systems, and that the debt compounds the way technical debt does. The follow-up post is more measured: Storey synthesises responses from researchers, engineering leaders and developers themselves, and finds the picture is genuinely contested. Some teams report deeper understanding because AI lowers the cost of exploration; others see juniors who can ship features without being able to debug them. The honest answer is that the data is qualitative, the effects appear bimodal, and the moderating variable is whether the human stays in the loop on intent rather than just review.

What lifts this above hot-take territory is the reading list and the framing it gives managers. Storey points at instrumentation we do not yet have, including ways to measure the depth of an engineer's mental model independent of their output. For organisations adopting agentic workflows aggressively, the practical implication is to budget for deliberate skill maintenance, not assume that productivity gains are free. The post is also useful as ammunition for engineering leaders pushing back on naive headcount-replacement narratives from the executive layer.

AI Infrastructure

How OpenAI delivers low-latency voice AI at scale

OpenAI's engineering team published an unusually candid look at the latency budget behind the Realtime API, including the choices that made interruption handling, audio buffering and model serving feel conversational rather than walkie-talkie.

The headline number is sub-300ms median round-trip for speech-to-speech, achieved by collapsing the traditional ASR-to-LLM-to-TTS pipeline into a single multimodal model and pushing inference onto bespoke serving infrastructure. The post is detailed about the bits engineers usually cannot get to: how the speech encoder chunks audio, how voice activity detection is decoupled from generation so the model can plan its own interruptions, and how the team manages tail latency when the model decides to think for longer than the budget allows. There is also a useful section on what they had to give up, including some output diversity, to keep p99 within bounds.

Beyond the engineering gawk factor, the post matters because it sets the bar that every other voice-AI provider will be measured against in 2026. Anthropic, Google and the open-source efforts now have a public spec to beat. For anyone building a voice assistant on top of these APIs, the operational implications are the more interesting read: budget for jitter that comes not from your network but from the model's planning loop, and design your conversational UX assuming interruption is reliable rather than aspirational.

Supply Chain Vulnerability
Directly applies to you

Securing a DoD contractor: Finding a multi-tenant authorization vulnerability

Strix.ai disclosed a zero-auth multi-tenant authorization vulnerability in a DoD-backed startup, the kind of finding that quietly defines how supply-chain risk now travels through the small-vendor layer of national security work.

The write-up walks through the discovery: the vendor exposed a tenant-aware API where the authorization check trusted a tenant ID supplied in the request body rather than derived from the bearer token. Any authenticated user could enumerate other tenants and pull data, including sensitive operational records belonging to defence customers. The class of bug is unremarkable. The interesting part is the procurement context. The vendor is on a sole-source path with multiple programme offices, which means the same flaw was riding through the same authorities-to-operate paperwork into multiple downstream environments without independent technical assessment.

This is the supply-chain story that does not get covered. Not a SolarWinds-scale event, but the steady accumulation of small vendors with VC pressure to ship, government customers with no time to audit, and authorisation models that treat the tenant claim as truth. UK readers should recognise the pattern in MoD's accelerated digital procurement and in NHS England's third-party app gateway. The right defensive question is not whether your vendors have SOC 2, but whether your IdP issues tokens that bind tenant identity at the token layer rather than relying on the application to behave.

What to do

If you operate or buy multi-tenant SaaS, ask your vendor to demonstrate that tenant identity is bound to the access token, not derived from request context. Add a Burp or ZAP test to your annual review that swaps tenant IDs in API calls between two test accounts. Flag any vendor that cannot produce evidence within 30 days. For your own internal services, audit any tenant_id parameter that arrives in a request body.

Energy Transition

Heat pump sales rise across Europe

European heat pump sales rose 17% in Q1 as gas and electricity prices realigned in favour of electrification, the first quarter in two years where the policy and the unit economics pointed the same direction.

PV Magazine's analysis credits a combination of factors: gas prices stayed elevated through a colder-than-forecast winter, several member states finalised heat pump grant frameworks, and installer capacity in Germany and the Netherlands finally caught up with order books that had been backed up since 2024. The standout markets were France and Italy, where the rebate-plus-zero-VAT structure made the like-for-like swap from a condensing boiler genuinely cheaper over a five-year horizon. The UK lagged the Continental average, but the figure for England and Wales still rose from the very low base of 2025.

The wider read is that the European energy transition is now a story about installation labour and grid headroom, not consumer willingness. The product is competitive on price, the policy is settled, and the question is whether DNOs can absorb domestic load shifting at the rate the kit is being installed. For technology buyers and infrastructure planners, the second-order story is data centre siting: every megawatt of electrified residential demand competes with the AI and cloud build-out for the same substations. Expect the planning fight to intensify through 2026.

Regulation & Governance
Directly applies to you

Kids bypass age verification with fake moustaches

Children are defeating the UK Online Safety Act's age-estimation checks with printed fake moustaches and screenshots from family albums, an early empirical verdict on a regulatory regime that staked its credibility on confidently identifying who is and isn't an adult online.

The Register reports field tests by researchers and, charmingly, by teenagers themselves, in which the major age-estimation providers used by porn sites, social platforms and gambling services were tricked by paper props, makeup and recycled photographs of older relatives. Some platforms accepted a still image; others required a brief video, which a slow head turn defeated about as easily. Ofcom's response leans on the language of accuracy thresholds and risk-based assurance, but the practical effect today is that the regime imposes friction on adults while being routinely subverted by the cohort it was designed to protect.

The story matters for UK CISOs and product teams beyond its comic value. Any platform serving UK users now sits inside a compliance regime where the regulator will likely escalate technical requirements as failure is documented, which means age-assurance vendor selection is about to become an accountable, audited choice rather than a procurement convenience. The same dynamic will play out in the EU under the Digital Services Act. Choose vendors who publish their false-acceptance rates and who can prove independent testing, not just those with the slickest sales motion.

What to do

If you operate a UK-facing service that requires age assurance, ask your vendor for current independent FAR/FRR figures against adversarial inputs, not just demographic accuracy. Document the basis for your selection in writing now; Ofcom enforcement is going to ask. If you do not yet have an age-assurance posture, treat this as a 60-day priority and consult your DPO on the proportionality test under section 12 of the Act.

Tech & Society

Stop big tech from making users behave in ways they don't want to

An Economist by-invitation essay argues that the next wave of platform regulation should target the behaviour designs that nudge users into actions they later regret, rather than the speech or content that has dominated the debate since 2018.

The author's case is that two decades of content-moderation policy has run its course because the harm has migrated. The damage is no longer in what is said but in how interfaces are tuned to extract attention, predict capitulation and route users into actions that fail their own stated preferences. Endless scroll, dark-pattern unsubscribe flows, gambling-style reward variability and friction-free in-app purchases are presented as the proper unit of regulation, with a comparison to consumer protection law and food safety as precedents for regulating design rather than expression.

The piece will divide readers along familiar lines. Free-speech advocates will note that interface regulation slips back into product control quickly. Industry voices will call this paternalism. The interesting middle position is that the EU's Digital Services Act already encodes some of this thinking in its dark-patterns provisions, and the UK's incoming Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act gives the CMA broader investigative powers to act on exactly the design surfaces the essay names. Whether or not the framing wins on op-ed pages, regulators are already moving in this direction.
