Supply Chain Compromise
Directly applies to you

Postmortem: TanStack NPM supply-chain compromise

TanStack has published a postmortem on the npm supply-chain compromise that hit its packages, walking through the attacker's path, the blast radius for downstream consumers, and the controls that did and did not hold during the incident.

The postmortem is unusually candid for an open-source ecosystem incident. The attacker compromised a maintainer credential, then pushed malicious versions of widely-installed TanStack packages, the kind of dependency a CISO would not even see on a typical SBOM scan until well after the fact. TanStack walks through the timeline, the indicators of compromise, and the developer-workflow gaps that let a credential become a publish key. For anyone running a Node or React shop, the named versions and dates are the artefact to pull.

What makes this one worth your morning is the pattern, not the package. Every credible supply-chain attack in the last two years has rhymed: a single human factor, a CI/CD pipeline that trusts publishers more than packages, and a window of hours before anyone notices. The mitigations TanStack lists (provenance attestations, mandatory 2FA on publish, pinned dependencies) are not new. They are the things every Node-shop CISO has on the roadmap and has not yet finished. Treat this as the nudge.

What to do

Pull the affected TanStack package list from the postmortem and run it against your SBOM and lockfiles today. If you ship anything React-adjacent, assume the dev team has TanStack Query in there somewhere. Confirm npm provenance and 2FA enforcement on every internal publisher account this week, not next quarter.

Surveillance & Privacy

Hardware Attestation as Monopoly Enabler

GrapheneOS argues that hardware attestation, often pitched as a security primitive, is being quietly repurposed into a monopoly mechanism that locks users out of running their own software on devices they own.

The piece is a sharp, technical read on a debate most security teams skirt. Hardware attestation lets a remote service verify a device is running an unmodified vendor stack. That is great for fraud and great for enterprise managed devices. It is also, GrapheneOS argues, increasingly used to gatekeep banking, payments, and government apps to a narrow list of approved operating systems, effectively pushing alternative Android distributions, custom ROMs, and degoogled phones out of the market entirely.

The framing matters because attestation is going to land on enterprise CISO desks soon, dressed up as a compliance win. Before you sign off on attestation-only access for a customer-facing product, ask whose problem it actually solves and which users it quietly excludes. The trade between fraud reduction and user autonomy is a real one. It is just rarely surfaced when a vendor sells the control.

Identity & Data Protection
Directly applies to you

Gmail registration now requires scanning a QR code and sending a text message

New Gmail account creation now requires users to scan a QR code and complete an SMS verification, escalating the personal data Google collects at signup and effectively closing the door on anonymous Gmail registration.

The change appears to be rolling out globally and was first surfaced on the Privacy Guides forum. A phone number alone is no longer sufficient; Google now binds new accounts to a specific device via QR scan plus SMS, which means anyone wanting a working Gmail address needs a phone they are willing to associate with that identity. For staff who maintain burner or research accounts, for journalists, for anyone outside the standard consumer profile, this is a meaningful narrowing.

For UK and EU organisations there is a second-order question. GDPR principles around data minimisation and lawful basis sit uncomfortably with mandatory device-binding at signup, especially when the alternative is no service. Expect this to surface in DPIAs and, eventually, regulator correspondence. In the meantime, if your incident-response playbook assumes you can spin up a fresh Gmail in five minutes for a forensic mailbox or a phishing test, that assumption no longer holds.

What to do

Audit any operational workflow that depends on creating fresh Gmail accounts on demand: red-team ops, phishing simulations, incident communication backups, vendor signups. Move those to a domain you control or to a provider that still permits low-friction registration. Update your DPIA template to flag mandatory device-binding as a category-specific risk.

Developer Tooling

CUDA-oxide: Nvidia's official Rust to CUDA compiler

Nvidia has released CUDA-oxide, an official Rust front end that compiles to CUDA, opening a sanctioned path for Rust developers to target Nvidia GPUs without leaving the type system behind.

This is the first time Nvidia has shipped a Rust toolchain under its own banner rather than blessing a community project. CUDA-oxide compiles Rust kernels directly to PTX and integrates with the broader CUDA ecosystem, which means Rust shops doing ML inference, simulation, or numerics can stop maintaining FFI shims and unsafe blocks just to call into CUDA. The repo is on nvlabs and the docs are up.

The strategic read is more interesting than the technical one. Nvidia rarely puts the official badge on something unless they expect serious adoption, and Rust has been quietly winning the ML-infra layer for the last two years. Expect framework authors to start offering Rust kernels alongside C++, and expect the security story for GPU workloads to improve incrementally: fewer hand-rolled unsafe pointers, more compile-time guarantees. Worth tracking even if you do not write CUDA today.

AI Strategy

Local AI needs to be the norm

An argument for treating local-first AI not as a niche enthusiast preference but as the default posture, with data sovereignty, latency, and cost all pushing in the same direction.

The author makes the case methodically. Cloud LLM inference is metered, observable, and increasingly subject to provider policy changes that ship without notice. Local inference on consumer-grade hardware has crossed the threshold where 30 to 70 billion parameter models run usefully on a developer workstation. The combination, the argument goes, makes local AI the right default for any task that does not strictly require a frontier model, with the cloud reserved for the genuinely hard problems.

The piece is opinionated and skips over real friction. Local fine-tuning is still painful, the tooling story is fragmented, and most enterprise audit frameworks have no model for an LLM that runs entirely on a laptop. But the direction of travel is correct. Anyone building AI-assisted internal workflows should be benchmarking local equivalents alongside the cloud option, both for the cost story and to keep an exit ramp open if a vendor decides to change the rules.

Anthropic & Claude
Directly applies to you

Claude Platform on AWS

Anthropic has launched Claude Platform on AWS, bundling Claude with native AWS integration, Bedrock-aligned billing, and enterprise controls aimed squarely at customers who want Claude inside their existing cloud footprint.

The announcement positions Claude Platform on AWS as a first-class deployment target rather than a Bedrock model reference. Practically that means VPC controls, AWS-native IAM, AWS-billed consumption, and the operational hooks enterprise security teams already trust. For organisations that have a hard time green-lighting a separate Anthropic contract, this removes one of the most cited blockers: the vendor surface stays AWS-shaped while the model is Claude.

The interesting bit is what this signals about Anthropic's enterprise distribution strategy. Going deeper on a single hyperscaler trades some neutrality for a much shorter path through procurement. For Cowork and Claude Code users running in shops that already standardise on AWS, expect the cleaner integration to make the internal conversation easier. For anyone in a GCP or Azure shop, the gap just got wider, and that is a conversation worth having with your account team.

What to do

If your organisation runs on AWS and you have been blocked from rolling out Claude Code or Cowork due to procurement, take this announcement to whoever owns your AWS Marketplace and Bedrock relationship. The bundled-billing and VPC story is the lever. If you run on a different cloud, ask Anthropic explicitly what equivalent integration looks like on your stack and timeline.

AI-Assisted Attacks
Directly applies to you

Google says criminal hackers used AI to find a major software flaw

Google has disclosed that criminal hackers used AI tooling to identify a serious software vulnerability before defenders did, marking one of the first publicly attributed cases of generative AI being used operationally for offensive vulnerability research.

The New York Times reporting cites Google researchers describing an incident where AI-assisted analysis surfaced a real, exploitable flaw that traditional fuzzing and review had missed. The vulnerability itself has been patched. The disclosure matters because it confirms what red teams have been demonstrating in controlled settings for eighteen months: that off-the-shelf AI tooling shifts the economics of vulnerability discovery in favour of whoever is willing to run the prompts at scale.

Defensive playbooks have not caught up. Most vulnerability-management programs are still calibrated for human-paced discovery, where you can roughly forecast the rate of new findings against an asset. AI-assisted offensive research compresses that timeline. The honest implication is that patch cadence, exposure reduction, and detection coverage all need to be reviewed against a faster attacker discovery cycle, not the historical one. Expect this story to be cited in every board deck for the next quarter.

What to do

Brief your security leadership and, where appropriate, your board on this disclosure within the next two weeks. Frame it as a credible inflection in attacker capability, not a one-off. Use it to reopen any deferred conversations about reducing time-to-patch on internet-facing assets and on prioritising attack-surface reduction over detection-only strategies.

European AI

AMÁLIA and the future of European Portuguese LLMs

AMÁLIA, a new model family focused on European Portuguese, makes the case that high-quality LLM coverage of smaller European languages requires deliberate national investment rather than waiting for the frontier labs to get around to it.

The post lays out the data and benchmarks behind AMÁLIA and is refreshingly honest about the gap between English-first frontier models and what is available for languages with fewer training tokens. Portuguese spoken in Portugal is treated by most large models as a noisy variant of Brazilian Portuguese, which produces predictably awkward output. The AMÁLIA team curated a Portuguese-specific corpus and fine-tuned to close that gap, with measurable improvements on local benchmarks.

The wider story is the one to watch. The EU AI Act's preference for sovereign capability is starting to produce concrete artefacts: national-language models, regional inference infrastructure, public funding for open-weights projects. Whether those projects converge into something competitive with frontier models or remain politely niche is the open question. Either way, the trend is real, and a CISO in a UK or EU shop should expect to see at least one of these named in a future procurement requirement.

AI Governance

A consistent pattern of lying': trial exposes what insiders think of Sam Altman

Guardian coverage of the Musk-versus-OpenAI trial pulls together internal correspondence in which colleagues describe Sam Altman as having a consistent pattern of lying, surfacing governance questions that have been an open secret in AI circles for years.

The article quotes from depositions, internal Slack messages, and emails introduced in evidence. The pattern witnesses describe (selective disclosure to the board, parallel narratives told to different stakeholders, and decisions retconned after the fact) is now part of the official trial record rather than rumour. Whatever you think of Musk's case on the merits, the evidentiary disclosures are independently interesting and will be cited for a long time.

For anyone making procurement, partnership, or board-level decisions involving OpenAI, the governance question is not theoretical anymore. The relevant due diligence is no longer whether the technology works; it is whether the organisation behind it can be relied on to keep commitments through a leadership transition or a strategic pivot. That is a different conversation, and it belongs in the same risk register as cloud concentration and data residency.

AI & The Craft

I'm going back to writing code by hand

A working developer's account of stepping back from AI-assisted coding after a year of heavy use, arguing that the long-run cost to skill, comprehension, and craft has not been honestly priced in by the people selling the tools.

The post is calm rather than reactionary. The author is not anti-AI; they used Copilot and Claude Code heavily and shipped real work. The argument is that the feedback loop of writing, debugging, and slowly understanding a system (the thing that builds engineers) gets short-circuited when the model fills in the blanks. Productivity stays high. Capability quietly decays. The post documents what they did about it and what they kept.

It is worth reading even if you disagree. The honest version of the AI-coding question is not whether the tools work, it is what they are doing to the people who use them every day, and what that means for hiring, mentoring, and the half-life of a senior engineer's skills. CISOs care about this because the security implications of code that no one fully understands are not abstract. Comprehension is a control. When you outsource it, you should know what you are trading away.
