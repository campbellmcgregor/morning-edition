Surveillance & Privacy

Supreme Court Says Geofence Warrants Require Constitutional Protections

The US Supreme Court has ruled that geofence warrants — which sweep up the location data of everyone near a crime scene — implicate the Fourth Amendment, forcing law enforcement to clear a probable-cause bar before mining the digital dragnet that Google and others quietly maintain.

For years, geofence warrants let police reverse the usual logic of a search: instead of naming a suspect, they named a place and a time, then demanded the identities of every device that passed through. The ruling reframes that as a search of the many to find the one, and says the Constitution has something to say about it. Companies sitting on dense location histories now have firmer legal footing to push back, and a clearer obligation to scrutinise the warrants they receive rather than rubber-stamping them.

The practical fallout lands on anyone who custodies location data at scale — ad-tech, mapping, fitness, retail footfall. A warrant that would have been routine last week may now be challengeable, and the volume of data retained becomes a liability rather than an asset. The decision will not end location surveillance, but it raises the cost and the paperwork, and it hands privacy teams a precedent worth citing. Expect data-minimisation arguments to get a fresh hearing in product reviews.

A US Supreme Court Ruling Just Blew Up EU–US Data Transfers

Privacy group noyb warns that the same bulk-surveillance reasoning undercuts the legal fiction propping up the EU–US Data Privacy Framework — the mechanism thousands of European companies rely on to lawfully send personal data to American cloud providers.

The Data Privacy Framework has always rested on an uneasy promise: that US surveillance law offers Europeans protection "essentially equivalent" to the GDPR. noyb's argument is that a fresh ruling on bulk data exposes how far that promise stretches, and hands Schrems-style litigants more ammunition to challenge transfers in European courts. We have been here before — Safe Harbor and Privacy Shield both collapsed — and the pattern suggests the current framework is one adverse judgment away from the same fate.

For a UK or EU organisation, this is not abstract. If the framework wobbles, every contract that quietly assumes "we can use a US SaaS vendor because DPF" needs a fallback: standard contractual clauses with a real transfer impact assessment, or EU data residency. The risk is not a fine tomorrow; it is discovering mid-incident that your lawful basis for moving data has evaporated and your vendor map runs entirely through Virginia.

What to do

Pull your data-transfer register and flag every processor that relies on the Data Privacy Framework as its sole legal basis. For the critical ones, line up standard contractual clauses plus a transfer impact assessment now, and ask your top US vendors what EU-residency options they offer — before a court forces the question.

One Million Passports Leaked Online

A trove of more than a million passport images and identity documents has spilled onto the open web, traced to age-verification and membership systems used by cannabis clubs and other venues that demanded a scan of government ID at the door.

The breach is a case study in the hidden cost of "just verify everyone's ID." Each venue that insisted on a passport scan became a custodian of the most sensitive identity document a person owns, usually with none of the security maturity that responsibility demands. The data reportedly sat in misconfigured storage, accessible to anyone who knew where to look — the boring, recurring failure mode behind a large share of mass exposures.

This is the tangible downside of the age-verification mandates spreading across the UK and EU. Every new law that requires a face or a document to access a service manufactures another honeypot, and the breach shows what happens when the honeypot leaks. For a security leader, the lesson is upstream: if a compliance requirement forces you to collect identity documents, the safest architecture is one where you verify and then never store — or hand the liability to a provider who actually can.

What to do

Inventory every place your organisation collects or retains government ID — onboarding, KYC, age gates, visitor sign-in. Delete what you do not need, move verification to a provider that returns a yes/no rather than the raw document, and confirm any retained scans are encrypted, access-logged, and on a deletion schedule.

Open Source Security

JumpServer: Open-Source Privileged Access Management

JumpServer offers a self-hostable bastion for privileged access — brokering SSH, RDP, database, and Kubernetes sessions through a single audited gateway, with recording, MFA, and just-in-time access, as an open alternative to expensive commercial PAM.

Privileged access is where breaches turn into catastrophes, and yet full-fat PAM products have long been priced and architected for large enterprises. JumpServer plants a flag in the gap: a credential vault, a session broker, and a recording layer that sit between your admins and your fleet, so that no human types a root password directly into a production box. Everything routes through the gateway, everything is logged, and access can be granted for a window and then revoked.

The appeal for a leaner security team is obvious — you get the audit trail and the choke point without a seven-figure contract. The caveats are equally real: a bastion is a high-value target, so it must be hardened, patched, and monitored like the crown jewels it guards, and self-hosting means you own that work. Evaluate it the way you would any single point of trust, but it is a credible starting point for teams that have outgrown shared SSH keys.

Software Assurance

What Can You Confidently Guarantee About Your Software?

An ACM Queue essay asks an uncomfortable question every engineering leader should sit with: stripped of optimism and marketing, what can you actually prove about the behaviour of the systems you ship — and what are you merely hoping is true?

The piece draws a sharp line between properties you can guarantee and properties you assume. Most teams, it argues, operate on a thick layer of assumption — that inputs are well-formed, that dependencies behave, that the happy path is the only path — and only discover the gap when production proves otherwise. Real guarantees come from constraints you can enforce and verify: types, invariants, tests that exercise the ugly cases, and architectures that make whole classes of failure impossible rather than merely unlikely.

For a security leader, the framing is a useful audit lens. "What can you confidently guarantee?" is precisely the question a regulator, a customer, or an incident reviewer will ask, and "we assumed it was fine" is not an answer that survives scrutiny. The discipline of separating the proven from the hoped-for maps neatly onto threat modelling: the assumptions you cannot defend are exactly where the attacker, or the outage, will be waiting.

Qwen 3.6 27B Is the Sweet Spot for Local Development

A widely shared review argues that Qwen 3.6 27B hits the practical sweet spot for running a capable coding model on your own hardware — strong enough to be genuinely useful, small enough to fit a single workstation GPU, and entirely free of the cloud.

The case is partly about economics and partly about control. A 27-billion-parameter model is large enough to handle real refactoring, code review, and agentic tasks, yet fits comfortably on consumer-grade silicon, which means no per-token bill and no rate limits. For developers who have felt the friction of metered frontier APIs, a competent local model that runs offline changes the calculus of what you can experiment with freely.

The more interesting angle for a security leader is data residency. A local model means prompts, source code, and proprietary context never leave the building — no third-party retention policy to audit, no transfer mechanism to justify, no vendor breach to inherit. For regulated work, or anything touching sensitive code, the ability to keep an AI assistant entirely on-premises is not a hobbyist novelty; it is a legitimate architectural option worth piloting alongside the hosted frontier tools.

What to do

Stand up Qwen 3.6 27B on a spare GPU workstation and trial it against a non-sensitive internal codebase to gauge real quality. If it holds up, it becomes your default for any work where source code or data must not leave the network — a private complement to cloud assistants, not a wholesale replacement.

Internet Governance

European ISPs Want Rightsholders Held Accountable for Overblocking

A coalition of European internet providers is pushing for rules that would make rightsholders liable when their site-blocking demands knock legitimate services offline — a pointed response to anti-piracy blocklists that routinely catch innocent domains in the dragnet.

Site-blocking has become a favoured tool of media and sports rightsholders, who obtain court orders compelling ISPs to null-route piracy domains. The problem is collateral damage: shared IP addresses, CDNs, and hastily assembled blocklists mean legitimate businesses regularly find themselves unreachable with no warning and little recourse. The ISPs' argument is one of accountability — if you demand the block, you should own the consequences when it goes wrong.

For anyone running infrastructure in Europe, this is a quiet operational risk worth understanding. A neighbour on a shared host, or a CDN edge that also serves a flagged domain, can drag your service into a blocklist you never appeared on. The push for liability would create both a deterrent against sloppy blocking and a route to redress. It also previews the broader fight over who bears the cost when automated enforcement meets the messy reality of shared internet plumbing.

Developer Tooling

A Native Graphical Shell for SSH

A new project reimagines the remote terminal as a native graphical surface rather than a stream of escape codes — letting an SSH session render rich, interactive UI elements while still running over the same trusted, encrypted channel admins already use.

The terminal protocol is a marvel of longevity and a museum of accumulated hacks; everything from colours to cursor movement is smuggled through arcane control sequences. This project asks what a remote shell would look like if the server could hand the client structured, graphical components instead — tables you can sort, output you can fold, controls you can click — while keeping SSH as the transport. It is a thoughtful attempt to modernise the experience without abandoning the security model that makes SSH trustworthy.

For security-minded operators, the appeal is keeping everything inside the SSH trust boundary rather than bolting on a separate web console with its own auth and attack surface. The caution is the usual one for any new richness over a privileged channel: more capability means more parsing, and more parsing means more places for bugs to hide. Worth watching as it matures, and worth a careful look at how it handles untrusted server output before it touches anything that matters.

Industrial Policy

South Korea to Spend $1T on Memory Chips and Humanoid Robots

South Korea has unveiled a trillion-dollar industrial bet, pouring state and corporate money into expanded memory-chip production and humanoid robotics — a strategic move to entrench its position at the foundation of the global AI hardware supply chain.

Memory is the unglamorous bottleneck of the AI boom: every accelerator needs vast quantities of high-bandwidth memory, and South Korea's manufacturers already dominate that market. A trillion-dollar commitment is a statement of intent to keep it that way, and to extend the same industrial muscle into robotics before that market consolidates. It is the kind of long-horizon, nation-scale capital allocation that Western economies have largely forgotten how to coordinate.

The supply-chain implications ripple outward. Concentration of critical hardware production in a single geography is a resilience question as much as an economic one, and AI infrastructure now sits squarely in the category of strategic dependency. For leaders thinking past the next quarter, the lesson is that the AI stack has a physical base, that base has a geography, and that geography is increasingly a matter of statecraft. Hardware availability, not model cleverness, may be the constraint that bites first.

Free Speech

A 30-Year Sentence for Transporting Zines

A man has been handed a thirty-year sentence connected to the transport of printed zines — a punishment so disproportionate to the act that civil-liberties writers are calling it a five-alarm fire for free expression and a chilling signal about where prosecutorial power is heading.

The details matter less than the precedent. A sentence of this magnitude attached to the movement of printed political material is the kind of outlier that, left unchallenged, becomes a tool. Free-speech advocates read it as deterrence by example: a punishment severe enough that the next person thinks twice not about a crime, but about the act of distributing ideas. The printed zine — cheap, analogue, hard to surveil — has always been a medium of dissent, which is precisely why its suppression carries weight.

For anyone who works in or around information and privacy, the case is a reminder that the threats to expression are not only technical. Encryption and anonymity tools matter, but so does the legal climate that decides what speech costs. A regime willing to impose decades of imprisonment for moving paper is one to watch closely, because the logic rarely stays confined to paper. It is the ethical undertow beneath every conversation about surveillance, attribution, and the right to communicate.
