Critical Vulnerability
Directly applies to you

Fabricked: Misconfiguring Infinity Fabric to Break AMD SEV-SNP

Researchers demonstrate that misconfigurations of AMD's Infinity Fabric interconnect can break the integrity guarantees of SEV-SNP, the feature that confidential-computing workloads on AMD EPYC silicon are explicitly meant to depend on.

AMD SEV-SNP is the trust anchor for the bulk of confidential virtual machines now sold by every major cloud, from Azure DCasv5 through to GCP's Confidential VMs and the AWS Nitro Enclave alternatives. The marketing pitch is that even a malicious hypervisor cannot read or tamper with guest memory. The Fabricked work shows that this guarantee can collapse when the platform's Infinity Fabric is configured outside the parameters AMD assumed, allowing an attacker with platform-level access to undermine the attestation chain that customers are bargaining for.

For anyone running regulated workloads on confidential VMs, this is the moment to read the firmware advisories rather than the press release. The interesting question is not whether AMD ships a microcode patch — it will — but how quickly cloud providers roll it and whether the attestation reports your tenants are checking still mean what they did last week. Treat any compliance claim that leans on SEV-SNP as provisional until your provider confirms platform remediation.

What to do

Pull the list of workloads you depend on SEV-SNP attestation for, even indirectly through a confidential-computing SaaS, and ask your cloud account team for a written timeline on patched firmware and updated attestation roots. If you signed customer contracts that name confidential computing as a control, get ahead of the disclosure conversation before procurement does.

Mozilla to UK regulators: VPNs are essential privacy and security tools

Mozilla files a sharply worded submission to UK regulators arguing that the current trajectory of Online Safety Act enforcement is treating VPNs as a workaround to be closed rather than as the everyday security control they actually are for businesses and individuals.

The political pressure on VPNs in the UK has been building for over a year, driven by the awkward fact that age-gating and content-blocking under the Online Safety Act collapses the moment a user installs Mullvad. Mozilla's letter pushes back firmly: VPNs are not a loophole, they are how remote workers connect to their employers, how journalists protect sources, and how anyone on hostile Wi-Fi keeps their session tokens out of someone else's hands.

For UK-based security leaders this is more than ideology. If Ofcom or DSIT moves towards treating consumer VPNs as suspicious by default, expect knock-on effects in app stores, in payment-processor risk scoring, and eventually in the kind of corporate VPN configurations that get flagged. The submission is worth reading in full because it lays out the exact language a CISO can borrow when their own legal or comms team asks how to respond to a regulator's questionnaire.

What to do

If your organisation operates in the UK, draft a one-page internal position on VPN use now: which corporate VPN you mandate, which categories of personal VPN you tolerate on company devices, and what you tell staff who ask whether they should keep using them privately. Send it to legal for review before regulators ask you the same question with less warning.

Directly applies to you

Reverse engineering Android malware from popular Chinese projectors

A hobbyist reverse-engineer walks through using Claude Code to dissect the Android firmware shipped on a popular brand of cheap Chinese projectors and finds the kind of telemetry, sideloading, and beacon behaviour that no one buying a home cinema gadget signed up for.

The post is two stories braided together. The first is the artefact: an Android-based projector marketed for living rooms that ships out of the factory phoning home, side-loading APKs from a CDN the owner cannot reach, and listening on ports that have no business being open on a device meant to display Netflix. The second is the method: Claude Code is being used as a credible junior analyst, unpacking the ROM, walking the smali, and explaining the function of each daemon in plain language. The line between a curious owner and a malware analyst is now a weekend project.

For a CISO, the practical lesson is procurement. Cheap projectors, conference-room cameras, and signage players all run the same kind of opaque Android builds, and they are very rarely on the asset register. They share Wi-Fi with the boardroom. The defensive answer is not to ban them but to put them on a guest VLAN, block egress to anywhere they do not need, and treat the meeting-room budget the way you would treat any other supplier you have never audited.

What to do

Pull the procurement records for any networked AV gear bought in the last 12 months. Confirm each device is on a segregated network with no path to your corporate VLAN and no outbound to anything except the vendor's documented update endpoints. Add a clause to procurement that AV hardware must list the firmware origin and update mechanism in writing.

Show HN: Semble – Code search for agents that uses 98% fewer tokens than grep

Semble is a code-search index designed not for humans but for coding agents, designed to give Claude Code, Cowork, and similar tools a structured way to find the right file without burning thousands of tokens on grep output.

The premise is sharp. Naive code search inside an agent loop is wasteful: grep returns hundreds of lines of incidental matches, the model has to read them all, and the cost per task balloons. Semble pre-indexes the repository with structural signals and exposes a search API that returns small, ranked, semantically meaningful chunks. The claim of 98% token reduction is the kind of number to verify on a real codebase, but the direction is correct and the implementation is open source.

If your team is already running Claude Code or Cowork in anger, the question is not whether to look at Semble but whether your current code-search story is silently inflating your token bill. A spike on a medium repository will tell you quickly. The wider story here is that the agent-tooling layer is maturing: we are now optimising the substrate underneath the model, not just prompting more cleverly. Expect more of these structural wins in the next six months.

What to do

Run Semble against one of your real repositories alongside whatever search tool your coding agent currently uses, and compare token spend on three representative tasks. If the gap is meaningful, write it up as an internal note before someone else on the team discovers it the hard way through a budget alert.

Practice & Craft

Don't Outsource the Learning

Addy Osmani argues that the convenience of AI-assisted coding is quietly eroding the deliberate practice that turns a junior engineer into a senior one, and offers a framework for using the tools without paying that price.

The piece is not a luddite refusal. Osmani is clearly using Claude and Copilot daily. His worry is more specific: that the moment of struggle, the half-hour of staring at a stack trace before something clicks, is where senior judgement is built, and outsourcing that moment to an agent rewards short-term throughput at the cost of long-term capability. The argument generalises beyond code: the same dynamic applies to anyone whose craft was previously sharpened by friction.

For leaders managing teams that lean on AI tooling, the actionable part is the framework. Use the agent for known, repetitive work; reserve novel problems for unaided practice; require people to explain in plain English the code the model wrote for them before they merge it. None of this is policy you can enforce with a linter, which is precisely why it matters. The teams that get this balance right will compound; the teams that do not will discover in 18 months that no one can debug anything that does not have a model in the loop.

AI Tooling

GenCAD

GenCAD presents a generative model trained to produce parametric CAD geometry rather than pixels, opening a credible path to text-to-mechanical-part workflows for engineers who have so far been ignored by the image-model wave.

Generative AI in physical design has mostly stalled on the same problem: pretty pictures are not manufacturable. GenCAD takes a different approach, generating the underlying CAD operations — sketches, extrudes, fillets — rather than the rendered output, which means the result is a real part you can edit, simulate, and send to a machinist. The demo gallery is encouraging without being miraculous: simple brackets and housings, not yet complex assemblies.

For a security leader the angle is procurement and IP. As these tools become useful, your mechanical engineering team will start pasting drawings into hosted models, and your supply chain will start receiving GenCAD-style outputs from vendors. The data-classification rules that apply to source code apply here too, just less obviously. Worth a five-minute chat with whoever owns engineering tooling before someone leaks a flagship product geometry into a free tier.

AI & Public Mood

University of Arizona students boo Eric Schmidt's AI cheerleading

Eric Schmidt's commencement address at the University of Arizona, framed as inspirational guidance about AI as a career engine, was met with audible booing from the graduates being addressed.

The substance of the speech was familiar Silicon Valley boilerplate: AI is inevitable, the graduates should embrace it, those who do not adapt will be left behind. The response was less familiar. Booing at a commencement is unusual; booing the keynote speaker over their framing of an industry technology is rarer still. The reporting suggests a cohort that has watched two years of layoffs, hollowed-out entry-level pipelines, and breathless model launches, and is no longer in the mood to be told this is good for them.

This matters operationally because the people booing are the next intake into your security teams, your engineering teams, and your data programmes. If your internal AI rollout speaks the same language as Schmidt's address, expect the same reception. The leaders quietly getting buy-in are the ones who treat AI tooling as a thing that needs to earn its place in a workflow, not a thing to be evangelised. Worth a quick audit of how your own AI announcements have been landing.

Security Tooling

Show HN: Mezz, a curl-able WiFi sandbox for IoT pentesting

Mezz is a small, scriptable sandbox that exposes WiFi networks for IoT and embedded testing via a curl-friendly HTTP interface, giving security teams a fast way to reproduce the kind of network conditions cheap consumer devices misbehave on.

The pitch is mundane in the best way. Pentesting IoT firmware usually means an awkward dance of physical access points, repurposed routers, and shell scripts that nobody on the team can read six months later. Mezz collapses all of that into a clean HTTP API: spin up a network with these characteristics, see what the device does, tear it down. It is not glamorous and it is exactly the kind of tool that quietly improves the quality of a red team's output.

The wider point is that the supply chain story from earlier in this edition needs a defensive counterpart. Every CISO with a meaningful AV, signage, or IoT estate should have someone whose job is to drop a representative device on a controlled network and watch what it does. Mezz makes that test cheaper to run, which means it becomes harder to justify not running it. Worth at least a spike.

Strategy & Governance

AI is a technology not a product

John Gruber argues that the framing of AI as a discrete product, sold and bought in branded boxes, fundamentally misunderstands what is happening, and that the companies treating AI as a technology layer underneath other products are quietly winning.

The argument is a useful corrective. Most of the noise in the industry is about which model is best this month, which assistant has the cleverest demo, which startup has the cleanest interface. Gruber's point is that none of this is how customers actually consume the thing. The interesting question is what new products become possible when language and reasoning are cheap utilities, and the companies that answer that question are not the ones running keynote events about their latest model release.

For a security leader this reframing changes procurement. If AI is a technology layer rather than a product, then your vendor inventory of AI is already incomplete: every SaaS tool you bought in the last twelve months has quietly absorbed a model into its workflow, and your DPA, your data classification, and your incident response playbooks need to account for that. A useful exercise this quarter is to ask every SaaS vendor in your top twenty by spend exactly which model they call, where, and on what data.

A Quiet Heresy

I don't think AI will make your processes go faster

A thoughtful counter-essay argues that the productivity narrative around AI mistakes individual task speed for organisational throughput, and that without rebuilding the surrounding process the headline gains evaporate.

The piece works through a specific example: a developer who can now write a feature in two hours instead of six, sitting inside a team where features still ship every two weeks because of review, QA, deploy windows, and meetings. The bottleneck was never the keystroke rate. The faster a process becomes locally, the more it exposes the slow steps elsewhere, and most organisations have not done the work to rethink those.

There is a quieter point underneath, which is that the metric most worth watching is not lines per day or tickets closed. It is whether the team has used the time freed at the keyboard to invest in the harder work: fixing tests, paying down debt, talking to customers. The teams that do compound. The teams that simply ship more average code at the same rhythm will look identical in twelve months to the teams that never introduced AI at all, only more tired.
