Encryption Under Threat
Directly applies to you

EU to Legislate Chat Control Behind Closed Doors

Patrick Breyer warns that the EU's mass-scanning regulation has been pulled out of public debate and into trilogue back rooms, where a watered-down compromise on client-side message scanning could be agreed with almost no scrutiny.

The Chat Control file refuses to die. After member states failed to reach a qualified majority on mandatory scanning, the proposal has shifted into closed-door trilogue negotiations, where Breyer argues the most dangerous outcome is now the most likely: a “voluntary” scanning regime made permanent, paired with pressure to scan end-to-end encrypted services anyway. The framing has changed from forcing providers to scan to letting them — but the practical effect on confidentiality is identical, and the democratic accountability is worse because the bargaining happens out of sight.

For anyone running services in or selling to the EU, this is not abstract. Client-side scanning breaks the security model of encrypted messaging by inserting an inspection point before encryption happens, which is exactly the capability every threat actor wants. A mandate — or even a durable “voluntary” norm — would force vendors to build that inspection point, and once it exists it becomes a target for abuse, scope creep, and foreign-intelligence interest. The relaunch of the fightchatcontrol.eu campaign is a signal that the window for public pressure is short.

What to do

If your organisation processes EU user communications, brief legal and product now on the client-side-scanning scenario so a position exists before any trilogue text leaks. Map which of your services rely on E2EE as a security or compliance control, and document the risk that a scanning obligation would introduce. As a UK-based leader, watch for parallel pressure under the Online Safety Act — the policy logic travels.

Surveillance & Privacy

Age Verification Is a Precursor to Attribution of Speech

The essay argues that mandatory age checks are not really about age at all — they are the first deployment of an infrastructure that binds every utterance online to a verified human identity, dismantling anonymity as a side effect rather than a goal.

The author's thesis is uncomfortable precisely because it is plausible: once a platform must verify that you are old enough, it must collect a durable, identity-linked token to prove it. That token does not stay in the age-gate. The same plumbing that confirms you are over eighteen can later confirm exactly who posted what, and the political appetite to make that link explicit follows the infrastructure, not the other way around. Age verification normalises the idea that speech online should be attributable by default.

This reframes a debate that is usually fought on child-safety grounds into one about the architecture of public discourse. Anonymity and pseudonymity are not loopholes; they are load-bearing for whistleblowers, abuse survivors, dissidents, and ordinary people who simply do not want every comment cross-referenced with their legal name. The piece is a useful corrective to the assumption that an age check is a narrow, contained control — technically and politically, it rarely stays that way.

Data Protection

The KIDS Act Would Require Age Checks to Get Online

The EFF warns that a new US bill would push age verification down to the device and app-store layer, turning a requirement to prove your age into a requirement to hand identity data to far more of the stack than any single website ever touched.

The KIDS Act is the latest in a wave of age-assurance legislation, but the EFF's objection is structural rather than partisan. By locating verification at the operating-system or app-store boundary, the bill would make age — and therefore identity — a precondition for access to broad swathes of the internet, not just adult-content sites. That centralises an enormous amount of sensitive personal data in a handful of gatekeepers and creates a single, attractive target whose compromise would expose verified identity data at population scale.

For a security leader, this is the clearest worked example of the previous story's thesis. Every age-verification mandate is also a data-collection mandate, and the systems built to satisfy it become high-value breach targets governed by whatever retention and access controls the cheapest compliant vendor can manage. The pattern is now transatlantic: US bills, the UK Online Safety Act, and EU proposals are converging on the same architecture, which means the breach surface is converging too.

GLM 5.2 Beats Claude in Our Cyber Benchmarks

Semgrep reports that Zhipu's GLM 5.2 outperformed Claude on its in-house cybersecurity evaluations, a result it presents with deliberate caution about what a single vendor's benchmark suite can and cannot tell you.

The headline is provocative, but Semgrep's own framing is the interesting part: a benchmark win on their cyber tasks is a claim about a specific distribution of problems, not a verdict on which model is “better” at security work in general. Open-weight models from Chinese labs have closed much of the gap with frontier US models on coding and reasoning, and security tooling is now a live battleground. For anyone who has standardised on Claude for code review or vulnerability triage, the result is a prompt to test rather than to switch.

The strategic read matters more than the scoreboard. If open-weight models are genuinely competitive on security tasks, the calculus around data residency, cost, and self-hosting changes — you can run capable models inside your own boundary instead of sending findings to an API. But provenance and supply-chain trust cut the other way: a model's weights are themselves an artefact you are choosing to trust. Treat the benchmark as a reason to run your own evaluation on your own representative tasks before drawing any conclusion.

What to do

Build a small, private evaluation set from your own real security tasks — triage tickets, code-review diffs, log analysis — and run Claude against GLM 5.2 and one other open-weight model on it. Decide on your own evidence, not a vendor benchmark. If you consider self-hosting an open-weight model, treat weight provenance and the update channel as supply-chain risks worth a formal review.

Research

Knowledge Distillation of Black-Box Large Language Models

A 2024 paper resurfaces on the front page, laying out how a smaller model can be trained to imitate a closed, API-only model using nothing but its outputs — a technique with sharp implications for both capability transfer and intellectual-property risk.

The work formalises what many practitioners do informally: query a strong, proprietary model at scale, collect its responses, and use them to fine-tune a cheaper open model that approximates its behaviour. The paper's contribution is making the distillation pipeline rigorous — selecting prompts, filtering noisy outputs, and aligning the student model — rather than treating it as ad-hoc data scraping. The result is a reproducible method for extracting much of a black-box model's practical value without access to its weights.

For a security leader the framing is dual-use. Distillation is how a competitor or adversary can clone the behaviour of a model you pay a premium for, which makes API outputs themselves a form of leakable intellectual property worth thinking about in terms of rate limits, terms of service, and abuse detection. It is also why “just use the open model” is rarely the whole story: a distilled model inherits not only its teacher's strengths but, potentially, its biases and blind spots, with none of the original provider's guardrails.

AI in Practice

I Used Claude Code to Get a Second Opinion on My MRI

A developer walks through feeding their own MRI data into Claude Code with Opus, using it not as a diagnosis but as a structured research assistant to understand a radiology report and ask their doctor sharper questions.

The piece is a calm, concrete account of an increasingly common pattern: using a coding agent as a general reasoning tool over personal data. The author is careful about the boundary — Claude Code is used to parse terminology, cross-reference the radiologist's findings, and prepare questions, not to replace a clinician. What makes it interesting beyond the medical angle is the workflow: the same agent loop you use for code becomes a way to interrogate any dense, jargon-heavy document you actually care about getting right.

For someone building AI-assisted workflows, the takeaway is the maturity of the tooling rather than the novelty of the use case. Claude Code's file handling and iterative loop are general enough that “analyse this corpus and help me reason about it” works whether the corpus is a repository or a stack of scans. The flip side is governance: the moment you put personal or regulated data into an agent, you inherit questions about where that data goes, what is retained, and whether the convenience is worth the exposure.

What to do

If your teams are already using Claude Code this way, get ahead of it: publish clear guidance on what categories of data may and may not be pasted into agents, and confirm your retention and logging assumptions for the deployment you use. The use case is legitimate and valuable — the risk is staff improvising data handling because no policy exists yet.

AI Tooling Risk
Directly applies to you

Codex Still Has No Way to Exclude Sensitive Files

A long-open GitHub issue documents that OpenAI's Codex agent still lacks a reliable mechanism to keep secrets, credentials, and other sensitive files out of its context — a gap that turns ordinary repository hygiene into a real exposure.

The complaint is mundane and exactly why it matters: developers want a dependable way to tell a coding agent “never read these paths,” and the issue argues Codex does not provide one that can be trusted. Without an enforced exclusion list, the agent can pull .env files, private keys, customer data, or internal configuration into its working context, where it may be sent to the model, surfaced in suggestions, or logged. The friction of the workaround means teams either disable the tool on sensitive repos or quietly accept the risk.

This is the unglamorous side of agentic coding that security leaders should be watching. The productivity case for these tools is real, but the data-governance defaults are immature, and “the agent read a file it should not have” is a leak path that bypasses most existing DLP controls because it happens inside a sanctioned developer tool. Compare your agents on this specific axis — enforced file exclusion — rather than on raw capability, because this is where the boring breaches will come from.

Reverse Engineering

Librepods: AirPods Liberated

An open-source project reverse-engineers Apple's AirPods protocol to expose battery levels, ear-detection, and noise-control features on non-Apple platforms, handing users control over hardware Apple intends to keep locked to its own ecosystem.

Librepods is the kind of community work that quietly expands what people can do with hardware they already own. By decoding the proprietary Bluetooth protocol AirPods use, it brings features long restricted to iOS and macOS — adaptive noise control, in-ear detection, battery reporting — to Android and Linux. It is interoperability in the classic sense: making a device serve its owner rather than only its manufacturer's platform strategy.

There is a security and governance subtext worth noting. Reverse-engineering a closed protocol surfaces exactly how a device authenticates and what it reports, which is useful both for users seeking control and for anyone assessing what a pair of earbuds actually transmits. Projects like this also live in legal grey areas under anti-circumvention rules, and their durability depends on a manufacturer's tolerance — a reminder that “you own the hardware” is, increasingly, a contested claim.

Systemic Risk

AI Boom Risks Global Financial Crash, Warn Central Bankers

Central bankers are sounding a familiar alarm: concentration of market value and capital expenditure in a handful of AI firms has reached a level where a correction could ripple well beyond the technology sector into the broader financial system.

The warning is less about whether AI is useful and more about how its valuation has become load-bearing for entire indices. When a small number of companies account for an outsized share of market capitalisation and of the capital expenditure flowing into data centres and chips, a disappointment in returns does not stay contained. Central bankers are flagging the structural fragility: the same enthusiasm that funds rapid build-out also concentrates the downside, and the financing chains linking chipmakers, cloud providers, and model labs are increasingly circular.

For a security and risk leader this is a useful reminder that “AI risk” is not only adversarial or technical — it is also macroeconomic, and it touches business continuity. If your organisation's strategy assumes ever-cheaper, ever-more-available AI capacity, a market correction that forces consolidation or price rises is a scenario worth having in your planning. The bankers are not predicting a crash; they are pointing out that the system has little slack if one comes.

AI & Integrity

Professor Denounces Mass AI Fraud on an Exam at Brown

A Brown University professor publicly describes discovering widespread AI-assisted cheating on an exam, framing it not as a few bad actors but as a systemic failure of how assessment, trust, and incentives now interact in an age of capable models.

The account is striking for its scale and for the professor's refusal to treat it as an individual disciplinary matter. When a large fraction of a cohort can plausibly outsource an exam to a model, the problem is no longer cheating in the old sense — it is that the assessment instrument has stopped measuring what it claims to. The piece sits in a growing genre of educators confronting the fact that the academic-integrity model assumed a friction that no longer exists.

The lesson generalises well beyond universities. Any organisation that relies on a test, certification, or written submission as a proxy for competence is now in the same position: the proxy is cheap to fake, and the honest are penalised relative to those who quietly use the tools. For security leaders this echoes in hiring, in compliance attestations, and in any control that depends on a human having genuinely done the work — verification has to move from trusting the artefact to observing the process.
