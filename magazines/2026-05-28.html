Insider Threat
Directly applies to you

Google employee charged with $1M Polymarket insider trading bet on search term

Federal prosecutors say a Google engineer placed a six-figure Polymarket position after seeing an internal search trend spike inside the company before the wider market caught on — the first criminal insider-trading case built around a prediction-market wager.

The complaint reads like a textbook insider abuse case retrofitted for the prediction-market era. The employee allegedly had access to search-term dashboards that surface real-time query volumes well before any external signal — exactly the kind of high-trust telemetry that data engineers, ML researchers and infrastructure SREs touch every day at any large platform. What is novel is the exit route: instead of trading equities, the bet sat on a public crypto-rails prediction market where six-figure positions on niche outcomes leave a very visible footprint on-chain. Prosecutors followed the wallet, not the wire.

For security leaders the read-across is uncomfortable. Most insider-threat programmes are still tuned for exfiltration of customer data or source code. They are not tuned for staff trading on internal signals such as search trends, ad spend, customer health scores or churn dashboards. None of those datasets are classified as crown jewels but every one of them is materially predictive of something tradeable. Expect this to be the first of several cases as DOJ leans on on-chain forensics — and expect insurers and audit committees to start asking pointed questions about who sees pre-public business telemetry.

What to do

Audit which internal dashboards expose pre-public signals (search trends, ad load, churn, support volume) and apply the same access controls you give to financials. Update your acceptable-use policy and code-of-conduct to explicitly cover prediction markets, not just equities, and require recertification for high-signal data access at least quarterly.

Directly applies to you

What Apple and Google are doing to push notifications

An independent researcher pulls apart the new push-notification telemetry quietly shipped in iOS and Android over the past two releases, showing how delivery receipts and decryption metadata now feed back to platform servers in ways the documentation never quite admits.

The piece is the most detailed teardown yet of what Apple Push Notification service and Firebase Cloud Messaging actually capture once a payload is on-device. Where the public API surface stops at "delivered" and "opened", the reverse-engineered traffic shows much richer signals — device wake state, the encrypted payload size class, and a per-app entropy fingerprint that survives across reinstalls. Crucially, the researcher demonstrates that these signals are available to the platform even when end-to-end encrypted messaging apps believe they are leaking nothing.

This matters far beyond the privacy-Twitter discourse. UK and EU regulators have been circling push-notification metadata for two years, ever since Senator Wyden's 2023 letter forced Apple and Google to disclose that governments routinely subpoena it. If the techniques described here are correct, the metadata footprint is materially larger than what either platform has acknowledged. Expect the ICO and the Irish DPC to ask follow-up questions — and expect any organisation relying on push for second-factor delivery or DLP alerts to revisit whether the channel is as opaque as they assumed.

What to do

Re-read your DPIA wherever push notifications carry anything sensitive (MFA codes, healthcare alerts, support escalations). Where possible move to in-app polling for the actual secret and use push only as a wakeup. Add platform push providers to your sub-processor register if they are not already there, and flag for your DPO.

Content Provenance
Directly applies to you

YouTube to automatically label AI-generated videos

Google's video platform will start applying machine-derived "AI-generated or significantly altered" labels to uploads at ingest, using C2PA content credentials where present and a fallback classifier where they are not — a quiet shift from creator-declared to platform-detected provenance.

The announcement leans on C2PA, the open content-credentials standard that Adobe, Microsoft, the BBC and now Anthropic all back. What is new is that YouTube will no longer wait for the uploader to self-declare; the platform will infer provenance from the file itself and label accordingly. For honest creators that is fine. For everyone else it is the first large-scale production deployment of provenance-by-default, and the failure modes are interesting — false-positive labelling of legitimate footage processed through AI noise reduction, and false-negative laundering of synthetic content through a cheap re-encode.

For security and trust teams this is the start, not the end, of the provenance rollout. Expect identical mechanics to land in LinkedIn, X and Meta within the year, then in enterprise content systems. The under-discussed corollary is that any internal video you produce — town halls, training, customer references — will now carry a provenance trail that downstream platforms can read and act on. If your brand cares about its outputs being trusted, you need to start signing them now rather than retrofitting later.

What to do

Pilot C2PA signing on at least one internal content pipeline this quarter — a corporate communications video or a customer case-study reel is a good target. Get your marketing and comms team familiar with content credentials before the platforms start surfacing them in public. Where you use AI tools in legitimate production, document it so the resulting label is not a surprise.

Claude Code as a Daily Driver: Claude.md, Skills, Subagents, Plugins, and MCPs

A practitioner's field guide to running Claude Code as a primary development environment — covering the CLAUDE.md memory pattern, the skill plugin model, subagent orchestration, and the MCP server ecosystem that now connects most enterprise tools.

The post is the closest thing yet to a coherent map of the Claude Code workflow as it actually exists in late May. The author works through CLAUDE.md as a shared context file checked into the repo, the skill-and-plugin layering that lets a team standardise on internal commands, the subagent pattern for keeping the main agent's context window clean during heavy work, and the practical realities of MCP servers — which work well, which need rate-limit care, and where Chrome MCP still beats DOM-blind workflows. It is opinionated in a useful way: the author tells you what to skip.

For anyone running an AI-assisted shop the article is worth reading line by line, because it is the first widely-shared write-up that treats Claude Code as the actual surface rather than as a curiosity. The CLAUDE.md pattern in particular has implications for source control hygiene — the file is a context-sensitive instruction set, and once you have one you have an internal AI policy artefact that needs the same review discipline as your CI config. The same goes for skills: a skill bundle is a piece of automation that can take destructive actions on behalf of any team member that triggers it.

What to do

If you do not yet have a CLAUDE.md in your main work repo, add one this week and put it under the same review as any other config file. Inventory which skills your team has installed across personal and shared marketplaces and decide which need governance. For the Cowork workflows you are already running, this is the closest external blueprint to compare notes against.

Security Research
Directly applies to you

Multi-Agent LLM System for Automated Vulnerability Discovery and Reproduction

An arXiv preprint reports a multi-agent pipeline that takes a CVE description, plans an exploit path, writes the reproducer and validates it end-to-end on a containerised target — closing what used to be a multi-day human loop into a sub-hour automated one.

The architecture is the interesting bit. Rather than one model trying to do everything, the authors split planning, code-writing, environment-setup and validation across cooperating agents, each with narrow tool access. The reported result is a meaningful step-change in reproducibility on the SecBench-style evaluation: cases that previously required a skilled vulnerability researcher to chase context across blog posts and pull requests now resolve from the CVE record alone. The paper acknowledges the obvious dual-use concern but does not soften it.

For defenders this is a forward indicator of the gap that is about to open. If a small lab can build a research artefact that turns CVE-to-PoC into an hourly job, well-resourced offensive teams are already doing it at scale. The downstream effect is that the practical time between disclosure and weaponised exploit collapses further; the operational window that patch teams plan around is no longer a week, it is a day. CISO programmes built on a 30-day patch SLA are quietly already in breach of their own threat model.

What to do

Pull your current patch SLA targets out of the policy document and stress-test them against a 24-hour weaponisation assumption for internet-facing assets. If you cannot patch in a day, decide explicitly what compensating control absorbs the risk in that window — virtual patching, WAF rules, exposure reduction — and document it before the auditor asks.

AI Industry
Directly applies to you

I think Anthropic and OpenAI have found product-market fit

Simon Willison's mid-year stocktake on the frontier-model business: the labs have moved past the demo phase into recurring enterprise revenue, the developer-tool layer is hardening around a small number of patterns, and the next eighteen months are an integration story rather than a model story.

Willison is one of the more careful chroniclers of this space, and the piece is worth reading because he resists both the boosterism and the doom framings. His argument is simple: both Anthropic and OpenAI now have multiple product surfaces that customers renew on without prompting, the developer ecosystem has converged on a small set of useful primitives (tools, agents, MCP, structured outputs), and the centre of gravity for the next year is integration depth rather than benchmark wars. The implication is that the buyer's decision is now less about which model is two points smarter and more about which one fits the workflow you actually run.

The interesting subtext for a CISO is governance. Once a model lands in the workflow of a thousand staff, the procurement question — "is this vendor approved?" — is already lost. The real question is what guardrails, audit trails, DLP integration and data-residency commitments the chosen vendor offers, and whether you can demonstrate to a regulator that you know what is happening. Willison's observation that the integration layer is hardening is good news here; it means the controls you build this year do not have to be thrown away next year.

What to do

If your AI usage policy is still a one-pager saying "approved models only", upgrade it to name the controls: audit logging, DLP, data-residency, sub-processor scope, and how an employee can request an exception. Anthropic and OpenAI both publish enterprise control matrices — read both, pick the one whose claims map to your existing GRC stack, and document the gap.

Platform Outage
Directly applies to you

GitHub: Incident with Pull Requests, Issues, Git Operations and API Requests

GitHub spent several hours degraded on Wednesday across the four surfaces most engineering teams depend on, with PR creation, issue updates, git push and API traffic all affected — the second multi-surface incident on the platform in as many months.

The status-page write-up is sparse, as these things always are during the live window, but the pattern is becoming familiar: a control-plane component failed, then the dependent surfaces (PRs, Issues, Actions queues, the API) cascaded. For most teams the day was a write-up of "we couldn't merge anything between 14:00 and 18:00". For a smaller number of teams that depend on GitHub Actions for production deploys, image builds or scheduled compliance jobs, the impact bled into runtime systems and not just developer productivity.

Two takeaways for security and platform leaders. First, GitHub is unambiguously now a tier-one production dependency for nearly every modern software organisation — the question is no longer whether to treat it that way in your DR plan, only how much redundancy you can afford. Second, several teams reported that their internal change-management process actually held up well, because they had a documented "if GitHub is down, here's what we can and cannot ship" runbook. If you do not, write one. The cost of writing it is small; the cost of improvising it during the next outage is not.

What to do

Add a GitHub-outage scenario to your next tabletop. Confirm what your team is allowed to do (deploy from a local clone? bypass PR review with explicit sign-off?) and what they are not. If GitHub Actions is in your prod path, document the manual fallback. Brief your auditors that this is now part of your BCP, because next year's SOC 2 will ask.

Resilient Comms

I'm Getting into Mesh Networks (Meshtastic, MeshCore, and Reticulum)

A clear-eyed introduction to the three open mesh-networking stacks that have matured into something more than hobbyist gear — covering the LoRa-based Meshtastic ecosystem, the newer MeshCore project, and the cryptographically-strong Reticulum network protocol.

The author walks through what each stack is actually good at, which is more useful than the usual evangelism. Meshtastic is the easy on-ramp with the largest community but the weakest privacy model. MeshCore is a more recent, more carefully designed alternative with better routing behaviour at scale. Reticulum is a different beast altogether — a network protocol that runs over LoRa, packet radio, TCP and serial links, with strong end-to-end encryption baked in and no central infrastructure required. The piece does a good job of explaining when each is the right answer.

This is one of those topics that sits adjacent to security without obviously being a security topic. The reason it matters is resilience: every enterprise comms plan now assumes the open internet is available, and the recent run of cable cuts, ISP outages and platform incidents is making that assumption look optimistic. A pocketable mesh radio is not a replacement for corporate IT, but it is a credible last-resort channel for a small leadership team and a useful piece of personal preparedness for anyone who plans for the worst day.

AI Governance
Directly applies to you

Ripgrep's AI Policy

Andrew Gallant publishes the AI-contribution policy for ripgrep — one of the most-used pieces of infrastructure in modern developer tooling — and the document is now circulating as a reference example for what an open-source AI policy can actually look like.

The policy is short and unfussy, which is most of the point. It does not ban AI-assisted contributions outright but it does require that the human submitting the PR has read, understood and stands behind every line, that mass low-effort AI-generated submissions will be closed without ceremony, and that maintainer time is the binding constraint rather than tool ideology. Several other significant open-source projects — including Curl and a handful of language standard libraries — have converged on similar wording.

The document is worth reading for a reason that has nothing to do with ripgrep. It is a short, well-written example of a real-world AI usage policy aimed at contributors rather than employees, and the framing translates directly into enterprise contexts. If you have been struggling to write an AI usage policy that is neither toothless nor unenforceable, this is a better template than most of the consultancy decks you will be sold this year — and it has the advantage of being battle-tested against an active community.

What to do

Take the ripgrep AI policy and the Curl one, sit with your head of engineering for an hour, and produce a one-page AI contribution and usage policy for your own engineering org. Publish it where your staff actually look (the engineering handbook, not the GRC SharePoint). Revisit in six months — the ground is moving fast enough that an annual review cycle is too slow.

Industry Ethics

Tech CEOs are apparently suffering from AI psychosis

A pointed TechCrunch column on the increasingly untethered public statements coming from frontier-model executives — and the gap between what their models actually do and what the keynote stage version claims they will do by Christmas.

The piece is critical without being cynical. The author lines up a year of public claims from various AI-lab executives, contrasts them with the lab's own technical reports and red-team disclosures, and asks the obvious question: who, exactly, do you serve when the public narrative outruns the documented capability by twelve to eighteen months? The answer, predictably, is the share price and the talent funnel — but the column is honest that this pattern has been around since the dot-com era and is not unique to AI.

For anyone whose job involves separating the demo from the deployment, the reminder is useful. The frontier labs continue to do genuinely impressive work and also continue to oversell. The CISO's job is to read the system cards, the model cards, the responsible-deployment write-ups and the SOC 2 reports — and to discount the keynote by a healthy margin. The vendors that are easiest to work with at procurement time are the ones whose public claims and private documents converge; the ones whose claims diverge are a tell.
