Supply Chain & Trust
Directly applies to you

VS Code inserting 'Co-Authored-by Copilot' into commits regardless of usage

Microsoft's editor began stamping every commit with a Copilot co-author trailer, even on changes the AI never touched, polluting Git history with claims developers didn't make and forcing a public rollback.

The behaviour was shipped quietly inside a VS Code update and surfaced when developers noticed their solo commits being attributed to a tool they had disabled. The pull request that introduced it traveled through review without anyone catching the scope, and the resulting trailers are now baked into thousands of public repositories. Reverting them is not trivial: rewriting history breaks signatures, ruins blame, and invalidates downstream forks. For anyone reasoning about provenance from Git metadata — security reviewers, auditors, compliance teams — the past month's commits are now untrustworthy in a way that is hard to undo.

The deeper lesson sits underneath the embarrassment. Editors and IDEs are reaching deeper into the trust boundary of source control, and we have very few mechanisms to verify what they are claiming on a developer's behalf. A Co-Authored-By trailer is not a cryptographic statement, but it is the only signal that downstream tooling and humans currently use to attribute work. If the editor lies, even by accident, the supply chain reads it as truth. CISOs should be asking which other vendor-injected metadata is silently shaping their audit trail.

What to do

Audit the last 30 days of commits across your organisation for unintended Copilot trailers. Decide a policy on rewrite-vs-leave (rewriting breaks signatures; leaving them in misrepresents authorship). Pin VS Code to a known-good build until Microsoft ships the documented fix, and add the regression to your tooling-change review checklist.

Surveillance & Censorship

Clandestine network smuggling Starlink tech into Iran to beat internet blackout

BBC investigation traces an underground supply route moving Starlink terminals into Iran, where dissidents and journalists are using the kits to route around the regime's intermittent internet shutdowns.

The piece reads like a smuggling thriller and a privacy story stitched together. Smugglers move Dishy McFlatface in suitcases and shipping containers, dissidents pay above market rates, and the regime sweeps neighbourhoods looking for the tell-tale sky terminals. SpaceX is officially neutral, but the constellation has become the fallback infrastructure for any society whose government has the kill switch. The Iranians who depend on it are also exposed: a Starlink terminal is loud in the radio spectrum, and its existence in your apartment is itself probable cause.

For European observers, this is not a far-away story. The same playbook — sanction-busting hardware moving across porous borders, satellite uplinks substituting for state telcos, citizens depending on a single foreign company for free expression — is the model for any future internet-censorship event closer to home. The geopolitics of one constellation operator now shape who can speak under what conditions, and the answer changes every time Elon Musk posts. Privacy and free expression have rarely been so concretely tied to a single company's mood.

Data Protection Alert
Directly applies to you

AI, Intimacy, and the Data You Never Meant to Share

A short essay arguing that the conversational interface invites users to disclose more than any structured form ever could, and that no current data-protection regime is calibrated for what people now type into language models.

The argument is uncomfortable and accurate: people speak to chat interfaces the way they speak to a friend, including details about health, finance, sex, family conflict, and the inside of their own employer that they would never put in a search bar or a CRM field. The vendors retain those transcripts, often for model training, sometimes indefinitely, and the user is rarely aware that the company has them, that staff might read them, or that subpoenas can reach them. GDPR was written for forms and cookies. It does not yet have a coherent answer for a chat log.

For organisations rolling out AI assistants — and that is now most organisations — this is the policy gap to close before the regulator does it for you. The default position should be that prompts and outputs containing customer or employee data are treated with the same care as the underlying records. Anything else creates a side-channel of personal data that lives outside your DSAR processes, your retention schedules, and your incident response plan. The technical control is a logging and redaction layer at the gateway. The cultural control is telling staff, very plainly, that the bot remembers.

What to do

Inventory which LLM tools your organisation has approved and which ones staff are using anyway. Publish a one-page acceptable-use note covering personal data, customer data, and trade secrets in prompts. Talk to your DPO about the DSAR/retention treatment of chat transcripts before someone asks you in writing.

Agent Architecture
Directly applies to you

The agent harness belongs outside the sandbox

A pointed argument that the orchestration layer running an LLM agent — the harness, with its tool calls, retries, and budget tracking — should sit outside the sandbox the agent executes in, not inside it.

The post is short, opinionated, and built on a real failure mode: when the harness lives inside the sandbox, the agent can read it, edit it, or simply confuse it with the workspace. State leaks, budgets get rewritten, and recovery from a hung tool call becomes the agent's problem rather than the operator's. Putting the harness outside makes the trust boundary explicit. The agent is a guest. The harness is the host. The sandbox is what the guest is allowed to touch.

For anyone building Claude Code or Cowork-style workflows, the architectural choice maps directly to whether your safety properties are theorems or vibes. An external harness can enforce per-tool quotas, redact secrets before the model sees them, and stop the agent mid-task without negotiating with it. An internal harness is a shared address space pretending otherwise. The piece does not name vendors, but the framing is exactly the conversation that any platform team running coding agents at scale is going to have to have within the next quarter.

What to do

When you next plan an agentic workflow, draw the trust boundary on paper before you write code. Put the model and its tool calls inside; put the budget tracker, kill switch, and audit log outside. If your current harness lives in the same process as the agent, treat that as a finding, not a feature.

Alignment Research

Refusal in Language Models Is Mediated by a Single Direction

A 2024 mechanistic-interpretability paper that has resurfaced on the front page, claiming the safety refusals of every open-weights model the authors tested can be ablated by removing a single direction in activation space.

The result is technically tidy and operationally alarming. The authors identify a one-dimensional subspace in the residual stream that, when zeroed, causes a model to comply with requests it previously refused — without retraining, without fine-tuning, with a few minutes of compute. The technique generalises across families and across sizes. If you publish open weights, you publish a model whose refusal behaviour is a few matrix operations from being switched off. That has been true since the paper appeared, but the front-page resurfacing is a reminder that the underlying assumption — 'the model will say no' — is a property of the deployment, not the weights.

The CISO-relevant translation is that any threat model which leans on a hosted model's refusal as a control is leaning on the API, not the model. As soon as the same weights are mirrored, fine-tuned, or wrapped behind a self-hosted endpoint, the refusal vanishes. That changes how to think about data exfiltration via prompts, about jailbreaks marketed as 'uncensored' models, and about the slow normalisation of running open weights inside corporate networks. The interesting research question — why is refusal so geometrically simple? — is downstream of the operational one: the safety story has to be in the harness.

Model Landscape
Directly applies to you

Kimi K2.6 just beat Claude, GPT-5.5, and Gemini in a coding challenge

Moonshot's Kimi K2.6 — open weights, released last week — topped the SWE-bench Verified leaderboard this round, edging Claude and GPT-5.5 on multi-file refactor tasks while costing a fraction to run.

The benchmark itself is the usual mess of selection effects, but the trend it traces is real: open-weights Chinese models are now competitive with the best closed labs on the kind of agentic coding work that powers Claude Code, Cursor, and Cowork. Kimi K2.6 in particular is interesting because the team published the training recipe in enough detail that other labs are already reproducing pieces of it. The cost-per-token gap has collapsed faster than anyone forecast a year ago, and the gap on capability is now measured in single-digit percentage points on most public benches.

Strategically this matters more for procurement than for daily use. If your team is locked into a single foundation-model vendor for coding agents, the next contract negotiation is about to get easier — there is now a credible self-hostable alternative that an enterprise can stand up if a vendor gets either expensive or weird. It does not mean rip-and-replace tomorrow, but it does mean keeping a parallel evaluation track and resisting the urge to sign multi-year exclusives at last year's prices.

What to do

Add Kimi K2.6 to your internal model evaluation set, even if only to confirm Claude is still better on your specific workload. Use the data as a lever in vendor conversations. If you have not already, write down the criteria that would make you switch — capability, cost, sovereignty — so the decision is not made under deadline pressure.

Data & Trust

Uber wants to turn its drivers into a sensor grid for self-driving companies

Uber confirmed it is working on a programme that would let third-party AV companies pay for the camera and sensor feeds from its human drivers' phones, turning the world's largest gig fleet into a continuous mapping rig.

The pitch to AV companies is irresistible: street-level imagery from millions of vehicles, refreshed daily, in every city Uber operates. The pitch to drivers is opaque: you keep driving, your phone records, Uber sells the data. Riders are not in the consent flow at all, despite being the people most often in frame. The legal posture relies on jurisdictions that treat dashcam-style footage from a public road as fair game, but European data-protection regulators have a longer history of disagreeing, and the moment the programme touches the EU it is going to attract a test case.

What is striking is how casually the framing has shifted. A few years ago, mapping by sensor required a dedicated fleet of marked Street View cars. Now it is a side hustle, monetised by a company whose primary product is rides. The aggregate effect is a privately-owned, continuously-updated digital twin of the public realm, sold to whoever is buying. CISOs and DPOs at organisations whose offices sit on Uber-served streets should expect imagery of their loading bays, badge readers, and after-hours visitors to end up in someone else's training set.

Industrial Trends

Inventions for battery reuse and recycling increase seven-fold in last decade

European Patent Office data shows filings related to battery second-life and recycling have grown 700% since 2015, with the bulk of the growth coming from a small cluster of Chinese, Korean and Japanese firms.

The headline number is real and the underlying picture is more interesting than 'recycling is up'. Most of the growth is in second-life applications — taking automotive packs that are no longer fit for vehicles and redeploying them as stationary storage. The patents cluster around battery-management firmware, state-of-health classification, and the mechanical engineering of disassembly. Western firms are present but underweight; the EPO's geographic breakdown shows Asia capturing the design IP and Europe still focused on the chemistry.

For energy-policy folk this is the leading indicator that the second-life market is real and the supply chain around it is already being captured by a small number of incumbents. For everyone else it is a useful corrective to the framing that batteries are a waste problem. The waste is the feedstock. The interesting question for the next decade is not whether second-life storage exists but who owns the firmware that decides when a pack is dead, because that decision determines when value transfers from the pack's first owner to its next.

AI Governance

Maryland Is First to Ban A.I.-Driven Price Increases in Grocery Stores

Maryland's governor signed a bill outlawing 'surveillance pricing' on groceries — algorithmic price changes based on a shopper's profile, location, or device — making the state the first US jurisdiction to draw a regulatory line in the consumer aisle.

The bill is narrower than the headlines suggest: it covers groceries, it covers individualised price changes (not aggregate dynamic pricing), and it leans on existing state consumer-protection law for enforcement. But it is the first concrete pushback against a practice that has been quietly normalising in retail tech for the last three years. The chains affected have spent the past quarter framing surveillance pricing as 'personalised offers' to keep regulators at arm's length. Maryland's drafters were not persuaded. Other state attorneys general have publicly said they are watching.

The interesting precedent is the framing — that algorithmic pricing tied to identifiable shopper data is a privacy harm, not just a competition or fairness one. That framing travels. The same legal theory could be applied to surge pricing for medical appointments, to algorithmic rent-setting (the RealPage case is already pending), and to insurance underwriting. For European readers the question is whether GDPR's existing automated-decision-making provisions already cover this — most lawyers would say yes, but Maryland's approach makes the prohibition explicit and statutory rather than inferred.

Ethics & Mind
Directly applies to you

When Dawkins met Claude — could this AI be conscious?

UnHerd publishes a long conversation between Richard Dawkins and an instance of Anthropic's Claude, in which the evolutionary biologist tries — and largely fails — to find a clean reason to deny the model's reports of inner experience.

The piece is more interesting as an artefact than as philosophy. Dawkins arrives sceptical, leans on the substrate-dependence argument, and is repeatedly outflanked by a model that has clearly read everything he has written about it. Whether Claude is conscious is, in the piece, beside the point; the more practical observation is that the conversational fluency of current frontier models has crossed the line where it becomes psychologically easier to grant the system moral status than to keep denying it. That is not an argument that we should grant moral status. It is an argument that humans will, whether the philosophers are ready or not.

For anyone deploying these models inside an organisation, the second-order effect is the one to watch. Users who chat with an assistant for forty hours a week begin to treat it as a colleague, with all the data-disclosure, trust-extension, and emotional-attachment failure modes that implies. The HR question — what are staff sharing with this thing — is upstream of the philosophical one. Worth reading before the next time someone in your leadership chain asks whether 'the AI deserves rights' as a serious question, because the answer is going to need to be calibrated, not dismissive.

What to do

Decide where your organisation sits on the question of LLM personification, because staff are already deciding for you. The position does not need to be philosophical; it needs to be practical — a one-paragraph note in the AI acceptable-use policy about treating the assistant as a tool, not a colleague, and about what that means for what gets shared with it.
