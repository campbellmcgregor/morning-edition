RedSun: System-Level Access on Windows via the April 2026 Update

Directly applies to you

A new open-source tool called RedSun exploits a flaw introduced in the April 2026 Windows update to gain SYSTEM-level user access on Windows 10, Windows 11 and Windows Server.

The tool, published on GitHub by Nightmare-Eclipse, demonstrates a privilege escalation path that gives attackers the highest level of access on affected Windows systems. This is not a theoretical proof-of-concept buried in an academic paper. The exploit code is live, public and trivially executable.

What makes this particularly dangerous is the timing. The vulnerability was introduced in Microsoft's own April 2026 cumulative update, meaning organisations that patched promptly may have inadvertently introduced the flaw. Systems running Windows 10, Windows 11 and all current Windows Server editions are affected.

For any environment running Active Directory, this is a critical-severity issue. SYSTEM-level access means full control: credential dumping, lateral movement, persistence. Red teams will be using this within days if they are not already.

What to do

Check whether your estate has applied the April 2026 cumulative update. Monitor Microsoft's advisory channels for an out-of-band patch. Brief your SOC team on the RedSun tool and ensure detection rules are updated for SYSTEM-level privilege escalation indicators. Consider temporarily delaying the April update rollout on critical servers until a fix is confirmed.

Bluesky Under DDoS Attack for Nearly a Full Day

The decentralised social network Bluesky has been battling a sustained distributed denial-of-service attack lasting close to 24 hours, causing intermittent outages for its growing user base.

The Verge reports that Bluesky's infrastructure has been under sustained volumetric attack since early on the 16th. While the platform has remained partially accessible, performance has degraded significantly and some users have been unable to connect at all.

This is notable for two reasons. First, Bluesky has been growing rapidly as a Twitter/X alternative, which makes it an increasingly attractive target. Second, the AT Protocol's federated architecture was supposed to offer resilience against exactly this kind of centralised failure. The fact that the attack has been this effective suggests the protocol's practical resilience may not yet match its theoretical design.

For security leaders, this is a useful case study in how decentralised platforms handle availability attacks. If your organisation uses Bluesky for communications or brand presence, have a fallback plan. More broadly, any platform migration strategy should account for the target platform's DDoS resilience track record.

Cybersecurity / Analysis

AI Cybersecurity Is Not Proof of Work

Antirez, the creator of Redis, argues that AI-generated security measures create a false sense of safety because they lack the deep understanding that comes from doing the work yourself.

This essay cuts at something uncomfortable for anyone relying on AI tools for security work. Antirez draws a parallel to proof-of-work in cryptocurrency: the value of security measures comes partly from the effort invested in understanding the threat model, not just from the output itself.

The argument is that when AI generates security configurations, vulnerability assessments or incident response playbooks, the human operator may not understand why specific choices were made. This creates brittleness. When something unexpected happens, you cannot adapt a plan you do not understand.

This does not mean AI has no place in cybersecurity. But it suggests that the right model is AI-assisted rather than AI-delegated. The security professional needs to remain the one who understands the "why" behind every control, even if AI accelerates the "how."

For organisations evaluating AI deployment strategies, this represents the ongoing democratisation of capable models. The gap between proprietary and open-source is narrowing in specific domains, particularly code generation. This has implications for data sovereignty, cost management and vendor lock-in decisions.

US Bill Mandates On-Device Age Verification

A new US bill would require on-device age verification, raising concerns about surveillance infrastructure being built into consumer devices under the banner of child safety.

The bill, reported by Reclaim The Net, would mandate that device manufacturers implement age verification mechanisms directly in hardware or operating systems. The stated goal is protecting minors from harmful content. The practical effect would be a government-mandated identity verification layer on every device sold in the US.

The privacy implications are significant regardless of intent. On-device age verification requires either biometric scanning, government ID integration or behavioural analysis. Each approach creates data that could be repurposed for broader surveillance. Once the infrastructure exists, the scope of what it verifies tends to expand.

For UK and EU organisations, this matters because US regulatory precedent often influences transatlantic policy. The UK's Online Safety Act already includes age verification requirements. If the US mandates on-device implementation, hardware manufacturers may roll it out globally rather than maintaining region-specific firmware. Watch this space.
