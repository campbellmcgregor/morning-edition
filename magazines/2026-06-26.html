LastPass Notifies Users of Yet Another Data Breach

The password manager that has become shorthand for repeated compromise is once more writing to its customers — the latest entry in a years-long ledger of incidents at a company whose single job is to keep the vault sealed.

There is a grim familiarity to a LastPass disclosure now. The 2022 breach, in which attackers exfiltrated encrypted vaults along with the metadata needed to mount offline cracking campaigns, was never really closed in the public mind — it simply metastasised into a slow drip of drained crypto wallets and resigned customers. Each new notice lands against that backdrop, and the reflexive question is no longer “how bad?” but “why is this still the custodian of anyone’s secrets?” A vault provider does not get to treat trust as renewable; once you have demonstrated that the master vaults can leave the building, every subsequent incident is read as confirmation rather than anomaly.

The deeper lesson for anyone running security is about concentration risk, not LastPass specifically. A password manager is the single most attractive target in an organisation’s stack — compromise it and you inherit everything downstream. That is the trade you accept for the convenience, and it is a defensible trade only if the vendor treats breach prevention as existential. Repeat incidents are the signal that the trade has gone bad. The honest move is to assume the vault contents are recoverable by a motivated attacker and design as though they already have a copy.

What to do

If your organisation or family still runs LastPass, treat this as the prompt to migrate to a manager with a cleaner record (Bitwarden, 1Password) and, crucially, rotate the high-value credentials — banking, email, cloud admin, anything reused — rather than just moving the vault wholesale. Turn on hardware-key or passkey MFA wherever it is offered so a cracked vault entry alone does not grant access. Audit for password reuse while you are in there; that is where a leaked vault does its real damage.

The 'Papers, Please' Era of the Internet Will Decimate Your Privacy

An essay from a free-expression group argues that the global drift toward mandatory age and identity verification is quietly rebuilding the open web as a series of checkpoints — each one demanding proof of who you are before you may pass.

The framing is deliberately uncomfortable: a web where you show your papers to enter. Age-verification mandates, sold as child-protection measures, almost always require linking a real-world identity — a face scan, a government ID, a credit check — to activity that was previously pseudonymous. The piece’s sharpest point is that the harm is structural rather than incidental. Once a site is legally obliged to know your age, it must collect and retain identity data it never wanted, creating fresh honeypots and a permanent record of who read what. The privacy loss is not a side effect of enforcement; it is the enforcement.

For a UK reader this is not abstract. The Online Safety Act’s age-assurance duties are already pushing platforms toward exactly the verification architecture the essay warns about, and the EU is circling similar requirements. The uncomfortable truth for security leaders is that “verify everyone” and “minimise data” are in direct tension, and the law is currently tipping the scale toward verification. Every identity check is a breach waiting to happen, and the organisations compelled to run them rarely have the appetite to secure that data to the standard it demands.

What to do

If your organisation operates UK- or EU-facing services that may fall under age-assurance duties, get ahead of the data-minimisation question now: prefer verification providers that use tokenised or zero-knowledge attestation over those that retain ID images, and document the retention and deletion policy before regulators ask. Treat any identity-verification store as crown-jewel data with its own threat model. Personally, favour privacy-preserving age-check methods and be sparing about which services you hand a real ID to.

What Happened After 2,000 People Tried to Hack My AI Assistant

A developer opened his personal AI assistant to the public as a live red-team target, then wrote up what two thousand strangers actually tried — a rare, concrete field report on how prompt injection plays out when the attackers are real.

Most prompt-injection writing is theoretical. This is a logbook. By exposing a working assistant — one wired to tools and context — and inviting the crowd to break it, the author captured the real distribution of attacks: not clever academic jailbreaks but blunt, repeated attempts to extract the system prompt, escalate tool permissions, and trick the model into treating attacker text as trusted instructions. The value is in the specificity. You see which guardrails held, which folded under persistence, and how quickly a determined group converges on the same handful of effective techniques once one of them works.

For anyone building AI-assisted workflows, the takeaway is sobering: the model is not the security boundary. The moment an assistant can call tools, read files, or act on instructions embedded in untrusted content, every input is a potential injection vector, and “the model was told not to” is not a control. The defences that actually held were the boring architectural ones — least-privilege tool scoping, treating model output as untrusted before it triggers actions, and keeping sensitive capabilities behind explicit human confirmation rather than behind a polite instruction in the prompt.

What to do

If you are wiring Claude Code or similar agents into real workflows, threat-model the tool surface, not just the prompt: scope each tool to least privilege, never let model output auto-execute privileged actions without a confirmation step, and treat any content the agent ingests (web pages, emails, files) as hostile by default. Run your own small adversarial test against your setup before it touches anything that matters — the article is a ready-made checklist of what to throw at it.

Open Source Tooling

OpenKnowledge — An Open-Source, AI-First Alternative to Obsidian and Notion

A new project pitches itself as a knowledge base built around language models from the start rather than bolted on afterward — local-first notes that an AI can read, link, and reason over without surrendering them to someone else’s cloud.

The note-taking space is crowded, so the interesting claim here is the architecture, not the feature list. “AI-first” usually means a chat sidebar grafted onto an existing app; OpenKnowledge argues for the inverse — a store designed so retrieval, linking, and model context are native operations. For people who have spent two years duct-taping their second brain to an assistant, that is the right ambition. The open-source, self-hostable posture also answers the obvious objection to AI note tools: if a model is going to ingest your entire thinking, you would rather it happen on infrastructure you control than inside a vendor’s training-adjacent pipeline.

Scepticism is warranted, as it should be for any day-one Show HN. The hard problems in this category are not the demo — they are sync reliability, conflict resolution, and not corrupting years of accumulated notes during an upgrade. Those are exactly the things that separate a weekend project from something you trust with your knowledge base, and they take time and real users to shake out. Still, the design instinct is sound, and a credible open alternative to the Obsidian/Notion duopoly that treats AI as a first-class citizen is worth watching.

An Entire Herculaneum Scroll Has Been Read for the First Time

The Vesuvius Challenge has crossed the threshold it was built for: a complete carbonised scroll, sealed shut by the eruption of AD 79, has now been read end to end without ever being physically unrolled — recovered entirely through scanning and machine learning.

This is what applied machine learning looks like when it is pointed at something that matters. The Herculaneum scrolls were carbonised into brittle lumps by Vesuvius and are physically destroyed by any attempt to open them. The breakthrough stack — high-resolution CT scanning to capture the rolled layers, segmentation to virtually unwrap them, and trained models to detect the near-invisible traces of ink on carbon — has now matured from reading isolated words to recovering an entire text. It is a reminder that the most consequential AI work is often the least flashy: no chatbot, just patient pattern recognition restoring a library humanity assumed was lost.

The broader significance is the model of the thing. A crowdsourced prize, open data, and incremental published progress turned an intractable problem into a solved one in a handful of years. That is a template worth noting for any leader thinking about how hard problems actually get cracked — not by a single heroic system but by a well-scoped challenge, shared corpora, and many independent attempts compounding on one another. The result is the first new classical text in generations, and the method may be more reusable than the artefact.

AI Economics

The Unbearable Cheapness of Open-Weight Models

An essay argues that the collapsing cost of running capable open-weight models is reshaping the AI market faster than the frontier-lab narrative admits — and that “good enough, nearly free, and yours to run” is a more disruptive proposition than another benchmark record.

The thesis is that the centre of gravity is shifting from raw capability to economics. Open-weight models have closed enough of the gap on everyday tasks that, for a large slice of real workloads, the relevant question is no longer “which model is smartest?” but “why am I paying per token for something I can run myself for the price of electricity?” Once a model is cheap enough to run locally or on commodity hardware, the unit economics of API-metered AI start to look fragile for any use case that does not genuinely need the frontier.

For a security leader, the cheapness cuts both ways. The upside is real: sensitive data can stay inside your perimeter, with no third-party API ingesting it, and cost stops being a reason to avoid automation. The downside is governance — when anyone can spin up a capable model on a laptop, shadow AI proliferates beyond the reach of procurement and DLP, and the attacker’s cost to generate phishing, malware variants, and synthetic content drops to near zero too. The economics that empower your engineers empower everyone else as well.

IP & Provenance

'You Didn't Vibe-Code Your Data Room — You Took It From Papermark'

The founder of open-source document-sharing tool Papermark publicly accuses a startup of passing off his project’s code as its own AI-generated build — a small, sharp dispute that lands squarely on the question of provenance in the vibe-coding era.

The accusation is specific: a company allegedly took Papermark’s open-source codebase, restyled it, and presented the result as something they had “vibe-coded” from scratch with AI. Whatever the full facts, the episode crystallises a tension that is only going to intensify. Open-source licences grant broad rights but almost always carry attribution and licensing obligations, and a culture that celebrates generating software in an afternoon has a convenient blind spot about where the underlying code actually came from. When the provenance trail is “the model wrote it,” the temptation to launder lifted code through that story is obvious.

For anyone shipping software, this is a governance flag, not just startup drama. As AI-assisted development becomes the norm, code provenance and licence compliance get harder to track precisely when they matter more — generated output can echo training data, and contractors or tools may import open-source code without honouring its terms. The reputational and legal exposure of getting this wrong is real. The defensible posture is to treat provenance as something you can evidence, not assume, regardless of how the code was produced.

Semiconductors

IBM Debuts Sub-1-Nanometer Chip Technology

IBM says it has demonstrated the first chip technology breaking the sub-one-nanometre barrier — a research milestone that points to where compute density goes after the current node names stop describing anything physical at all.

The “nanometre” in modern process nodes has long been a marketing label rather than a measured dimension, so treat the headline number with the usual caution. What matters is the underlying claim: a credible path to packing transistors more densely once today’s leading nodes run out of room. IBM’s research arm has a habit of demonstrating the device physics years before it reaches a fab line, and that is the right frame here — this is a signal about the trajectory of compute, not a product you will buy soon.

The strategic reading for a technology leader is about the curve, not the press release. If transistor scaling has more headroom than the “Moore’s Law is dead” chorus suggests, the economics of AI and high-density compute keep improving, which compounds everything in this edition about cheap models and abundant capability. The counterweight is supply concentration: leading-edge fabrication remains in very few hands and geographies, and a research breakthrough does nothing to change the geopolitical fragility of who can actually manufacture at the frontier.

AI Governance

Political Bias in AI: Where the Models Actually Stand

A new comparison sets out to measure the political lean of the major language models side by side — an attempt to put numbers on a debate that usually runs on anecdote, screenshots, and partisan grievance.

The instinct to quantify model bias is healthy; the methodology is where these efforts live or die. Political orientation is slippery to measure — the questions you choose, the framing, and the scoring rubric can manufacture whatever lean you set out to find, and a single benchmark can be gamed or misread. Read this kind of study for its method first: how the prompts were constructed, whether the axis is defensible, and whether the differences between models are large enough to mean anything. The headline ranking is the least interesting part.

For a leader deploying these systems, the practical point stands regardless of where any given model lands: every model carries the imprint of its training data and alignment choices, and that imprint shows up in summarisation, drafting, and judgement calls, not just in answers to overtly political questions. If you are using AI to triage, draft policy, or summarise contested material, that subtle skew is an operational risk to be managed — through human review of consequential output and awareness of where the tool’s defaults sit — not a culture-war talking point to be won or dismissed.

In Memoriam

Om Malik Has Died

Om Malik — technology journalist, founder of GigaOm, and one of the steadier, more humane voices to come out of the blogging era — has died at 60, drawing tributes from across a tech press he helped shape.

Malik belonged to a generation of writers who treated technology journalism as a craft of independent judgement rather than access. GigaOm, the publication he founded, was an early proof that a blog could become serious, sustainable trade press, and his own writing — particularly after a health scare reordered his priorities — turned increasingly toward what technology was doing to people, not just to markets. He wrote about attention, craft, and slowness with a sincerity that was unfashionable in an industry addicted to the next launch.

His death is a marker of how much the media that covers this industry has changed. The independent, writer-led tech blog he helped legitimise has been largely supplanted by platform feeds, newsletters, and algorithmic distribution — the very dynamics this edition keeps circling. For readers who came up on his work, the loss is also a prompt: the humane, sceptical, unhurried voice he modelled is exactly what the current moment, awash in cheap content and automated everything, is most short of. Worth reading him this morning.
