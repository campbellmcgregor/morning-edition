Breach Disclosure
Directly applies to you

1,000 Data Breaches Later, the Disclosure Lag Is Worse

Troy Hunt reviews a decade of Have I Been Pwned ingest and shows the gap between when a breach happens and when the public hears about it is widening, not closing.

Hunt's data is the closest thing the industry has to ground truth on breach timing. His core finding is that the median lag between incident and disclosure has grown across the last three cohorts of incidents, despite regulations like GDPR and the UK Data Protection Act 2018 explicitly demanding 72-hour reporting to supervisory authorities. Companies are either failing to detect compromise, or detecting it and choosing to sit on it. Both options should worry anyone holding accountability for an incident response plan.

The piece is also a quiet indictment of how breach intelligence still depends on one person volunteering to triage stolen data. If you operate in the UK, the ICO's enforcement appetite has sharpened in 2025, and "we didn't know" is not an answer that survives a Section 165 notice. The practical takeaway is to stop treating breach disclosure as a legal-team afterthought and wire it into the SOC runbook with named owners, decision trees and pre-drafted comms.

What to do

Pull your incident response playbook today and check three things: who decides the 72-hour clock has started, what triggers the call to the ICO, and whether your breach comms templates exist or are still TODO. If any of those is unclear, that is a Q3 board paper.

SDSU Wired Its Dorms with 1,300 AI Cameras Without Telling Students

San Diego State University installed a network of 1,300 AI-equipped cameras across student residences and only disclosed the rollout after students discovered the hardware themselves.

The technical detail that matters: these are not dumb CCTV. The vendor stack ships with behavioural analytics enabled by default, which means every student in residence is now a training subject for a commercial computer vision model. The university's public statements lean on the standard "safety and security" framing while declining to publish a DPIA-equivalent, the retention period, or the access policy for the resulting footage. Students were not asked. Parents paying tuition were not asked.

For a CISO this is the cautionary tale you forward to your physical security team before they buy the next "AI-enabled" upgrade. The reputational damage from being caught deploying surveillance kit without an impact assessment is now larger than the cost of running one. UK and EU institutions sitting under Article 35 of the UK GDPR have even less room to wing it. A DPIA is mandatory for systematic monitoring of a large area and an enforcement notice is the predictable end state of skipping it.

Algorithmic Monocultures in Hiring

A new analysis shows that when employers all license the same handful of LLM-driven screening tools, candidates who fail one are increasingly likely to fail all of them. The labour market quietly collapses into a single biased filter.

The argument is straightforward and uncomfortable. If five vendors dominate the resume-screening market and three of those vendors fine-tune from the same base models, the diversity of decisions across the whole hiring funnel is far lower than the apparent number of independent companies suggests. A candidate rejected once is functionally rejected by the whole sector. The paper backs this with empirical correlation in screening outcomes across vendors and warns that anti-discrimination law was built for a world of many independent judges, not one trained-once-deployed-everywhere model.

This is a live regulatory issue under the EU AI Act, which classifies employment-related AI as high-risk and is now in active enforcement mode. UK employers are not off the hook either: the EHRC has signalled it will treat algorithmic screening under existing Equality Act 2010 case law. If your organisation uses any AI in hiring, the legal exposure is real and the supplier's "we are compliant" line is not a defence you can rely on.

What to do

Audit your hiring stack for any AI screening step, request the vendor's bias testing results and the underlying model family, and add an Article 22-style human review path before the regulator does it for you. If you cannot name the model behind your ATS, you have an exposure.

Developer Tools

Lathe: Use LLMs to Learn a Domain, Not Skip Past It

A new open-source CLI builds tutoring sessions around an unfamiliar codebase or topic, deliberately slowing the LLM down so the user does the cognitive work instead of just accepting the answer.

Lathe takes the opposite position to the autocomplete-everything school. The tool wraps a model in a Socratic loop: the user gets questions back, not answers, until they can demonstrate understanding of the moving parts. The repo includes prompt scaffolds for ramping up on a strange codebase, internalising a new framework, and walking through a research paper. It is a small, sharp design that picks a fight with the current default of LLM-as-oracle.

There is a real developer-tooling thesis underneath it. Teams already see junior engineers who can ship code they cannot explain, and the cost shows up later in incident reviews and security regressions. Tools that force comprehension rather than replace it are a useful counter-balance, and the pattern transfers cleanly to security training, runbook authoring and onboarding. It is worth a look even if you do not adopt the tool itself.

Industry Analysis

LLMs Are Eroding My Software Engineering Career

An anonymous senior engineer publishes a long, careful essay arguing that the LLM productivity gains being claimed by management are being booked at the expense of the engineering craft that made those gains possible in the first place.

The author's central observation is that the trade is asymmetric. Management measures throughput in tickets closed and PRs merged, both of which scale beautifully with LLM assistance. What does not scale, and is degrading, is the bench of engineers capable of writing the next generation of those tools, debugging the failure mode no model has seen yet, and architecting systems that survive contact with reality. The essay is not anti-AI; it is anti-pretending-the-substitution-is-free.

Read it as a workforce planning document. The piece is the most articulate version of a complaint that is now showing up in retention data across mid-career engineers, and a leader running a security or platform organisation should take it as a signal. If your team's career ladder is implicitly "prompt the model harder", you are mortgaging the senior engineer pipeline you will need in three years to handle incidents the model cannot.

Model Release

DeepSeek V4 Pro Beats GPT-5.5 Pro on Precision

DeepSeek's V4 Pro reports a precision lead over GPT-5.5 Pro on a closed benchmark, continuing the Chinese lab's pattern of releasing competitive frontier-class models under permissive licences.

Take the headline number with a pinch of salt. The benchmark is the lab's own and the precision metric is one of several you would want before drawing conclusions; recall, calibration and tool-use scores are not in the announcement. That said, the trajectory is what matters: V4 Pro is the fourth release in eighteen months where a non-Western lab matches a frontier US model on a flagship eval, and the trend line is not going to reverse on its own.

The practical implication for an enterprise buyer is that a single-vendor model strategy is increasingly indefensible. Any architecture that assumes one frontier provider is now a single point of failure on price, geopolitics, and content policy. The interesting design question is no longer "which model" but "how do I wire my system so I can swap the model without rewriting the application". Anyone procuring AI infrastructure should be reading their contract for that exit clause this quarter.

Critical Infrastructure

Texas Grid Flags Risks as Data Centres and Crypto Sites Fail Voltage Tests

ERCOT has warned that a growing share of large industrial loads, including hyperscale data centres and crypto-mining facilities, are tripping offline during voltage events, amplifying disturbances instead of riding through them.

The technical issue is that newer data centres are not behaving like the legacy industrial loads the grid was designed around. Power electronics on modern UPS and crypto rigs are deliberately tuned to drop load fast to protect the equipment, but that fast disconnect turns a manageable transient into a cascade. ERCOT's filings name the problem and hint at coming interconnection requirements that look a lot like the voltage ride-through standards already in force in Europe.

For UK readers this is a preview. National Grid ESO is moving in the same direction and DC operators here should expect tighter Grid Code conditions on new connections. Anyone designing a UK AI deployment around "we will just build a data centre" should also be running the resilience and grid-code conversation in parallel. The energy supply story is now a security and continuity story.

I Design with Claude More Than Figma Now

A Jane Street designer publishes a working note on the shift from visual design tools to LLM-driven prototyping, describing a workflow where Claude Code produces working interfaces faster than they can be sketched in Figma.

The post is not a hot take. The author walks through specific tasks where the trade has flipped, including exploring layout variants, generating accessible component patterns, and translating a written brief into a clickable prototype. They identify where Figma still wins, which is mostly collaboration and design system curation. The honest framing makes this the most credible "AI is changing design" piece on the front page in months.

If you, like Euan, are already running a Claude-centric workflow, the relevance is direct. The design discipline is now a likely next adopter of the same pattern you have in code. Worth forwarding to whoever owns design in your organisation, both as a heads-up and as a template for how to do the transition without losing the parts of design tooling that actually matter.

What to do

Send this to your design lead, but also reread it through the lens of your own Claude Code workflow. The author's framing of what Figma still wins on maps cleanly to a question you should be asking about your IDE: what does it win on that Claude does not, and how long is that runway?

Anthropic, Please Ship an Official Claude Desktop for Linux

A GitHub issue calling for a first-party Linux build of Claude Desktop has rocketed up Hacker News, surfacing the awkward gap between Anthropic's developer-tooling story and the platform most of its developers actually use.

The thread is more interesting than the request itself. Commenters point out that the Claude Code CLI is excellent on Linux but that Cowork and the rest of the desktop surface still assume macOS or Windows, which fragments the experience for the exact audience Anthropic is trying to grow. The maintainers have responded but stopped short of a roadmap commitment. The signal value of nearly five hundred upvotes is hard to ignore.

For an enterprise buyer this is a useful proxy for product priorities. If you are standardising on Anthropic for any serious internal tooling, the platform gap matters and is worth raising on your next account review. Cowork in particular has a Linux-shaped hole you may bump into the moment you try to scale it past one person.

What to do

If you have a path to your Anthropic account team, add a voice to this thread. Product priorities respond to enterprise demand signals, and the Linux gap will eventually constrain how far you can roll Cowork out internally. Worth a sentence in your next QBR.

Industry Reflection

Building From Zero After Addiction, Prison, and a Felony

Gavin Ray writes a quiet, careful account of rebuilding a software engineering career out of conditions most of the industry pretends do not exist, and what the gatekeeping mechanisms actually look like from the other side.

The essay is the most-discussed piece on the front page this morning and deserves its position. Ray is specific about what helped: open source contributions as a portable proof of work, employers willing to read the explanation rather than the background-check flag, and a small number of communities that valued the output over the biography. He is equally specific about what did not help, including a hiring industry built on the assumption that life history is a clean linear line.

Worth reading even, or especially, if you do not personally identify with the path. As a hiring manager it is a reminder that the structures built around "culture fit" and background screening are doing work we may not have intended. As a person it is a reminder that the industry's surface mythology of meritocracy contains a lot of unexamined assumptions about who gets to start from zero.
