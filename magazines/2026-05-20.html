CISA Admin Leaked AWS GovCloud Keys on GitHub

A senior CISA staffer pushed live AWS GovCloud access keys to a public GitHub repository, exposing federal cloud infrastructure to anyone with a search tool and stripping the agency of its own brand-promise: secrets are never safe by accident.

Krebs reports the keys sat in plain view long enough to be indexed and scraped. GovCloud is the high-side environment that runs sensitive federal workloads, and a leak from the cybersecurity agency itself lands harder than the same mistake at any other organisation. The incident is the textbook case study every CISO has been quietly preparing slides for: a senior person, a one-line commit, a public repo, and a key that should have been rotated, vaulted, or never created in human hands at all.

The deeper problem is procedural rather than technological. Every credible scanner from gitleaks to GitHub's own secret scanning would have caught this in seconds, which means the controls either were not enabled or were enabled and ignored. Expect the post-mortem to land on missing pre-commit hooks, weak IAM hygiene, and a culture that still treats personal repos as outside the blast radius. For everyone else, this is a free rehearsal: assume your own people will do the same thing this week.

What to do

Run a secrets-scanning sweep across every repository your staff touch, personal accounts included. Confirm GitHub push protection is on for the organisation, enforce short-lived cloud credentials via OIDC or SSO federation, and rotate any long-lived AWS access keys older than ninety days. Then check what your incident playbook says about a publicly-leaked credential and time how long it would take to revoke it.

Architect of the UK Online Safety Act Calls for Its Complete Repeal

One of the original architects of the UK Online Safety Act has publicly called for its complete repeal, arguing the law has produced surveillance creep, age-verification chaos, and chilling effects on legitimate platforms without measurably reducing the harms it was supposed to address.

When a person who helped draft a piece of legislation breaks publicly to demand its repeal, it usually means the political wind has changed and the practical wind never moved at all. The Online Safety Act has driven smaller forums offline rather than face Ofcom's risk assessments, pushed mainstream platforms toward age-gating that doubles as identity collection, and created a duty-of-care framework that lawyers can interpret but engineers cannot implement. The result is more compliance theatre and less actual safety.

For any UK-based organisation, the practical risk is less the law itself than the audit aftermath when it eventually changes. Data collected for age verification is regulated data, and platforms that hoovered up ID scans to satisfy Ofcom may find themselves on the wrong end of an ICO investigation if the legal basis weakens. Treat the current regime as load-bearing but temporary, and design any new identity flow on the assumption that the rules will move under you within the next eighteen months.

What to do

Inventory any age-verification or identity-proofing data your platforms collect under the Act. Confirm retention windows are minimal, the lawful basis is documented, and deletion is automatic. If you commissioned a third-party age-assurance vendor in the last year, re-read the contract for what happens to the dataset if the underlying obligation disappears. Your DPIA should already cover this — if it does not, today is the day.

GitHub is investigating unauthorized access to their internal repositories

GitHub has confirmed it is investigating unauthorised access to internal repositories, the kind of disclosure that turns every CI pipeline downstream of github.com into a question mark until the scope is published.

The company's status post is brief and the timeline is open, but the underlying concern is not. Internal repositories at GitHub contain Actions runners, signing infrastructure, and the engineering plumbing that millions of organisations trust by default in their build pipelines. Any compromise that touches that machinery has a blast radius measured in customer organisations rather than employees, which is why the incident deserves more attention than the terse acknowledgement suggests.

There is no public evidence yet that customer code or Actions secrets have been touched, and GitHub has handled previous incidents transparently. The play for now is to assume neither best nor worst case and to tighten the few controls that take minutes to change. Treat any unusual Actions activity or unexpected token use over the last week as a potential indicator and pull the relevant logs before they roll off.

What to do

Audit GitHub Actions secrets, rotate any that are shared across repositories, and pin third-party Actions to a SHA rather than a tag. Review token scopes for any service accounts and revoke unused fine-grained PATs. Add an alert on unexpected workflow runs or new self-hosted runners registering against your organisation. Subscribe to the GitHub status feed if you have not already.

Developer Tooling

Gemini CLI will stop working from June 18, 2026

Google is sunsetting the standalone Gemini CLI on 18 June 2026, folding its functionality into the new Antigravity CLI and leaving every script, cron, and shell alias that depends on the old binary roughly four weeks to migrate.

The announcement is a clean reminder that vendor-provided developer tools are operationally unfunded software. Google built Gemini CLI as a wedge into the agentic-coding market, watched the centre of gravity shift to Antigravity, and is now consolidating. That is rational corporate behaviour, but it puts every team that wired Gemini CLI into pipelines or CI on a short clock to either migrate, switch tools, or run a deprecated binary in production.

The wider lesson is to treat AI CLIs the way you treat any other piece of vendor SDK: pin versions, encapsulate calls behind a thin shim, and budget for the deprecation cycle from day one. Antigravity CLI may be more capable than its predecessor, but the next reorg will move it too. Teams that wrapped their automation around a single vendor's surface area are about to spend an unbudgeted sprint catching up.

Agentic Research

Show HN: Forge – Guardrails take an 8B model from 53% to 99% on agentic tasks

A new open-source project called Forge claims that wrapping an 8-billion-parameter model in tight guardrails lifts its success rate on agentic tasks from 53 percent to 99 percent, a result that, if reproducible, makes a real argument for small models with good plumbing over large models with weak constraints.

The Forge approach is unfashionable in the era of trillion-parameter releases. Rather than throwing more weights at the problem, the authors invest in deterministic validation, structured retries, and a state machine that refuses to let the model leave a task before its preconditions hold. The eval is narrow, the dataset is curated, and the headline number deserves the usual scepticism reserved for any 99 percent claim — but the underlying insight is sound. Most agentic failure is not capability failure, it is the model wandering outside the rails.

For builders running Claude or any frontier model in agentic loops, the takeaway is to invest in the harness rather than the head. Tools that validate the model's structured output against a schema, retry on a single bounded failure mode, and refuse to advance the workflow on ambiguous state will move your pass rate further than the next minor version bump. Forge is open source. If nothing else, the patterns translate.

Model Release

Gemini 3.5 Flash

Google has shipped Gemini 3.5 Flash, a small, cheap, fast model aimed at the workloads where latency and unit economics matter more than benchmark gold medals, and aimed squarely at the same niche Anthropic targets with Haiku.

Flash is the workhorse tier rather than the flagship, and the launch matters less for what it does than for what it signals. The frontier labs have all decided that the next year of revenue is in cheap, fast models routed intelligently by middle layers, not in the headline model wars. Google's pricing is aggressive, the context window is comfortable, and the tooling around it slots into the existing Gemini API. Teams already on Vertex will adopt it almost by default.

The interesting question is no longer which model is best, but which model is cheap enough to put on the inside of an agent loop where five or fifty calls happen per user action. Flash makes a credible case for itself there. So does Haiku. The teams that win the next eighteen months will be the ones who route by task rather than committing to one vendor, and the routing fabric is the part that no lab is yet building well.

Critical Vulnerability
Directly applies to you

Copy Fail, Dirty Frag, and Fragnesia kernel vulnerabilities

Three named Linux kernel vulnerabilities — Copy Fail, Dirty Frag, and Fragnesia — landed together via Gentoo, covering memory copy edge cases, fragmentation-driven privilege escalation, and a use-after-free in the network stack that has working proof-of-concept code.

The naming is theatrical but the underlying flaws are not. Dirty Frag in particular allows a local user to escalate to root through carefully sequenced fragmentation primitives, and Fragnesia gives a remote attacker a window into kernel memory if they can shape the right network packets at a vulnerable host. The patches are already in mainline; the work is in getting them onto every server, every container host, and every fleet of edge devices running an older kernel.

Most organisations will patch within a fortnight and forget. The interesting question is the long tail: appliances, NAS boxes, internet-facing routers running stale kernels, and the embedded devices nobody on the security team knows by hostname. Those are where the next opportunistic ransomware campaign will land. Treat the named-vulnerability blast radius as a forcing function to run an honest inventory of which kernels you actually run, not just which ones the inventory tool thinks you run.

What to do

Patch upstream kernels, then enumerate every Linux endpoint your organisation owns and confirm each is running a kernel that includes the fixes. Pay particular attention to embedded devices, appliances, and anything network-facing that was deployed more than two years ago. Add the three CVEs to your KEV-equivalent tracker and set a hard deadline.

Secure by Default

OpenBSD 7.9

OpenBSD 7.9 is out, continuing the project's steady cadence of security-first releases that the rest of the industry quietly borrows from while loudly claiming inventions of their own.

The release notes are unglamorous on purpose. Improved pledge and unveil coverage, kernel hardening updates, a refreshed pf, better hardware support, and the usual quiet janitorial work on the userland. OpenBSD does not chase features, which is exactly why it remains the reference point for what a secure operating system looks like when you optimise the codebase for fewer footguns rather than more checkboxes.

For production fleets the question is almost never whether to switch to OpenBSD wholesale but whether the patterns it institutionalises — privilege separation, default-deny syscall surfaces, smallest-possible attack surface — are visible in your own stack. Read the changelog as a lightweight audit of how your Linux or BSD systems compare. If the OpenBSD team felt a pledge primitive was worth adding, the underlying class of bug is worth checking for elsewhere.

I’ve joined Anthropic

Andrej Karpathy has joined Anthropic, capping a multi-year arc from OpenAI co-founder to Tesla Autopilot lead to independent educator and now back to a frontier lab — a signal that the centre of gravity in AI hiring has shifted from the lab that started it to the one writing the safety-shaped playbook.

Karpathy's choice is not a personality story but a market one. The talent at the top of the field has options measured in tens of millions of dollars and equity in pre-IPO labs. When a person with that optionality picks Anthropic, the read-through is that the work happening there — Claude, the agentic tools, the Constitutional AI program — is where they think the next interesting decade is being built. For any organisation already running on Claude or Cowork, the underlying platform just got reinforcing capital.

The wider signal for technology leaders is that the AI vendor landscape continues to consolidate around three or four serious players, and Anthropic's position is strengthening rather than weakening. Procurement decisions made on the assumption that today's vendor mix is the long-term equilibrium will age badly. Plan for an ecosystem where two or three frontier labs cover most production use cases, and where switching costs between them are the leverage worth investing in.

What to do

If your AI workflows are pinned to a single vendor, schedule a quarterly review of vendor concentration risk. Confirm your prompts, evals, and tooling can move between Claude, Gemini, and one other provider without rewriting the agent layer. The lab will keep shipping faster than your procurement cycle.

In Memoriam

Disney erased FiveThirtyEight

Nate Silver writes about the slow disappearance of FiveThirtyEight from Disney's servers, the methodical removal of a decade of public-interest journalism, and what it says about the durability of any work that lives on a corporation's storage.

FiveThirtyEight was not a small operation. It published thousands of pieces of statistical journalism that researchers, journalists, and curious readers still cite years after the relevant election. Disney's quiet decision to take the archive down is not a content moderation question but an institutional memory question, and the answer is that institutional memory at a publicly-traded media company is exactly as durable as the next quarterly cost review.

The Internet Archive's mirror of the site is now the canonical version, which is its own commentary on the state of the open web. For anyone who has built a knowledge base, a research portal, or a body of writing on infrastructure they do not control, the lesson is to keep your own copy of the originals and to assume that the URL you cite today will return a 404 within five years. Permanence is a property you have to engineer rather than rent.
