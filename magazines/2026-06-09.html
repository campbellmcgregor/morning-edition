Supply Chain Attack
Directly applies to you

Microsoft’s open source tools were hacked to steal passwords of AI developers

Attackers compromised several popular Microsoft open-source packages used by AI developers, injecting credential-harvesting code that exfiltrated API keys and passwords to attacker-controlled infrastructure before anyone noticed.

This is exactly the kind of supply chain attack that keeps CISOs awake. The compromised packages sat in widely-used AI development toolchains, meaning the blast radius extends well beyond Microsoft’s own ecosystem. Any organisation with developers pulling these dependencies—and that includes most teams building on Azure AI services—needs to audit their lockfiles and CI pipelines immediately. The attack vector exploited trust in upstream open-source maintainers, a pattern we’ve seen repeatedly since the SolarWinds and codecov incidents.

What makes this particularly sharp is the target: AI developers often work with elevated API credentials for model endpoints, training data stores, and cloud infrastructure. A stolen AI API key doesn’t just cost money—it grants access to proprietary training data and model weights. The remediation path is clear but painful: rotate every credential that touched an affected environment, pin dependency versions, and implement software composition analysis if you haven’t already.

What to do

Audit your dependency trees for affected Microsoft packages immediately. Rotate any API keys or credentials used in AI development environments. Ensure your CI/CD pipelines use pinned dependencies and run software composition analysis (SCA) on every build.

Surveillance & Privacy

FCC wants to kill burner phones by forcing telecoms to get all customers’ IDs

The US Federal Communications Commission is pushing telecoms to collect government-issued identification from all customers, effectively eliminating anonymous prepaid phones in the name of combating fraud and national security threats.

The proposal frames ID verification as a counter-fraud measure, but its practical effect is the elimination of anonymous communications in the United States. Prepaid phones have long served as a privacy tool for journalists, domestic abuse survivors, activists, and anyone who needs a communications channel that isn’t tied to their permanent identity. Killing that capability in the name of security is a familiar trade-off—and one that rarely delivers the promised safety benefits while reliably eroding civil liberties.

For organisations operating across borders, this matters because US telecom policy often sets precedents that other regulators follow. The UK already requires some form of ID for SIM registration in practice, but a formal US mandate would accelerate the global trend toward mandatory identity verification for all communications. Security teams should track this for its implications on operational security practices, anonymous reporting channels, and whistleblower protections.

Certificate Infrastructure
Directly applies to you

Let’s Encrypt bans certificate usage in any US sanctioned territory

Let’s Encrypt has updated its subscriber agreement to explicitly prohibit the use of its certificates in any territory subject to US sanctions, raising questions about the fragmentation of internet security infrastructure along geopolitical lines.

This is a significant shift in the web’s trust infrastructure. Let’s Encrypt issues roughly half of all active TLS certificates on the internet, and a policy change of this scope has real operational consequences. While most UK and EU organisations won’t be directly affected, any business with operations, partners, or customers in sanctioned regions now faces a certificate management gap. The change also sets a precedent: if the largest free CA can be compelled to enforce geopolitical boundaries, the assumption that TLS is a neutral, universally available technology no longer holds.

For CISOs, the immediate action is to audit whether any of your infrastructure—including third-party SaaS, CDNs, and partner integrations—relies on Let’s Encrypt certificates in ways that could be affected by sanctions compliance. The broader lesson is about certificate authority diversity: if your entire estate depends on a single CA, you’re exposed to policy changes you can’t control. This is a good moment to review your CA strategy and ensure you have fallback options.

What to do

Review your certificate inventory for Let’s Encrypt dependencies, especially in any infrastructure touching sanctioned regions. Ensure your CA strategy includes fallback providers. Check that your compliance team is aware of the updated subscriber agreement terms.

Agentic Architecture

Is Grep All You Need? How Agent Harnesses Reshape Agentic Search

New research examines how the scaffolding around AI agents—the harnesses that orchestrate tool use, search, and retrieval—matters as much as the underlying model for effective agentic search behaviour.

This paper cuts to the heart of something practitioners already suspect: the agent harness—the orchestration layer that decides when to search, what tools to invoke, and how to combine results—is often more important than the raw model capability underneath. The researchers systematically compare different harness architectures for agentic search tasks and find that simple, well-designed orchestration consistently outperforms complex approaches that rely on the model to figure out its own workflow. The title’s provocation (is grep all you need?) is deliberately reductive, but the underlying finding is sound: tool selection and invocation patterns matter enormously.

For anyone building AI workflows, the practical takeaway is to invest more in your orchestration logic and less in chasing the latest model. A well-structured harness with clear tool boundaries, good retrieval primitives, and sensible fallback chains will outperform a frontier model running inside a naive loop. This validates the approach of systems like Claude Code, which pairs model intelligence with a carefully designed tool-use framework rather than treating the model as a black box that should handle everything internally.

Platform Shift

Apple reveals new AI architecture built around Google Gemini models

Apple has unveiled a revamped AI architecture at WWDC that centres on Google’s Gemini models rather than building its own foundation models, signalling a fundamental shift in how the world’s most valuable company approaches on-device intelligence.

Apple choosing Gemini over building in-house is a strategic concession with ripple effects across the entire AI landscape. It means Google’s models will be deeply embedded in the device ecosystem used by over a billion people, giving Google unprecedented reach into Apple’s traditionally walled garden. For enterprises that deploy Apple devices, this creates a new data-flow consideration: AI features that previously stayed on-device may now route through Google’s infrastructure, with different privacy properties than Apple’s historical approach.

The competitive dynamics are worth watching. This partnership potentially marginalises smaller AI providers in the consumer space while simultaneously validating the “model-as-infrastructure” business model. For security leaders, the immediate concern is understanding which AI features will process data locally versus through Google’s cloud, and whether your mobile device management policies need updating to account for this new data pathway. Apple’s privacy reputation has been a selling point for enterprise deployments—this partnership complicates that narrative.

AI Code Quality

Cleaning up after AI rockstar developers

A developer’s account of inheriting and maintaining codebases produced by AI-assisted “rockstar” developers reveals a growing pattern: code that passes review but accumulates hidden debt at an alarming rate.

This piece articulates what many engineering leaders are starting to see: AI-assisted coding dramatically increases output velocity while quietly degrading codebase health. The “rockstar” pattern described here—a developer who ships features at three times the normal rate using AI tools, then moves on before the maintenance burden becomes apparent—is becoming a recognisable archetype. The code works, passes tests, and even looks reasonable in review. But it lacks the structural coherence that makes software maintainable over time, because the AI optimised for the immediate task rather than the long-term architecture.

For security teams, this is not just a code quality concern. AI-generated code that lacks structural coherence is harder to audit, harder to reason about for security properties, and more likely to contain subtle vulnerabilities that surface months later. The mitigation isn’t to ban AI coding tools—that ship has sailed—but to invest in automated analysis tooling, enforce architectural decision records, and ensure that code review processes are calibrated for the failure modes specific to AI-generated code rather than human-written code.

EU AI Regulation
Directly applies to you

Apple decided not to roll out Siri in EU after denied request for exemption

Apple has withdrawn its AI-powered Siri features from the European Union after the European Commission denied the company’s request for a regulatory exemption, marking the first major AI product blocked by EU enforcement.

This is the EU AI Act showing its teeth for the first time against a major technology company. Apple’s inability—or unwillingness—to make Siri’s AI features comply with EU regulations, and the Commission’s refusal to grant an exemption, sets a clear precedent: no company is too large to be held to the regulatory standard. For any organisation deploying AI tools in the EU, this is the clearest signal yet that compliance is not optional and that regulators will not bend the rules for commercial convenience.

The practical implication for UK and EU organisations is twofold. First, if you’re building AI features for European users, your compliance roadmap just became more urgent—the Commission has demonstrated it will enforce rather than negotiate. Second, the withdrawal of Siri’s AI features from the EU creates a fragmented user experience that your support and IT teams need to account for. European employees using Apple devices will have different capabilities than their counterparts elsewhere, which affects any workflow that assumes uniform device functionality.

What to do

Review your AI deployment roadmap for EU AI Act compliance gaps. Ensure your legal and compliance teams are tracking the enforcement precedent this sets. Update internal documentation to account for the divergence in Apple AI features between EU and non-EU regions.

AI Governance

Judge Learns Both Sides Used AI, Cancels Trial, Kicks Everyone Off the Case

A US judge cancelled a trial and removed all attorneys from the case after discovering that lawyers on both sides had used AI to generate legal filings without disclosure, escalating the judicial backlash against undisclosed AI use in courts.

We’ve moved past the “lawyer cites fake cases” phase of AI in legal practice and into something more structurally concerning: both sides of a case using AI without disclosure, effectively meaning the judge was adjudicating a dispute between two language models rather than two legal arguments. The judge’s response—cancelling the trial entirely and removing all counsel—is the most severe sanction we’ve seen, and it signals that courts are losing patience with the lack of transparency around AI use in legal proceedings.

For organisations, this has direct implications for any legal matter where your outside counsel might be using AI tools. The question is no longer whether AI should be used in legal work—it increasingly will be—but whether your legal partners have clear disclosure and quality-assurance policies. Ask your outside counsel directly: what is your AI use policy, and how do you ensure AI-assisted work product meets the accuracy and disclosure standards that courts are now enforcing? This is a governance gap that in-house legal teams need to close before it becomes their problem.

AI Culture

‘Sloppenheimer:’ Amazon Employees Mock the Company’s AI on Slack

Amazon employees have coined the term “Sloppenheimer” to mock their company’s internal AI tools on Slack, revealing widespread internal scepticism about the quality and usefulness of mandated AI integrations.

When your own engineers are coining derisive nicknames for your AI tools on internal channels, you have a cultural signal that no amount of executive enthusiasm can paper over. Amazon’s experience mirrors what many large organisations are discovering: mandating AI adoption without ensuring the tools actually improve workflows breeds cynicism rather than productivity. The “Sloppenheimer” label is memorable because it captures a specific failure mode—AI that produces output that looks impressive at first glance but falls apart under scrutiny, delivered at industrial scale.

This is a useful cautionary tale for any leader rolling out AI tools across an organisation. The gap between “AI can do this” and “AI does this well enough to trust” is where adoption efforts die. Employees who are forced to use tools they don’t trust will find workarounds, generate surface-level compliance metrics that mask real dissatisfaction, and ultimately undermine the initiative. The lesson is to instrument for genuine usefulness—not just adoption rates—and to create safe channels for employees to flag when AI tools are producing slop rather than value.
