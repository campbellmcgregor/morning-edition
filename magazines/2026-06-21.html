Incident · Public Alerting

Unauthorized alert sent to cell phones across Brazil

An unauthorised message was pushed to mobile phones nationwide in Brazil, hijacking the kind of cell-broadcast channel normally reserved for governments to warn of floods, fires, and emergencies — and proving the channel can be commandeered.

Cell broadcast is the most trusted push surface a population has: it bypasses apps, lands on every handset in a coverage area at once, and arrives with the implicit authority of the state. That is exactly what makes its abuse so corrosive. Whoever sent this message did not need a botnet or a phishing campaign — they needed access to, or a flaw in, the alerting infrastructure that carriers and regulators operate on the public's behalf. A single illegitimate blast trains millions of people to distrust the next genuine warning.

For a security leader the lesson is not Brazil-specific. The same emergency-alert plumbing exists across the UK and EU, and it sits at the intersection of telecom operators, government agencies, and a long chain of integration vendors — a textbook supply-chain and authorisation problem. The interesting question is not "could this happen here" but "who can prove they authorised the last alert, and how fast would anyone notice a fake one." Trust in public-safety channels is a national-resilience asset, and it degrades the moment authenticity cannot be demonstrated.

Surveillance & Privacy

Loupe: an iOS app that shows what native apps can see

A research project from Mysk packages a blunt demonstration: open the app and watch, in plain language, the breadth of device signals a perfectly ordinary native iOS app is allowed to read without asking — far beyond the permissions most users imagine they granted.

Permission dialogs trained a generation to think privacy is binary: you said yes to the camera, no to location, and that was the deal. Loupe exists to puncture that comfort. Plenty of valuable signal — device model, system settings, network details, sensor metadata, accessibility state — is readable with no prompt at all, and in aggregate it fingerprints a person more reliably than any single identifier the platform pretends to protect. The app's whole point is to make the invisible legible by simply showing you the haul.

For anyone responsible for a mobile fleet this reframes the threat model. The risk is not only the obviously creepy app demanding location; it is the dozens of mundane apps quietly assembling a fingerprint from data the OS treats as free. That matters for BYOD policy, for vendor app-vetting, and for how much you trust "we don't collect personal data" claims that quietly exclude everything obtained without a prompt. Awareness tools like this are worth handing to staff — seeing the inventory changes behaviour faster than a policy memo.

Data Protection · UK Policy
Directly applies to you

Routing traffic outside the UK over Online Safety policy

A UK technologist writes that the trajectory of the Online Safety Act has pushed him to deliberately route more of his personal internet traffic through nodes outside the country — a quiet, individual vote of no confidence in where domestic policy is heading.

The post is modest in tone and large in implication. The author is not alleging a single catastrophic measure; he is reacting to a direction of travel — age verification, expanding scope, pressure on encryption and lawful speech — and concluding that the rational personal response is to move trust offshore. When competent, law-abiding technical people start treating their own jurisdiction as a privacy risk to be engineered around, that is a signal about the policy, not the people.

For a Scotland-based security leader this is close to home in every sense. The same regime that shapes consumer behaviour shapes the obligations on UK organisations: what you must verify, what you must retain, what you can be compelled to hand over. Watching where individuals choose to route, host, and store is a leading indicator of where customers and staff will expect you to as well. The cheap, defensible move is to know precisely which of your services depend on UK-only routing or storage assumptions before policy — or public sentiment — forces the question.

What to do

Inventory where your organisation's data actually lives and traverses, flagging anything that assumes UK-only routing, hosting, or retention. Brief whoever owns legal/compliance on the Online Safety Act's current obligations versus your controls, and decide proactively where EU or offshore hosting reduces exposure — before a deadline or an incident makes it a scramble.

Agent Security · Tooling
Directly applies to you

Temporary Cloudflare accounts for AI agents

Cloudflare introduces short-lived, automatically-scoped accounts designed for AI agents to act on your behalf — so an autonomous workflow gets just enough access to do its job, and that access evaporates instead of lingering as a permanent credential.

This is infrastructure catching up with how people actually deploy agents. The naive pattern — hand the agent a long-lived API key with broad scope — is a standing liability: the key outlives the task, sprawls across logs and config, and becomes the single most valuable thing for an attacker to steal. Temporary accounts invert that. The agent is issued ephemeral, narrowly-scoped credentials per task; when the work is done the account is gone, shrinking both the blast radius and the window of exposure.

For someone building Claude Code and Cowork workflows, this is the operational maturity layer that separates a demo from production. The principle generalises well beyond Cloudflare: every agent you run should answer "what can it touch, for how long, and how do I revoke it instantly." Ephemerality is the cleanest answer. As agentic automation moves from personal tinkering to something colleagues rely on, treating agent identity as disposable-by-default — rather than as another permanent service account to track — is the pattern worth standardising on early.

What to do

Audit the credentials your existing agent workflows hold: any long-lived, broadly-scoped API key is a candidate to replace with short-lived, per-task access. Adopt ephemeral, narrowly-scoped agent identities as the default for new automations, and make instant revocation a tested capability rather than a theoretical one.

Analysis · Agentic AI

Building reliable agentic AI systems

Writing on Martin Fowler's site, a team at Bayer documents what it actually took to make an LLM-driven agent reliable in production — the guardrails, evaluation loops, and engineering discipline that turn a clever prototype into something a business can depend on.

The piece is valuable precisely because it is unglamorous. Reliability in agentic systems does not come from a better prompt; it comes from treating the LLM as one unreliable component inside a system engineered to contain it — bounded actions, explicit verification of outputs, retries with judgement, and continuous evaluation against real cases rather than vibes. The authors describe the iteration that production demanded: measuring failure modes, narrowing what the agent is permitted to do, and building the scaffolding that catches its mistakes before users do.

For anyone shipping AI-assisted workflows, this is the difference between a tool you trust and a tool you babysit. The same discipline that makes agents reliable also makes them auditable and safe — you cannot evaluate what you have not bounded. Read it as a maturity model: the questions Bayer had to answer about evaluation, observability, and failure containment are the same ones any team will face the moment an agent moves from "helps me" to "acts for the organisation." The engineering rigour is the moat, not the model.

Vendor Risk · Litigation

Tesco sues VMware for breach of contract

UK supermarket giant Tesco has taken VMware to court over an alleged breach of contract — a dispute, resurfacing on the front page, that crystallises the licensing and support anxieties many enterprises have felt since VMware's ownership and pricing changed.

The specifics matter less than the pattern. A blue-chip customer of a foundational virtualisation vendor concluding that litigation is the rational path is the loud version of a quiet conversation happening in countless IT shops: what happens when a vendor you built your infrastructure around changes the terms, the price, or the support model after you are thoroughly locked in? Tesco has the scale to sue. Most organisations only have the option to grumble, absorb the cost, or undertake a painful migration.

For a security and risk leader, this is vendor concentration risk made concrete. Virtualisation, like identity or cloud, is the kind of dependency that is invisible until the commercial relationship sours — and then it is everywhere at once. The discipline worth borrowing is to treat critical-vendor contract terms as a security control: know your exit cost, your renewal exposure, and your realistic alternatives before the negotiation, not during it. Lock-in is a risk you accept deliberately or discover painfully.

Memory Safety · Engineering

Linux eliminates the strncpy API after six years

After six years and roughly 360 patches, the Linux kernel has finished removing strncpy — a C string function whose quietly dangerous semantics have produced a long tail of buffer bugs — replacing it with safer, clearer alternatives throughout the tree.

strncpy is one of those functions that looks safe and is not: it does not guarantee null termination, it pads in surprising ways, and decades of well-meaning code has misused it into subtle memory bugs. Excising it from a codebase the size of the Linux kernel is not glamorous work — it is 360 patches of careful, reviewed, case-by-case replacement, sustained across six years and many contributors. That it is finally done is a small landmark in the long campaign to make systems software memory-safe by construction rather than by vigilance.

The security lesson is about process, not C trivia. Whole classes of vulnerability are eliminated not by clever exploits or new tools but by patiently removing the dangerous primitive so the bug can never be written again. It is the same philosophy behind memory-safe languages and hardened APIs: make the unsafe thing impossible rather than merely discouraged. For leaders, it is a reminder that the highest-leverage security work is often unglamorous, slow, and structural — and that "we removed the footgun" beats "we got better at not shooting ourselves" every time.

Governance · Election Security

White House delays US voting-machine vulnerability report

Reuters reports that the White House has delayed the release of a study into US voting-machine vulnerabilities as the midterm elections approach — a timing decision that raises uncomfortable questions about transparency precisely when public trust in the process is most fragile.

Vulnerability disclosure in election systems is genuinely hard: publish too much detail too publicly and you hand a roadmap to bad actors; publish too little, too late, and you feed the narrative that something is being hidden. But a delay that lands right before an election inevitably reads as political rather than prudent, regardless of intent. The corrosive effect is the same as a fake emergency alert — when people cannot tell whether silence is protecting them or protecting someone else, trust in the machinery itself erodes.

For a security leader the transferable lesson is about disclosure governance under pressure. Every organisation eventually sits on a finding whose release is inconvenient, and the instinct to delay until a calmer moment is universal and usually wrong. Credibility is built by having a disclosure process you follow consistently — timelines, stakeholders, and criteria agreed in advance — so that decisions are not seen to bend to whatever is politically or commercially convenient that week. Transparency is a control; its value collapses the moment it appears discretionary.

Craft & Ethics

When I reject AI code even if it works

A developer makes the case that "it passes the tests" is not enough: code that works but cannot be understood, maintained, or trusted by the humans who own it is a liability — and sometimes the right call is to reject AI-generated code despite a green build.

The argument cuts against the grain of pure velocity culture. Working code is necessary but not sufficient; software is read far more than it is written, and AI is very good at producing output that is locally correct and globally inscrutable. The author's standard is comprehension and ownership: if no human on the team can explain why the code does what it does, the organisation has taken on debt that no test suite measures. Rejecting working code is uncomfortable, but it is a deliberate vote for long-term maintainability over short-term throughput.

For a leader steering teams toward AI-assisted development, this is the cultural guardrail that keeps the productivity real. The danger is not that AI writes bad code; it is that it writes plausible code faster than anyone can meaningfully review it, quietly shifting the bottleneck from writing to understanding. The teams that win with these tools will be the ones that hold the line on review, comprehension, and accountability — treating the model as a fast junior whose work still has to be understood, not an oracle whose output is accepted because it compiles.
