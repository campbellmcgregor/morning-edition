AI Governance

U.S. government will decide who gets to use GPT-5.6

OpenAI has confirmed that access to its newest model will be gated by federal vetting: Washington, not the company, signs off on which organisations are permitted to use GPT-5.6 — a profound shift in who controls frontier compute.

This is the moment the “AI as critical infrastructure” framing stops being rhetorical. When a government inserts itself as the gatekeeper of who may run a commercial model, the model stops behaving like a product and starts behaving like a dual-use export — closer to encryption or centrifuges than to a SaaS subscription. The justification is national-security risk, but the mechanism is a vetting list, and vetting lists have a long history of being opaque, slow, and shaped by politics rather than threat.

For anyone outside the United States, the question is sharper still. If the most capable models ship first to a federally-approved domestic cohort, the rest of the world inherits a structural lag — not because the technology is unavailable, but because the paperwork is. Security leaders should read this as a forecast: the AI supply chain is fragmenting along jurisdictional lines, and your roadmap assumptions about “we’ll just call the best model’s API” now carry geopolitical risk you didn’t price in.

Surveillance & Privacy

We can still stop California's 3D printer surveillance scheme

The EFF is rallying opposition to a California proposal that would build tracking and reporting obligations into consumer 3D printers — a template for turning ordinary manufacturing hardware into a surveillance endpoint.

The pitch is always public safety — here, untraceable firearms — and the method is always the same: push the monitoring down into the device so it watches the user by default. A 3D printer that phones home about what it prints is, architecturally, no different from a laptop that reports what you type. Once the principle is accepted for one category of “dangerous” hardware, the boundary is purely political, and political boundaries move.

The EFF’s point worth internalising is that this is still stoppable, which means it is still a policy fight rather than a fait accompli. For a security leader the relevance is the pattern: regulators increasingly reach for device-level telemetry mandates as a control. Expect the same logic applied to AI accelerators, network gear, and anything that can be cast as a national-security concern — and expect “built-in reporting” to arrive as a compliance requirement, not an opt-in.

Digital Ownership

PlayStation is deleting 551 movies from customers' accounts

Sony is removing 551 purchased films from PlayStation libraries after a licensing deal with StudioCanal lapsed — wiping content that customers paid to “own,” with no refund and no recourse.

“Buy” in a digital storefront has always been a comfortable fiction, and this is the fiction collapsing in public. Customers clicked a button labelled purchase; what they actually received was a revocable licence contingent on contracts they never saw and can’t influence. When the rights expire, so does the “ownership.” The films vanish from libraries that, by every consumer expectation, should have been permanent.

The security and governance lesson generalises well beyond movies. Any asset you depend on but do not control — a SaaS data export, a vendor-hosted archive, an API your product is built on — can be withdrawn when a commercial relationship ends. If continuity matters, contractually-guaranteed offline copies and exit clauses are not paranoia; they are the only thing standing between you and a quiet deletion email. Treat “it lives in their cloud” as a dependency to be mitigated, not a convenience to be trusted.

Developer Tooling
Directly applies to you

Smart model routing directly in Claude, Codex and Cursor

Workweave's open-source router slots into Claude, Codex and Cursor and picks the cheapest model that can handle each request — promising lower spend without a rewrite of your agent workflows.

Model routing is quietly becoming the most practical lever in an AI-assisted workflow. Most prompts do not need a flagship model; a small or mid-tier model answers them at a fraction of the cost and latency, and a router’s whole job is to make that decision per request so you stop paying frontier prices for trivial work. Doing it inside the tools you already drive — rather than behind a separate proxy — is what makes this worth a look.

The caution is the one every router raises: it sits in the path of your prompts and, by definition, sees them. Before wiring an external router into a Claude Code setup, read exactly where requests are evaluated, what is logged, and whether anything leaves your machine. For an individual workflow the upside is real; for anything touching client or sensitive material, the routing layer becomes part of your data-flow diagram and deserves the same scrutiny as any third-party that can read your traffic.

What to do

Trial it on a throwaway project first and watch which models it selects and what it logs. If the routing is purely local and nothing leaves the box, it’s a low-risk way to cut Claude Code spend on routine tasks. Keep it off anything carrying client data until you’ve confirmed prompts aren’t forwarded or retained.

Open Models

The gap between open-weights and closed-source LLMs

A careful analysis argues the frontier between downloadable open-weight models and the closed flagships is narrowing on quality but widening on the things enterprises actually buy: tooling, reliability, and support.

The headline-grabbing benchmark gap between open and closed models keeps shrinking, and that is the part everyone repeats. The more useful observation in this piece is that raw capability was never the whole purchase. What closed providers sell — and what open weights make you assemble yourself — is the surrounding apparatus: evaluation harnesses, guardrails, uptime, abuse handling, and someone to call when it breaks. For a lone researcher, open weights win on control and cost; for an organisation, the total cost of ownership looks very different.

For a security leader this reframes the open-versus-closed decision as a build-versus-buy decision in disguise. Self-hosting open weights buys you data residency and independence from the vetting regimes now appearing around closed models — genuinely valuable as jurisdictions fragment. But it transfers the entire safety, monitoring, and patching burden onto your team. The right answer is rarely ideological; it is a portfolio call, matched to the sensitivity of the workload and the maturity of the team running it.

Frontier Models

Previewing GPT-5.6 Sol: a next-generation model

OpenAI previewed GPT-5.6 “Sol,” pitching step-change gains in reasoning and agentic reliability — the same model whose access, per today’s lead story, will now be gated by government vetting.

Read on its own, this is a routine capability announcement: a new flagship, better reasoning, stronger tool use, the usual cadence. The numbers will matter to anyone tuning agent pipelines, and the agentic-reliability claims are the ones to test hardest, because that is where last year’s models still quietly failed in production. Treat the preview as a prompt to re-run your own evals, not as a reason to rip out a working stack.

The context is what makes Sol interesting. The same launch that promises a more capable model arrives bundled with a gatekeeping regime deciding who may use it. That pairing — more power, more control over distribution — is the real shape of the frontier-AI market now. Capability and access are being negotiated together, and the practical question for planners is no longer just “is it better?” but “will I be allowed to use it, and on what terms?”

Isolation & Sandboxing

MicroVMs: isolated sandboxes with full lifecycle control

AWS Lambda now exposes microVMs with full lifecycle control, giving developers Firecracker-grade isolation for running untrusted code and AI agents with strong boundaries and managed teardown.

The timing is not a coincidence. As AI agents increasingly execute code, browse, and call tools on a user’s behalf, the question “where does this run, and what can it touch?” becomes a first-order security problem. MicroVMs answer it with hardware-backed isolation that is far stronger than a container yet far lighter than a full VM — and exposing lifecycle control means you can spin a clean sandbox per task and destroy it after, leaving no persistent state for an agent to poison.

For a security leader, this is the infrastructure that makes “let the agent run arbitrary code” defensible rather than reckless. The pattern to adopt is ephemeral, single-use sandboxes with no standing credentials and tight egress control, so a compromised or manipulated agent is contained to a disposable boundary. It won’t solve prompt injection, but it dramatically limits the blast radius when injection inevitably happens — and blast-radius reduction is the part of agent security you can actually buy today.

U.S. allows Anthropic to release Mythos AI to 'trusted' US organizations

The U.S. government has cleared Anthropic to release its powerful “Mythos” model to a select set of trusted American organisations — the closed-model mirror image of the GPT-5.6 vetting on today’s cover.

Two of today’s biggest stories tell the same tale from opposite ends: where OpenAI’s newest model is gated by who Washington will vet, Anthropic’s Mythos is gated by who Washington will trust. Either way, the most capable systems are arriving first inside a curated, U.S.-centric perimeter. For someone whose daily workflow is built on Claude and Cowork, this is not abstract — it is a direct signal about which Anthropic capabilities reach you, and when.

The likely outcome for a UK security leader is a tiered world: a frontier tier available to approved U.S. entities, and a general tier for everyone else, with a lag between them. That lag is now a planning input. If a roadmap assumes parity of access to the best Anthropic models, stress-test it against the possibility that the strongest capabilities sit behind a trust boundary you cannot cross — and make sure nothing critical depends on a tier you may not be cleared for.

What to do

Watch Anthropic’s availability notices for whether Mythos-class capabilities reach UK customers and on what terms. Don’t build a critical workflow on the assumption of frontier-tier access; design it to degrade gracefully to generally-available Claude models, and keep an eye on whether “trusted org” gating starts shaping enterprise contracts in your region.

In Memoriam

Om

John Gruber marks the death of Om Malik — blogger, founder of GigaOm, and one of the writers who shaped how a generation thought and wrote about technology — in a brief, personal tribute.

Om Malik belonged to the era when independent technology writing carried real authority — when a single thoughtful blogger could set the agenda that the trade press followed. He built GigaOm into a genuine institution, survived a serious illness that reordered his priorities, and kept writing with a humanity that the analytics-driven content of today rarely matches. Gruber’s tribute is short because the people who knew his work do not need much said.

It is worth pausing on, even in a feed dominated by model launches and policy fights, because Malik represents something the industry keeps mislaying: the value of independent voice, patient judgement, and writing for readers rather than for engagement. In a week where the news is largely about who controls the machines, it is a fitting reminder that the things worth remembering are usually the people, and the clarity they brought.
