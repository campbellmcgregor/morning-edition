Zero-Day Disclosure
Directly applies to you

Anonymous GitHub account mass-dropping undisclosed 0-days

An unattributed GitHub repository, “exploitarium,” is publishing working proof-of-concept exploits for previously undisclosed vulnerabilities with no coordinated-disclosure window — handing attackers and defenders the same loaded weapon at the same moment.

The repository drops full exploit code against widely deployed software without warning the affected vendors first. That detonates the usual playbook: there is no patch window, no embargo, no quiet period for defenders to prepare. The instant a PoC lands, the time-to-exploitation collapses from weeks to hours, because commodity attackers no longer need to develop the capability themselves — they fork it. For any organisation running the named products, the exposure window opened the moment the commit was pushed.

What makes this dangerous is not novelty but scale and anonymity. Mass, anonymous, uncoordinated disclosure removes every lever that responsible disclosure was built to provide, and it makes attribution and takedown slow. GitHub will likely pull the repo, but mirrors and forks survive. The defensive question is no longer “will this be weaponised” but “is the thing I run on the list, and do I have compensating controls up before my next patch cycle.”

What to do

Pull the repository's product/CVE list and cross-reference it against your asset inventory today, not at the next change window. For anything you run, assume exploitation is imminent: prioritise emergency patching, tighten WAF/IPS signatures, and raise monitoring on the affected services. Brief your SOC so they know the PoCs are public and can hunt for the specific indicators now.

Surveillance & Exposure
Directly applies to you

IP Crawl: a living atlas of open webcams on the public internet

A continuously updated index catalogues webcams and connected cameras that respond on the open internet with no authentication — a searchable map of other people's living rooms, loading bays, and server closets, assembled entirely from devices that should never have been reachable.

This is Shodan's uncomfortable lesson made vivid: vast numbers of cameras ship with default credentials or none, get plugged in, and are forgotten. A crawler that catalogues them turns scattered misconfigurations into a single browsable directory. For the people on the other end of those lenses it is a privacy catastrophe; for an organisation it is a reminder that any device with an IP and a default password is part of your attack surface whether or not IT knows it exists.

The deeper issue is shadow inventory. Cameras, badge readers, building-management controllers and printers rarely live in the CMDB, yet they sit on the same networks as everything else and frequently bridge to the internet through helpful UPnP rules. Tools like this raise the floor for attackers — reconnaissance that once took skill is now a search box — and they should raise the floor for defenders too: if a public crawler can find your device, so can everyone else.

What to do

Run an external scan of your own ranges this week and treat anything that answers as a finding. Inventory IoT and OT devices, kill internet-facing camera and admin interfaces, disable UPnP at the edge, and change every default credential. Put cameras and building systems on segmented VLANs that cannot route outbound to the internet unless there is a documented reason.

Threat Landscape
Directly applies to you

Post-Mythos cybersecurity: keep calm and carry on

As the dust settles on the “Mythos” AI moment, a security practitioner argues the fundamentals have not changed: the threat landscape shifted in degree, not in kind, and the right response to AI-accelerated attackers is disciplined defence, not panic-buying.

The piece pushes back on the prevailing narrative that frontier models have rewritten the rules of attack and defence overnight. Yes, AI lowers the cost of phishing, recon, and exploit development; no, it has not invented a new physics of compromise. The kill chain still runs through the same chokepoints — identity, patching, segmentation, logging — and the controls that worked last year work now, just under more pressure and at higher tempo. The temptation to buy an “AI defence” product for every “AI attack” headline is exactly the reflex to resist.

For a security leader the value here is framing. “Keep calm and carry on” is not complacency; it is a brief against fear-driven spending and toward boring excellence — knowing your assets, closing your gaps, drilling your response. AI changes the speed at which mistakes get punished, which makes the unglamorous work of hygiene more valuable, not less. The organisations that fare worst will be those that chase the narrative instead of fixing the basics it exposes.

What to do

Use this as a board-level talking point to counter AI-threat hype with a resourcing argument: fund identity, asset visibility, and detection-and-response before any “AI security” line item. Re-run your tabletop assuming attackers operate faster and more cheaply, and measure whether your detection and patch timelines can keep pace. Reward hygiene, not heroics.

Privacy Tooling

Choosing a public DNS resolver

A practical, opinionated guide to picking a public DNS resolver, weighing privacy policies, logging, filtering, encrypted transport (DoH/DoT), and performance — the unglamorous plumbing that quietly shapes what your network leaks and to whom.

DNS is the most under-examined dependency on most networks. Every domain a device looks up is visible to whoever runs the resolver, and the default is almost always the ISP — which logs, sometimes monetises, and is trivially compelled. The guide walks through the real differences between providers: who retains query logs and for how long, who supports DNS-over-HTTPS and DNS-over-TLS to stop on-path snooping, and who offers malware or content filtering as a side benefit. The recommendations are concrete rather than tribal.

For a security leader this is both a personal and an organisational lever. Resolver choice is a cheap, high-leverage control: encrypted DNS closes a passive surveillance channel, and a filtering resolver blocks a meaningful slice of phishing and malware at the name-resolution layer before a connection is ever made. The catch is centralisation — pointing all queries at one provider concentrates trust — so the guide's emphasis on reading the actual privacy policy, not the marketing, is the right instinct.

AI Research

DSpark: speculative decoding accelerates LLM inference

A DeepSeek paper details DSpark, a speculative-decoding approach that uses a small draft model to propose tokens a larger model then verifies in parallel — cutting latency and serving cost without changing the output distribution of the big model.

Speculative decoding is one of the most important quiet wins in LLM serving. Instead of generating one token at a time from an expensive model, a cheap draft model guesses several tokens ahead and the large model verifies them in a single pass, accepting the run until the first disagreement. DSpark refines this with better drafting and acceptance strategies, and because verification is exact, the larger model's quality is preserved — you get the same answers, faster and cheaper. The 751-point reception reflects how hungry the field is for inference efficiency.

The strategic relevance is cost and sovereignty. Every factor-of-two on inference latency is a factor-of-two on the economics of running AI features, and techniques like this push capable serving toward smaller, cheaper, potentially on-premise hardware. For a leader weighing whether AI features can be run in-house rather than rented from a hyperscaler, the steady drumbeat of efficiency research is the thing that gradually moves “too expensive to self-host” into “feasible.”

Agent Tooling

Adrafinil: keep a lid-closed Mac awake only while agents work

A small utility keeps a laptop awake exactly when autonomous agents are running and lets it sleep the rest of the time — a neat answer to the new problem of long-running AI agents that need the machine alive but not perpetually burning power.

As people hand longer tasks to coding agents and background automations, a mundane friction appears: the machine sleeps mid-run and the agent stalls. The usual fix — disabling sleep entirely — wastes energy and heat for the ninety per cent of the time nothing is happening. Adrafinil scopes wakefulness to the work itself, holding the system awake only while agent processes are active and releasing it the moment they finish, even with the lid shut. It is a small tool that names a genuinely new category of annoyance.

The signal here is bigger than one Mac utility. Agentic workflows are becoming long-lived enough that they need their own operational plumbing — power management, session persistence, scheduling — the kind of supporting tooling that grows up around any workload that runs unattended for hours. For anyone building AI-assisted pipelines, it is worth watching this layer mature; today it is keep-awake hacks, tomorrow it is proper lifecycle management for agents that outlive a single sitting.

AI & Geopolitics
Directly applies to you

Asian AI startups launch Mythos-like models as Anthropic's export ban drags on

With access to Anthropic's top-tier “Mythos” model gated by US export controls, Asian startups are shipping their own near-frontier models to fill the gap — a reminder that capability restrictions create markets as fast as they close them.

Export controls on frontier AI were meant to slow rivals; the predictable second-order effect is a scramble to build domestic substitutes. As Anthropic's most capable model stays out of reach in much of Asia, local labs are racing to ship comparable systems, and customers locked out of the US supply are happy to adopt them. The result is a more fragmented, multipolar model landscape where “the best model” is increasingly a question of jurisdiction as much as raw capability.

For anyone whose stack depends on Anthropic — and Euan's does, via Claude Code and Cowork — this matters in two ways. First, geopolitics is now a supply-chain risk for AI tooling: availability, pricing, and feature parity can shift on policy, not just engineering. Second, a proliferation of capable alternatives is a hedge worth understanding, even if you stay on Claude. The era of a single obvious frontier vendor is giving way to a portfolio question.

What to do

Treat AI model access as a supply-chain dependency in your risk register: document which workflows rely on Anthropic, what your fallback would be, and how a sudden access or pricing change would hit you. Track the credible alternatives now so a switch is a tested option rather than a fire drill — even if Claude remains your primary.

Hardening

Enhancing X11 application security with LXC

A walkthrough of using Linux containers to isolate X11 applications, blunting the protocol's notorious lack of separation between windows — a pragmatic way to contain the keylogging-and-screen-scraping risks baked into the desktop Linux many engineers run.

X11 has a well-known design flaw: any client connected to the display can read every other client's input and contents, so a single compromised app can silently keylog or screenshot everything on screen. Wayland fixes this by design, but plenty of tooling still depends on X11. The article shows how LXC containers can wrap individual applications, constraining what an untrusted or sketchy app can see and touch without forcing a wholesale migration. It is defence-in-depth for a layer most people never think about.

The broader point is that the endpoint, not just the server, is where a lot of real-world compromise plays out — and developer machines are high-value targets precisely because they hold credentials, source, and production access. Containerising risky desktop apps is the same instinct as sandboxing a browser tab: assume any given program might be hostile and limit its blast radius. For teams running Linux workstations, it is a cheap, concrete hardening step that closes a genuinely old hole.

Power & Accountability

Zuckerberg's war on whistleblowers

Cory Doctorow chronicles Meta's aggressive efforts to silence internal critics and former employees, arguing that suppressing whistleblowers is a Streisand-effect own goal that also chills the disclosures regulators and the public depend on.

The essay catalogues the tactics — NDAs, legal threats, arbitration clauses, and reputational pressure — used to keep former insiders from speaking, and frames them as part of a wider corporate strategy to convert uncomfortable truths into legal liabilities for the person telling them. Doctorow's sharpest point is structural: when the only people who can credibly describe a platform's harms are bound by contracts designed to keep them quiet, accountability depends entirely on the rare individual willing to risk ruin.

For a security and governance audience this is not just a Meta story; it is a culture warning. Whistleblower suppression and a healthy security culture are opposites — the same instinct that punishes the person who reports wrongdoing punishes the engineer who reports a vulnerability or a near-miss. Organisations that want to hear about problems early have to make speaking up safe. The piece is a useful prompt to examine whether your own reporting channels protect people or merely look like they do.

Automation & Labour

Ford hired AI and sacked humans. It backfired badly

Ford's push to replace human workers with AI-driven automation produced costly failures and reversals, a cautionary tale about treating AI as a headcount-reduction button rather than a tool that still needs human judgement around it.

The reporting describes a familiar arc: ambitious automation promised efficiency, displaced experienced staff, and then ran into the messy reality those staff had quietly been managing. When the AI underperformed or hit edge cases, the institutional knowledge needed to catch and correct it had already been let go, turning a cost-saving initiative into an expensive unwind. It is a concrete data point against the fantasy that AI is a clean substitute for people rather than a complement that shifts what people do.

The lesson for leaders deploying AI is governance, not Luddism. The organisations getting value from AI are pairing it with human oversight, keeping experts in the loop, and automating tasks rather than firing the judgement that surrounds them. Cutting headcount first and hoping the model holds is the pattern that keeps backfiring — in manufacturing, in support, and increasingly in software. Deploy AI to augment capacity and free people for higher-value work, and keep the humans who understand what “wrong” looks like.
