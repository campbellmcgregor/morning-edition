Privacy · Government access · Data retention

Google broke its promise — and now ICE has the data.

Cory Doctorow's account at the EFF of how an opt-out disappeared, a retention schedule quietly shifted, and the data Google swore it wouldn't keep ended up in the hands of US Immigration and Customs Enforcement.

The top-scoring story on Hacker News this morning (1,313 points, 570 comments) is not a product launch. It is an argument that the platforms carrying most of our identity metadata cannot be relied on to honour their own word. The piece traces a specific case where Doctorow says Google assured him certain data would not be retained, and where that same data was later handed to a federal enforcement agency following a lawful process request.

For UK and EU organisations, the interesting fact is not the US domestic politics. It is that the retention and disclosure pathway sat entirely inside a US-headquartered vendor's infrastructure, regardless of where the data subject lived. That is the CLOUD Act problem in concrete form. Your Data Processing Agreement may say one thing about subprocessors and data location. The enforcement order lands in California.

The piece will get picked up by privacy campaigners and likely by regulators. It will also be read inside board rooms, because "we promised it wouldn't be kept, and it was" is the cleanest possible version of the residual-risk story you have been trying to tell finance for two years.

What to do

Before next board: pull your top five US-headquartered SaaS providers, map where user-generated content and telemetry actually lives, and confirm what their lawful-access posture is. If you process UK or EU personal data through those services, make sure your Transfer Risk Assessments explicitly address US government access under FISA 702 and the CLOUD Act. If they don't, this article is the reason to revisit them this quarter.

Surveillance · Censorship circumvention

Stealth signals are slipping past Iran's internet blackout.

IEEE Spectrum on the satellite-TV side-channels, low-bandwidth mesh protocols and steganographic tricks being used to keep Iranian citizens connected during successive national outages.

The piece reads as technical journalism but it is effectively a field report on what "resilient comms under a hostile regime" looks like in 2026. The techniques in scope include piggybacking on one-way satellite TV signals, short-burst HF, and protocols that look enough like legitimate streaming traffic to survive deep-packet inspection.

For a security leader the operational relevance is twofold. First, the threat model: the same tradecraft works in the other direction, and will appear in criminal exfiltration channels long before it appears in your IDS signatures. Second, the resilience model: if you have operations, suppliers, or staff in countries where internet access is politically contingent, the piece is a useful reading list for what realistic fallback comms look like when a corporate VPN is no longer an option.

It is also a reminder that "the internet" is a policy object, not a physical constant. The countries where it can be switched off now include some where Western firms still have active ERP connections.

Vulnerability · Windows · Privilege escalation

RedSun: SYSTEM on a fully patched Windows box, day one after the April update.

A public proof-of-concept posted this morning claims local privilege escalation to NT AUTHORITY\SYSTEM on Windows 10, Windows 11 and Windows Server, still working after the April 2026 Patch Tuesday rollup.

The repository (Nightmare-Eclipse/RedSun) appeared on Hacker News this morning with 36 points and effectively zero discussion, which is the usual pattern for a fresh exploit: the people who care are on other channels, quietly checking it against their own estate. The summary claims LPE from a standard user context on builds current as of 15 April 2026, including the April 2026 cumulative update.

Two things to watch. First, whether Microsoft's Security Response Center acknowledges the underlying bug class and issues an out-of-band — the timing, immediately after Patch Tuesday, suggests the researcher may have held it specifically to bypass the rollup. Second, whether any EDR vendor ships a detection for the behavioural signature before a mitigation is available.

Local privilege escalation on its own is not game over. Combined with a phishing-driven foothold, it shortens the path from user-mode initial access to domain-privileged lateral movement by approximately one morning.

What to do

Ask your endpoint team today to (1) confirm they are tracking the RedSun PoC and have a YARA or Sigma rule or an EDR behavioural profile queued, (2) check attack surface reduction and LSASS protection are actually enforced in policy rather than advisory, and (3) tighten any temporary local-admin assignments until Microsoft responds. Do not wait for a CVE before treating this as a working exploit.

Darkbloom: private inference on the Macs that are already in your cupboard.

A Show HN launch proposes running AI inference on idle in-office Apple Silicon hardware, locally, without shipping prompts or documents to a US hyperscaler.

The pitch from Darkbloom (darkbloom.dev, 92 points, 58 comments this morning) is unsentimental: most knowledge-work firms already own a large fleet of M-series Macs that sit at 2% utilisation overnight and during meetings. If you wire them together with a coordinator, you get a meaningful private-inference cluster for the classes of model that actually fit in Metal's unified memory.

For a CISO who is already building AI-assisted workflows on top of Claude and Cowork, the relevant question is not "does this replace frontier models" — it doesn't — but "does this give me a defensible place to run the tasks that cannot leave the perimeter". Examples: summarising unredacted HR material, first-pass review of contracts under NDA, internal knowledge-base Q&A.

Three things to ask the vendor before piloting: how are workloads isolated between the idle-Mac fleet and the user's active session, what is the key-management story for the distributed cache, and does the coordinator itself ever see prompt content in cleartext. The answers will tell you whether this is a real privacy story or a convenient one.

What to do

If you already have a "do not send this to a public model" policy, Darkbloom is a reasonable candidate to add to your AI-tooling shortlist next to Claude for the on-perimeter bucket. Do not pilot without first defining which data classes it would process. Do not let the pilot skip your TPRM workflow just because it runs on your own hardware.

Analysis · Industry critique

"Cybersecurity looks like proof of work now."

Drew Breunig's essay argues the security industry has quietly drifted into a regime where the cost of defending is the point — not the outcome. 346 points, 120 comments, and it is the most-shared piece inside security circles this week.

Breunig's thesis, in short: the volume of tools, frameworks, certifications, audit artefacts and bolt-on controls has grown faster than the demonstrable reduction in breach frequency or impact. The resulting programme is a Rube Goldberg of compliance activity whose real function, like proof-of-work in a blockchain, is to burn effort visibly so that the system appears to be doing something.

He is being uncharitable. Much of modern security work genuinely does reduce risk. But the essay is worth reading because it names something most security leaders have felt but struggle to articulate to a CFO: that we are paying for the appearance of rigour and not always for rigour itself.

The practical takeaway is a test. For any control, tool or process you are currently running, ask: what is the counterfactual? If we stopped doing this tomorrow, what specifically goes wrong, how do we detect that it has gone wrong, and how long until the consequence is felt? If the honest answer is "an auditor complains", the control is ceremonial. If the answer is "we fail to detect lateral movement for three more days", the control is load-bearing.

Applying the test across a mid-sized programme typically pulls ten to fifteen percent of spend into a "could be retired without loss" pile and frees headcount for the two or three things that are genuinely understaffed. That is the board-relevant version of Breunig's argument. Being able to reference his framing at the next audit committee lets you bring the conversation without owning it.

AI tools · Productivity

ChatGPT, now living inside Excel.

OpenAI has shipped a first-party Excel integration. 166 points, 118 comments, and a surprisingly mature feature set for a launch day.

The product lets users invoke ChatGPT as a sidebar inside a spreadsheet, operate on selected ranges, generate formulas, explain cells, and run multi-step analyses without leaving the workbook. It is effectively a direct answer to Anthropic's Claude for Excel, which shipped last year in private beta and widened earlier this quarter.

For security leaders the interesting detail is not the feature list — it is the data path. Early documentation suggests the integration sends selected cell content and inferred column semantics to OpenAI's cloud endpoints, with enterprise controls available under the business tier. That is unchanged from the existing ChatGPT Enterprise posture, but the surface area is wider: a spreadsheet is the most data-dense artefact most non-developers produce, and "send the selection to a model" is a one-click operation your finance team will use forty times before lunch.

If you already have ChatGPT Enterprise, review the default data-sharing setting for the spreadsheet app specifically. If you do not, this is the feature that will prompt the request.

Copyright · Shadow libraries · Default judgment

Anna's Archive loses a $322M piracy case without showing up.

TorrentFreak reports that the operators of the shadow library declined to defend the action brought by Spotify-affiliated plaintiffs, and took a default judgment in the ballpark of three hundred and twenty-two million dollars.

The practical effect of a default judgment of this size is limited — the operators are pseudonymous and outside US enforcement reach, and the hosted content continues to sit behind a lattice of mirrors. But the legal artefact now exists, and it will be used. Expect it to be cited in future ISP-blocking orders in the UK, in anti-piracy takedown proceedings, and as precedent in subsequent actions against any successor project.

The story is relevant to security leaders for two reasons. First, shadow libraries are a staple corner of any OSINT and threat-intel workflow, and many analysts quietly depend on them. A wave of jurisdictional takedowns would be felt quickly. Second, the 392-point thread on Hacker News is itself a useful read, because it is a live debate about the future of open access, preservation, and publisher economics.

If your research or legal function uses Anna's Archive in any capacity, have a conversation with them about alternative sources for case-law collections and academic preprints before the next judgment lands somewhere with extradition teeth.

AI tools · Platform

Gemini is now a native Mac app.

Google has shipped Gemini as a standalone macOS application. 125 points, 59 comments, and a predictably mixed reception.

The build is the usual Electron-and-glue wrapping of the web experience, but with system-level hotkeys, proper window behaviour, and what Google describes as "deeper context from selected text and screenshots". It joins ChatGPT's native Mac app, which has been shipping since 2024, and Claude's, which reached general availability last autumn. The AI assistant category now has four credible native desktop clients on macOS.

For enterprise settings the relevant question is whether the app introduces any new local data paths. Early reports suggest it requests full accessibility permissions in order to read the frontmost window, which is the same posture Apple's on-device Siri now adopts. If your MDM posture blocks accessibility grants by default, Gemini will not work without an explicit exception. That is a useful thing to decide deliberately rather than discover in a ticket.

Governance · Open source · Licensing

"Open source isn't dead" — Strix responds to Cal.com's exit.

Cal.com announced yesterday it is closing its source, citing AI-assisted competitive cloning. Strix's rebuttal argues the opposite: that the answer to that pressure is not to close up, it is to get better at the governance, sponsorship and trademark layers around the code.

The broader debate behind the post (319 points on HN) is a real one. Several mid-sized open-source businesses over the last two years — Redis, Elastic, HashiCorp, now Cal.com — have moved to restrictive licences citing some version of "AI is now training on our code faster than we can monetise it". Strix's position is that this confuses two distinct problems: freeloaders who ship competing SaaS, and licence violations, which existing mechanisms already address.

For a security leader the practical implication is supply-chain inventory. If you have internal tools, skills or automations that currently depend on open-source components from vendors who may follow Cal.com's path — Sentry, MinIO, anything in the "open-core commercial OSS" bucket — this is a good quarter to list those dependencies and check your exposure under the terms of any future licence change. Most of those licences have forward-looking clauses that trigger on version, not on install date. New features stop being free software. Running versions generally don't.

What to do

Ask platform engineering for a list of in-house workflows that sit on open-core projects, flag any that are mission-critical, and price out what a BSL or SSPL relicense would cost you. A one-page memo to the CTO is cheaper than learning the answer during a renewal negotiation.

Legal · AI · Privilege

US v. Heppner: conversations with a chatbot do not get attorney-client privilege.

Judge Rakoff (SDNY) issued a pretrial order on 14 April 2026 holding that a defendant's chats with an AI system — used effectively as ad-hoc legal counsel — are not privileged and are therefore discoverable.

The ruling itself is narrow: Rakoff held that the AI is not a lawyer, and the conversations do not become privileged merely because the user subjectively believed they were receiving legal advice. In the specific case the chats will be disclosed. The broader question the opinion opens, however, is what happens next time someone seeks the same ruling about enterprise deployments of AI tools used in the course of in-house legal work.

For anyone using Claude, ChatGPT or similar tools inside a security or legal function — including during incident response, when the model is asked to triage potentially privileged material — this is the canonical opinion to read before assuming those conversations are safe. They probably aren't. Treat AI-assisted analysis of sensitive matter the way you would treat a conversation with a consultant: useful, logged, and discoverable unless a specific privilege attaches through a specific mechanism.

What to do

With your in-house legal team: (1) write a short policy on what may and may not be discussed with a consumer AI, especially during live incidents; (2) confirm that any enterprise AI tool used for legal-adjacent work has a retention and discovery posture your general counsel is willing to defend; (3) mark Heppner as an opinion to watch — appeals and mirror cases in other circuits will follow within the year.
