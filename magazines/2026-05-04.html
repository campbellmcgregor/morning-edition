Surveillance Operations
Directly applies to you

Bad Connection: Global telecom exploitation by covert surveillance actors

Citizen Lab traces a covert surveillance vendor deep inside global telecom signalling, intercepting calls and locating subscribers across more than 50 networks before a single carrier had a name for the intrusion.

The investigation walks through how mobile interconnect protocols, designed when only state telecoms held the keys, now carry queries from grey-market intelligence brokers as if they were peers. Citizen Lab maps the requests back to a small group of European-incorporated companies selling location-on-demand services to anyone with a wire transfer. The technical detail is grim: SS7 and Diameter abuse continues unabated despite a decade of warnings, and the firewalls vendors charge handsomely for routinely fail open under attacker pressure.

For UK and EU operators, the report names participating networks and routes, which means regulators now have evidence to act on. For enterprise security, the read-across is sharper than the headlines suggest. Anything bound to a phone number, including SMS multi-factor codes, executive whereabouts inferred from device location, and call-routing for response teams, sits inside the threat model. If you are still treating the mobile network as trustworthy infrastructure, this report is the receipt that says you are not.

What to do

Audit every workflow that depends on SMS, voice OTP, or carrier location for assurance. Move executive accounts and break-glass paths to phishing-resistant authenticators. Ask your mobile carrier in writing what SS7 and Diameter filtering is in place, and demand evidence rather than a leaflet. Flag the Citizen Lab report to your fraud and physical-security peers; the named operators include several that serve UK and EU enterprises.

Anti-Tamper Collapse

Denuvo has been cracked in all single-player games it previously protected

After years of cat-and-mouse, the dominant commercial anti-tamper technology has been bypassed across the entire single-player catalogue, undermining the central marketing claim that Denuvo bought publishers a quiet release window.

Denuvo's pitch was always about delay rather than prevention; the value to publishers was the first three weeks before a crack appeared, when day-one revenue lands. The new toolchain reportedly defeats every protected single-player title in the back catalogue, and the technique generalises rather than chasing each title in turn. Several publishers were already quietly stripping Denuvo from older releases to claw back performance and battery life. This week's news will accelerate that retreat and reopen the debate inside studios about whether the licence fee was ever worth the friction it imposed on paying customers.

The story matters beyond gaming because Denuvo represents the high-water mark of commercial software protection. If a budget that buys studio integration, kernel-level hooks, and continuous obfuscation cannot keep a determined community out, every other anti-tamper, anti-cheat, and DRM vendor will be re-pricing their promises this quarter. Anyone selling content protection or copy control to a board should expect harder questions about residual risk and what the protection actually buys you in time, not absolutes.

Patch Cadence
Directly applies to you

Windows quality update: progress we've made since March

Microsoft posts a self-graded report on the patching turbulence that has shaken Windows fleets since the March cumulative update, claiming reduced regression rates while quietly conceding several still-unfixed printer and network driver failures.

The post is part status update, part damage control. Microsoft argues that telemetry on update-related rollbacks has dropped by double digits since March, and that a new staged-rollout policy has caught two driver regressions before they reached general availability. What it does not say is which specific regressions are still outstanding for managed estates, leaving CISOs and IT directors to triangulate from community channels and known-issue dashboards. Several printer manufacturers remain in active dispute with Microsoft over which side owns the post-update breakage, and SMB share regressions tied to the April hardening still show up in support queues.

For UK enterprises that paused updates after March, the practical question is whether to clear the queue now, before the May cumulative arrives and stacks new fixes on top of unresolved ones. Microsoft would prefer you said yes; the realistic answer depends on how exposed your fleet is to the still-open regressions and whether your test ring has room to absorb a fast catch-up. Either way, the gap between the marketing tone and the change-control reality has rarely been wider.

What to do

Re-baseline your patch posture this week. Pull the current known-issues list for the March, April and May cumulatives and map each item to a specific business workflow. If you paused updates, decide explicitly whether to catch up now or stage through May, and document the decision for audit. Brief service-desk leads on the printer and SMB regressions so tickets are triaged correctly rather than blamed on the user.

Agent Tooling
Directly applies to you

DeepClaude — Claude Code agent loop with DeepSeek V4 Pro, 17x cheaper

An open-source harness wires the Claude Code agent loop to DeepSeek V4 Pro through a translation layer, claiming a seventeen-fold cost reduction on equivalent coding tasks while keeping the prompt and tool conventions Claude users already know.

The project is a thin shim that intercepts model calls inside the Claude Code loop and routes them to DeepSeek's open-weights V4 Pro endpoint, with a small adapter to align tool-call syntax. The maintainer publishes a benchmark of agentic coding tasks that DeepSeek V4 reportedly clears at roughly the same pass-rate as Sonnet for one-seventeenth of the dollar cost. Real-world mileage will vary; benchmarks rarely survive contact with a complex codebase, and DeepSeek's hosted endpoint introduces a different data residency story than Anthropic's. But the engineering is real, and the speed of the project shows how interchangeable the harness layer is becoming.

The harder question is governance. Routing the same prompts through a Chinese-hosted model changes who has visibility into your code and intent, and changes the legal surface for any regulated workload. For a Cowork-style setup that mixes confidential context with everyday coding, the cost savings have to be priced against an entirely new vendor risk assessment. Open-weights deployments on UK or EU infrastructure dodge most of that, but at a meaningful uplift on operational complexity.

What to do

Do not point your live Claude Code or Cowork sessions at this without a deliberate model-routing policy. If you want the cost story, run DeepClaude in a sandboxed repo with synthetic data and capture both quality and latency over a representative week. Decide up front which prompts may leave Anthropic's environment, write that policy down, and brief whoever else on your team is experimenting before the convenience runs ahead of the controls.

Workflow Critique
Directly applies to you

Agentic Coding Is a Trap

A long, deliberately unflattering essay argues that the dominant agent-loop workflow optimises for the appearance of progress while quietly degrading the engineer's grip on their own system.

Faye's case is not that the tools are bad, but that the loop they impose pushes the human into a supervisory role they are not actually trained for. You watch the agent generate, test, and iterate, and your sense of what the code does drifts further from the code itself with every pass. He gathers anecdotes from teams that adopted agentic workflows aggressively and found the cost showed up six months later, in mounting incident reviews where nobody on the team could explain the failing path because nobody had written it.

Even if you disagree with the conclusion, the diagnostic work is sharp. He distinguishes between cases where agents genuinely shorten the loop, mostly narrow, well-bounded tasks with strong tests, and cases where the harness functions as a productivity simulator. For anyone running Claude Code or Cowork in anger, the essay is a useful counterweight to vendor demos. It does not tell you to stop, but it tells you what to measure: time-to-debug, novel-bug rate, and how confidently your team can answer questions about code their agent shipped last week.

What to do

Treat this as a prompt to instrument your own agentic workflow. Pick three repos where you've leaned hardest on agent loops, and quietly track time-to-resolve incidents in those repos versus your baseline. If the gap is widening, that's the data that beats any vendor benchmark. Share the essay with whoever is championing agent rollouts internally; it's a fairer reference document than another tooling demo.

Clinical AI

OpenAI's o1 correctly diagnosed 67% of ER patients vs 50-55% by triage doctors

A fresh study of A&E triage at a teaching hospital has o1 reaching the correct primary diagnosis in two-thirds of cases, well ahead of the human triage band, and reopens an awkward conversation about where assistive AI ends and decision-making AI begins.

The headline number deserves caution. The study evaluates initial differential diagnoses, not treatment outcomes, and triage doctors are by design optimising for throughput and risk, not accuracy. Even so, a roughly twelve-point gap is not noise, and the methodology is more transparent than earlier rounds of headline-friendly clinical AI claims. The model was given anonymised triage notes and asked for ranked differentials, scored against the discharge diagnosis. The authors are careful to flag that o1's confident-sounding wrong answers were qualitatively different from the doctors' wrong answers, with implications for how a clinician would integrate the tool in practice.

For NHS and broader UK healthcare bodies, the regulatory question is no longer hypothetical. The MHRA's existing guidance on clinical decision-support software was written before models could plausibly outperform triage clinicians on tasks like this. Expect a flurry of internal pilots, none of which can avoid grappling with liability when the model is right and the doctor overrides, or vice versa. As an industry story, it is also a reminder that the frontier-model upgrades that look incremental on benchmark suites can land discontinuously in specific professional workflows.

IP Leak

Metal Gear Solid 2's source code has been leaked on 4chan

A reportedly complete source tree for the HD remaster of Metal Gear Solid 2 surfaced on 4chan over the weekend, the latest in a steady drumbeat of game-studio source leaks that points squarely at supply-chain and offboarding hygiene.

The leak's provenance is, as ever, unclear, but the file structure and build artefacts visible in the dump are consistent with an internal Konami branch rather than reverse-engineered output. That makes the most likely vectors a third-party porting house, an outsourced QA partner, or an ex-employee taking work home years ago. None of these is unique to gaming. Every studio, agency, and outsourced engineering shop that has ever shared a Perforce credential or an SFTP key with a contractor faces the same residual risk; the difference is that game source code is unusually attractive to the wider internet, so the leak becomes news.

For security leaders the pattern is the lesson. The combination of long-tail outsourcing relationships, weak credential rotation, and source repositories that no one has audited the access list on in years is a slow-motion incident generator. The MGS2 leak will produce no breach notification and no regulator letter, which is precisely why it gets ignored until the data on the tree is your customer's, not Solid Snake's.

Industrial Design

Mercedes-Benz commits to bringing back physical buttons

After a decade of glass-fronted dashboards, Mercedes confirms physical controls will return to its mid-life refreshes from 2027, joining a quiet but meaningful reversal across the European premium segment.

The decision is being driven by two things. First, the European NCAP safety body has signalled that touchscreen-only HVAC and lighting controls will count against star ratings from 2026, which immediately changes the cost-benefit calculation for every premium brand. Second, customer research kept telling Mercedes the same thing: drivers under sixty want physical climate and volume controls, and drivers over sixty are unwilling to forgive their absence. The brand's recent EQS interior, applauded by reviewers and disliked by owners, became the canonical example.

The wider read-across is for any product team whose UX choices have been pulled toward minimalism by manufacturing economics rather than user research. The full-glass dashboard was always partly an industrial-design fashion and partly a way to defer per-model cost. As the regulatory wind shifts and warranty data hardens, the case for tactile primary controls is winning on its own terms. Expect Volkswagen, BMW and the Korean groups to follow with similar announcements before the year is out.

Security Philosophy

Security through obscurity is not bad

A careful and faintly contrarian essay reclaims security through obscurity as a legitimate layer in the defender's toolkit, separating its long-misquoted Kerckhoffs heritage from how the phrase has been used to dismiss perfectly sensible engineering choices.

The argument is not that obscurity replaces sound cryptography or hardened design, but that it sits above them as friction that costs the attacker time. Moving an SSH port off 22 will not stop a serious adversary, but it cuts the noisy half of automated scans by an order of magnitude and reduces alert fatigue for the team. The piece walks through several cases — non-default admin paths, randomised internal hostnames, varied HTTP banners — where obscurity, layered honestly on top of strong defaults, measurably helps without weakening anything underneath.

The essay is worth reading because the orthodoxy has been used as a thought-terminating cliche in too many design reviews. Security teams who reflexively veto any obscurity layer are sometimes optimising for argumentative purity rather than for the workload of the people they're protecting. The honest framing is what the author lands on: obscurity has costs, mostly to your own operability, and benefits, mostly to attacker effort, and the only credible answer is to weigh them rather than chant slogans.

Computing Ethics

A desktop made for one

A short and unfashionable essay argues that the modern personal computer has been quietly redesigned for an audience of one — its vendor — and asks what the machine would look like if it were once again built for the person sitting at it.

Isene's piece is a slow walk through the small surrenders that have accumulated since the era of the truly personal computer. The default search that is also a marketing surface, the assistant that requires a server round-trip to read your own files, the operating system update that resets the choices you made about telemetry. None of these is, by itself, an outrage. The cumulative effect, the essay argues, is a machine that listens more attentively to its manufacturer than to its user, and a generation of people who have stopped expecting otherwise.

The constructive turn is the interesting half. He sketches a personal computing setup built around local-first software, an offline-capable assistant, and the assumption that the machine should default to silent rather than to phoning home. None of it is novel; what is striking is how exotic each choice now looks against the mainstream default. For anyone responsible for an organisation's user computing posture, the piece is a useful reminder that what feels like product progress for the vendor is sometimes a quiet retreat for the user.
