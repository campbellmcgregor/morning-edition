Directly applies to you

CERT is releasing six CVEs for serious security vulnerabilities in dnsmasq

Six fresh CVEs have landed against dnsmasq, the tiny DNS and DHCP daemon shipped quietly inside routers, hypervisors, container hosts, and an embarrassing share of corporate edge appliances.

CERT coordinated the disclosure with Simon Kelley, the project maintainer, and the bundle covers memory corruption and information-leak issues in the DNS resolver path. Because dnsmasq is the default forwarder for so many vendor builds, the blast radius reaches every corner of an enterprise estate that nobody owns explicitly: branch office routers, lab VLAN gateways, the wireless guest network behind the canteen Wi-Fi, the OEM firmware on industrial control gear that the OT team swore was air-gapped. A patched upstream release is out, but the long tail of vendor-shipped builds will not catch up for months.

For a security leader the work is not chasing the headline CVE but inventorying where dnsmasq actually lives. Network access control logs, OEM bill-of-materials documents, asset-management exports from your endpoint and network tools, and the configuration manifests for any Kubernetes node images you maintain are the fastest ways to find it. Treat this as a supply-chain visibility test as much as a patching exercise: the organisations that find it embarrassing this week are the ones who did not have an answer ready.

What to do

Open a tracked task today to enumerate every system running dnsmasq across corporate, lab, branch, OT, and vendor-managed devices. Confirm with networking and infrastructure leads whether your edge firewalls or load balancers embed it. Patch what you control this week, push vendor support tickets for what you do not, and add a compensating control for any device that will not be patched within the month.

Surveillance & Privacy

Canada's Bill C-22 Is a Repackaged Version of Last Year's Surveillance Nightmare

Ottawa has reintroduced the lawful-access expansion that civil liberties groups killed last year, this time wrapped in safer language but carrying the same warrantless data demands and platform mandates.

The Electronic Frontier Foundation walks through how Bill C-22 reuses subscriber-information powers that previously required judicial authorisation, layers in service-provider obligations to retain and produce data on request, and creates a regime that pressures encrypted messaging vendors to engineer access. The Canadian government has packaged this as a child-safety and serious-crime measure, the same framing used in the UK Online Safety Act and the EU Council’s ongoing chat-control negotiations.

For anyone watching legislation as a leading indicator, the pattern matters more than the bill. Lawful-access expansions are now being copy-pasted between jurisdictions, with each one citing the previous as precedent. The contractual and engineering implications eventually reach platform vendors operating in the UK and EU, and any UK organisation that runs hosted communications for staff in Canada or sells SaaS into Canadian public sector will inherit the data-production obligations. The privacy reviews you do this year will quietly start asking new questions.

Data Protection

EFF to 4th Circuit: Electronic Device Searches at the Border Require a Warrant

The EFF has filed an amicus brief arguing that the Fourth Amendment must require a warrant before US Customs officers can pull a traveller’s phone or laptop apart at the border.

The case in front of the Fourth Circuit follows years of warrantless border device searches that have grown both more numerous and more invasive, with forensic extraction now routine rather than exceptional. EFF’s argument leans on the volume and intimacy of data carried on a modern phone: the device is not luggage, and treating it as such has stretched the long-running border-search exception past its rationale. A favourable ruling would split with the Eleventh Circuit and tee the question up for the Supreme Court.

The practical guidance for travelling staff has not changed: a US border officer can still ask for your device, and refusing has consequences ranging from delayed entry for citizens to denial of entry for visitors. What is changing is what corporate device policy should require. Burner devices for sensitive travel, full disk encryption with strong unlock secrets, and a documented procedure for what an employee does if their device is seized are no longer paranoid extras. Privacy law is not going to rescue your incident-response timeline.

Directly applies to you

Dead.Letter (CVE-2026-45185) — How XBOW found an unauthenticated RCE on Exim

XBOW disclosed an unauthenticated remote code execution flaw in Exim, the mail transfer agent that still moves a meaningful share of the world’s SMTP traffic, with a clean exploit path that needs nothing but a TCP connection.

The writeup walks through how the issue lives in Exim’s handling of malformed envelope data before authentication, allowing a remote attacker to corrupt memory and gain code execution as the mail user. XBOW says it confirmed exploitation against current stable releases. A patched build is available, but Exim is one of those packages that organisations forget they are still running: it ships by default on a number of long-lived Linux distributions, and a healthy fraction of the install base lives in legacy mail gateways or shared-hosting providers that nobody has logged into for two years.

The pattern is depressingly familiar: pre-auth RCE in a network-facing daemon, instantly weaponisable, with botnets typically reaching opportunistic mass scanning inside a week. If your perimeter has anything answering on port 25 or 587, you need to know exactly what software version is behind it before the weekend. Exim has had several incidents of this shape over the last decade, and the playbook has not changed: patch, restrict, log, hunt for indicators of pre-disclosure exploitation.

What to do

Run a port-25 sweep across every external IP block your organisation owns this morning, including subsidiaries, marketing domains, and dormant brands. Confirm Exim versions, patch to the fixed release, and where you cannot patch within 48 hours, block port 25 ingress at the perimeter or front the service with a hardened MTA. Then check your mail logs for the last seven days for the indicators XBOW published.

Engineering Essay

Learning Software Architecture

Alex Kladov returns with a long essay on how engineers actually develop architectural taste, arguing that pattern catalogues and architecture books are the worst possible place to start.

Kladov’s case is that architecture is taught backwards in most curricula and most engineering teams. The patterns and diagrams sit at the top of the stack, while the underlying skill is the boring one: reading large amounts of existing code, holding a system in your head at three different levels of abstraction simultaneously, and noticing when a design is paying for itself versus when it is paying interest. He walks through how he learned the discipline at rust-analyzer and Zed, with specific examples of which decisions paid off, which did not, and why each was difficult to evaluate at the time.

For a leader of a technical organisation the value is not the patterns themselves but the diagnostic for spotting a healthy architecture culture. Teams that can articulate the tradeoffs in their own systems, name the failure modes of designs they did not choose, and revisit prior decisions without shame are doing this work. Teams that quote books at each other and cannot explain why their actual architecture looks the way it does are not. The essay is also a quietly devastating critique of how senior interview loops still ask the wrong questions.

AI Tools

Show HN: Needle: We Distilled Gemini Tool Calling into a 26M Model

Cactus Compute has open-sourced Needle, a 26-million-parameter model distilled from Gemini that performs tool-calling well enough to drive agentic workloads on a laptop CPU.

The pitch is concrete: instead of paying frontier-model tax on every tool-call hop in an agent, route the tool-selection step to a tiny local model and keep the heavy reasoning for steps that actually need it. The Cactus team claims competitive function-calling accuracy against models orders of magnitude larger, with inference latency under a tenth of a second on commodity hardware. The release includes weights, training code, and benchmarks against the obvious baselines.

If the numbers hold up in independent testing, this is the kind of release that nudges the agentic stack toward a router-and-specialist pattern rather than one big model doing everything. Anthropic users will recognise the shape: small fast model for orchestration, frontier model for reasoning, deterministic tools for the rest. The interesting wrinkle is licensing and provenance. Distilling from Gemini has commercial-use implications that the project page handles loosely, so anyone considering using Needle in production should read Google’s API terms with a lawyer before shipping.

Breach & Ransomware
Directly applies to you

Instructure pays ransom to Canvas hackers

Instructure, the vendor behind the Canvas learning management system used by most of higher education, has confirmed it paid an undisclosed ransom after attackers exfiltrated customer data from its production environment.

The disclosure, picked up by Inside Higher Ed, says the intrusion involved the theft of student records and course content from multiple institutional tenants, with the ransom paid in exchange for a destruction certificate the company concedes it cannot fully verify. Customer notifications are going out in waves. Canvas is the dominant LMS at UK and Irish universities and is used by a number of UK further-education colleges, so the population of affected students includes a non-trivial slice of British higher education.

The interesting part is not the breach itself but the precedent. A SaaS provider paying a ransom on behalf of its tenants creates a strange new posture in third-party risk: the institution did not pay, did not authorise the payment, and inherits the reputational and regulatory consequences regardless. UK universities will need to walk this through their data protection officers, their information commissioners, and their own incident response retainers. The ICO has been clear that paying ransoms does not extinguish breach-notification duties, and Canvas customers in scope of UK GDPR are still on their own clock.

What to do

If your institution or any organisation in your group uses Canvas, contact your Instructure account team this week and demand the timeline, the categories of data confirmed exfiltrated, and the institutional impact summary. Pull your Canvas DPA and check whether you have notification, audit, and ransomware-payment clauses. Walk your DPO through whether a UK GDPR Article 33 notification is now in scope on your side regardless of Instructure’s position.

Developer Tools

The Future of Obsidian Plugins

Obsidian has published its long-promised roadmap for the plugin ecosystem, including a sandboxed runtime, a signed-plugin store, and a permissions model that finally separates filesystem from network.

The blog post is candid about what motivated the rewrite. Recent incidents involving malicious plugins masquerading as productivity tools, including the Phantom Pulse RAT campaign that abused an Obsidian plugin earlier this month, have made the unrestricted JavaScript runtime untenable. The new architecture introduces a manifest-declared permissions model, isolates plugins in workers with explicit filesystem and network grants, and adds a community-review process before plugins reach the public directory.

The downside, which the post is also clear-eyed about, is that some of the most loved plugins rely on undeclared access patterns that will break in the new runtime. Power users will lose functionality for several months while plugin authors migrate, and a chunk of the long tail will simply never update. For anyone running Obsidian as part of a personal knowledge stack, the trade is clearly worth it. For anyone evaluating Obsidian as a corporate notes tool, the upgrade closes the obvious security hole that made it hard to recommend at scale.

Open Source Governance

Bambu Lab is abusing the open source social contract

Jeff Geerling lays out, in patient detail, how 3D-printer manufacturer Bambu Lab has spent the last twelve months systematically degrading the open ecosystem its products depend on.

Geerling’s indictment is comprehensive: the gradual removal of network protocols that allowed third-party slicers to talk to Bambu printers, the firmware that quietly locks out community tooling, the legal pressure on the maintainers of forks like OrcaSlicer, and the public messaging that frames any pushback as ingratitude. He notes that Bambu is the second 3D-printer vendor in 18 months to walk the same path, after Prusa’s slower drift in the same direction, and argues the playbook is now mature enough to be recognised across consumer hardware.

The broader story is governance. Bambu got to market by leaning on a stack the open community had built and maintained, then closed the doors once it owned the customer. The pattern is unfortunately familiar to anyone who has watched smart-home, networking, or industrial-control vendors over the last decade. For a technology leader evaluating any hardware vendor, the right question is no longer whether the device is open today but what the vendor’s incentive is to keep it open once they reach scale. The contractual answer matters more than the marketing.

AI Ethics

Israel's AI targeting system: how data from a phone become a death sentence

A Los Angeles Times investigation walks through how the Israeli Defense Forces convert mobile-phone signals, social-graph proximity, and behavioural metadata into automated kill-list recommendations.

The piece reconstructs the data pipeline behind the systems known internally as Lavender and Gospel, drawing on interviews with current and former IDF intelligence officers. The reporting describes a probabilistic scoring layer that ranks individuals against learned features of past designated combatants, a recommendation surface that presents the score with minimal context, and a human-in-the-loop step that, in practice, has been compressed to under thirty seconds per decision. The error rate the article reports is not negligible, and the consequence of a false positive is death.

It is a hard read. It is also the most concrete public account yet of what fully operational machine-learning-assisted targeting looks like, and the patterns it describes have already begun to show up in other militaries’ procurement documents. For anyone who works in or around AI safety, security, or policy, this is the file the next generation of dual-use export rules will be written against. Reading it carefully is worth the discomfort.
