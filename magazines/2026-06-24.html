Vulnerability Management
Directly applies to you

Vulnerability Reports Are Not Special Anymore

Filippo Valsorda argues the security report has lost the scarcity that once earned it priority treatment, as AI-generated submissions flood maintainers and turn triage into a denial-of-service problem all its own.

For years the arrival of a vulnerability report carried an implicit gravity: someone competent had spent real effort to find a real flaw, and the only sane response was to drop everything. Valsorda's point is that the economics have inverted. Large language models now generate plausible-looking security reports at near-zero cost, and maintainers — especially in open source — are drowning in submissions that look serious, cite CWE numbers, and dissolve under scrutiny. The signal a report used to carry has been diluted to noise.

For a security leader the lesson is not to dismiss reports but to industrialise how you receive them. Triage capacity, not detection, is becoming the bottleneck. Programs that promised a human response to every submission are quietly insolvent. The teams that cope treat inbound vulnerability reports like any other untrusted input: rate-limited, reputation-weighted, and ruthlessly prioritised by exploitability rather than by the confidence of the prose describing them.

What to do

Audit your own disclosure pipeline before the flood reaches it. Set explicit triage SLAs, require proof-of-concept reproduction before deep engagement, and weight submitter reputation. If you run a bug-bounty program, brief your team that polished-sounding AI reports are now the default, not the exception.

Surveillance & Privacy

Signs You're a Dangerous Terrorist: Using Signal, Moving Zines

Ben Werdmuller dissects how the markers of ordinary digital hygiene — encrypted messaging, anonymity, the circulation of printed pamphlets — are being recast in official rhetoric as indicators of extremist intent.

The piece reads a recurring pattern in which the everyday tools of privacy are reframed as evidence of wrongdoing. Using Signal, carrying cash, distributing zines: each is mundane, each has a centuries-old analogue, and each is now cited in the same breath as genuine threat indicators. Werdmuller's argument is that when the act of protecting yourself becomes suspicious, the chilling effect does the state's work for it — people stop using the very tools that keep them safe.

This matters to anyone responsible for protecting a workforce, not just to activists. The normalisation of "encryption equals suspicion" undermines the case security leaders make internally for end-to-end messaging, password managers, and anonymised research. If regulators and employers absorb the framing, defensible privacy practices get harder to justify. The countermove is to keep articulating why these controls are baseline professional hygiene, not deviance — and to resist policies that treat them as red flags.

Meta Pauses Employee-Tracking Program After Internal Data Leak

Meta has suspended an internal program that monitored its own staff after the surveillance data it gathered leaked inside the company — an irony that doubles as a data-protection cautionary tale for any organisation tempted to watch its workforce.

According to Wired, the program collected detailed signals on employee behaviour and was paused after that very dataset spilled internally — a reminder that any monitoring system you build becomes a high-value target the moment it exists. The data you gather to manage risk is itself a liability: it concentrates sensitive information, expands your breach surface, and creates a record that can be subpoenaed, leaked, or quietly misused. Surveillance infrastructure is a two-edged asset.

For a CISO the governance question is sharper than "can we?" It is "should we, and who guards the watchers?" Employee-monitoring data sits squarely inside UK GDPR's special-category and proportionality tests; collecting it without a defensible lawful basis and tight access controls is a regulatory exposure as much as an ethical one. Meta's stumble is a live demonstration that the controls protecting surveillance data must exceed the controls on the data it was meant to protect.

What to do

Inventory every internal monitoring system you run — DLP, UEBA, productivity tracking — and treat each as a crown-jewel dataset. Confirm a documented lawful basis under UK GDPR, restrict access to a named few, set retention limits, and run a DPIA. If you cannot defend why you collect it, stop collecting it.

Developer Tools

Fired by Google for Building the Workspace CLI

A Google engineer says he was let go after creating an unofficial command-line interface for Google Workspace — a tool thousands relied on — reigniting the perennial tension between useful internal tooling and corporate control.

The engineer, Justin Poehnelt, recounts building a CLI that made Google Workspace scriptable and automatable — exactly the glue tooling power users and administrators crave — only to have it become a factor in his departure. The thread struck a nerve because it sits at the intersection of two truths: developers will always route around clunky official interfaces, and large companies are uneasy about unsanctioned tools that touch their platforms at scale.

For anyone running an engineering org, the cautionary reading is about how you treat builders. Useful internal tools are a signal of where your official product falls short; punishing the builder rather than absorbing the lesson tends to drive talent and shadow IT underground. From a security angle, unofficial CLIs that authenticate against corporate platforms are also a governance question worth getting ahead of — better to provide sanctioned, audited tooling than to leave a vacuum someone else fills.

AI Economics

AI's Affordability Crisis

David Rosenthal marshals the numbers behind the generative-AI boom and concludes the economics do not yet close: inference costs, capital expenditure, and subscription revenue point toward a reckoning that today's pricing has merely deferred.

The essay walks through the widening gap between what frontier AI costs to run and what customers currently pay for it. Hyperscalers are pouring capital into data centres on the assumption that demand and efficiency gains will eventually justify the spend, while end-user prices stay subsidised by investor patience. Rosenthal's sceptical reading is that much of today's pricing is promotional, and that the true cost of inference at scale has not yet been passed to the people who depend on it.

For an organisation building workflows on top of these tools, the strategic risk is dependency on prices that may not survive contact with profitability. Today's cheap API call can become tomorrow's painful line item. The prudent posture is to design for portability — abstract the model behind your own interface, track cost-per-task, and avoid architectures that only pencil out if inference stays artificially cheap. Treat current pricing as a floor that can rise, not a permanent feature.

The Coming Loop

Armin Ronacher sketches a near future where AI coding agents run in continuous, self-directed loops rather than single prompts, and argues the shift will reshape how software is built — and who, or what, is doing the building.

Ronacher's argument is that the interesting frontier is not a smarter single response but the agent that plans, acts, observes, and iterates without a human in every turn. As tools mature, the unit of work shifts from "answer my question" to "pursue this goal until done." He is clear-eyed about the friction — context limits, error compounding, the cost of letting a loop run — but treats the trajectory as inevitable rather than hypothetical.

For someone already building Claude Code and Cowork workflows, this reads as the operating manual for where the puck is going. The practical implication is investing now in the scaffolding that makes loops safe: tight verification at each step, durable state between iterations, and guardrails that stop a runaway agent before it does damage. The teams that win won't have the cleverest prompt; they'll have the best harness around an agent allowed to keep going.

What to do

Treat agentic loops as a capability to build deliberately, not stumble into. Start with low-stakes, well-bounded tasks; add verification gates between iterations; and instrument cost and behaviour so a loop can't quietly run away. The harness skills you build now in Claude Code compound directly as the loop model matures.

Operational Resilience
Directly applies to you

Trains Halted Across Germany After Communications System Failure

Deutsche Bahn brought rail traffic to a standstill across parts of Germany after a fault in its radio communications system — a stark reminder that a single shared dependency can take an entire critical-infrastructure network offline.

The outage, reported by the AP, stemmed from a problem in the radio system trains use to coordinate movements; when controllers lost reliable communication, the only safe response was to stop. No breach is alleged, but the failure mode is exactly the one security leaders worry about: a centralised, indispensable system whose loss cascades instantly into a full operational halt. Safety-critical networks tend to fail closed, which is correct, and also expensive.

For European organisations the lesson generalises well beyond rail. Map your single points of failure honestly — the communications layer, the identity provider, the one SaaS dependency everything routes through — and ask what happens when it simply stops, whether from attack or ordinary fault. Resilience is not only about preventing compromise; it is about degrading gracefully when a core dependency disappears. Tabletop the outage you assume can't happen.

What to do

Run a dependency-failure tabletop this quarter focused on your single most indispensable system — likely identity, network, or a key SaaS provider. Define what "fail safe" looks like, who has the authority to halt operations, and how you communicate when the usual channel is the thing that's down.

Clean Energy

Millimetre-Wave Drilling Bores 100 Metres into Granite

Quaise Energy says it has drilled 100 metres into solid granite using millimetre-wave energy rather than a mechanical bit, a milestone toward the deep geothermal wells that could make clean baseload power viable almost anywhere.

Conventional drills struggle and wear out in the hard, hot rock where the richest geothermal energy lives. Quaise's approach vaporises rock with directed millimetre-wave energy, sidestepping mechanical wear entirely, and the 100-metre granite result is a concrete step from lab demonstration toward field viability. The promise is enormous: if you can drill deep enough cheaply enough, geothermal stops being a regional curiosity and becomes dispatchable, carbon-free power available under almost any site.

It remains early — 100 metres is a long way from the multi-kilometre depths the economics ultimately require, and field conditions are unforgiving. But the significance is directional. Energy is increasingly a security and resilience concern as much as an environmental one, and technologies that promise local, always-on generation reduce dependence on fragile supply chains and volatile fuel markets. Worth watching as infrastructure, not merely as climate news.

Governance

How the War on Terror Primed America for Autocracy

Writing in The Economist, the author argues that two decades of post-9/11 emergency powers, surveillance infrastructure, and normalised exception built the institutional machinery that now makes democratic backsliding easier to execute.

The essay's thesis is that the apparatus assembled in the name of counter-terrorism — expanded executive authority, mass surveillance capability, and a public habituated to security exceptions — did not dissolve when the threat receded. It persisted, and infrastructure built for one purpose is available for another. The argument is less about any single administration than about how emergency powers, once granted, become permanent fixtures that lower the cost of future overreach.

For security professionals this is an uncomfortable but necessary mirror. We build the surveillance and control systems; we are the ones who must reckon with their dual-use nature. The governance lesson is that capability outlives intent, and that the controls and oversight you design today determine how the tools you deploy can be used by people you did not anticipate. Sunset clauses, audit trails, and proportionality are not bureaucratic friction — they are the brakes.

In Memoriam

In Memory of the Man Who Put Red and Green Squiggles Under Words

Microsoft's Raymond Chen marks the passing of the engineer behind one of computing's most quietly universal features — the wavy underline that has flagged our spelling and grammar mistakes for decades, mostly without thanks.

The red and green squiggle is the kind of interface invention so successful it became invisible. Chen's tribute remembers the person who shipped it — a feature now woven into nearly every text field on Earth, silently correcting billions of words a day. It is a reminder that the most enduring software is rarely the flashiest; it is the small, considered affordance that fits so well into how people work that they forget it was ever designed at all.

There is a quiet lesson here for anyone who builds tools. The squiggle respects the user: it flags without interrupting, suggests without forcing, and leaves the final call to the human. In an era racing toward systems that act autonomously and assertively, the humility of a feature that merely points and waits is worth remembering. Good tooling, like good editing, knows when to stay out of the way.
