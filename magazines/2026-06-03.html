Critical Vulnerability
Directly applies to you

1-Click GitHub Token Stealing via a VSCode Bug

A researcher chained a one-click flaw in a VSCode marketplace extension flow into a workflow that lifts a developer's GitHub OAuth token — the kind of finding that turns a quiet workstation into a supply-chain bridgehead.

The write-up walks through how a normal-looking install link can redirect the IDE's auth handshake to an attacker-controlled callback, then exfiltrate a token with the full set of scopes the developer ever approved. No malware, no zero-day in the OS, no exotic phishing. Just the editor doing exactly what it was designed to do, with a flow that nobody auditing the editor and nobody auditing GitHub felt was their problem.

For a CISO this is the same shape as every recent supply-chain incident: identity sprawl in developer tooling, OAuth scopes that nobody can name, and a blast radius that ends at production source control. The fix on the vendor side is straightforward; the harder question is whether your detection stack would notice a token being reused from an unexpected IP, and whether your developers are issued tokens narrow enough that a single compromise does not own every repo they touch.

What to do

Audit GitHub personal access tokens and OAuth app authorisations across the org this week. Force re-auth for any token scoped to repo, workflow, or admin. Confirm anomaly detection in your SIEM fires on PAT reuse from new ASNs.

Surveillance & Privacy

A walking tour of surveillance infrastructure in Seattle (2020)

A 2020 essay resurfaces on the front page: a literal walking tour of the cameras, license-plate readers, and acoustic sensors that turn a downtown into a passive collection grid — quietly, legally, almost invisibly.

The piece is structured as a route. You walk a few blocks, you look up, and the author names what you are looking at: the fixed PTZ, the gunshot triangulation array, the ANPR pole installed by a private parking operator and quietly fed into a public dataset. The instinct is to read it as a Seattle problem, but the same hardware vendors and the same procurement playbook now sit on every British high street.

Why it still matters in 2026 is that the conversation has moved from cameras to inference. The cameras were the cheap part. The expensive part — face matching, gait tracking, cross-feed re-identification — has dropped in price by an order of magnitude in the years since, and the operators of these grids rarely publish a model card. If you run an organisation with a physical footprint in a UK city, your staff are already in someone's training set.

Browser Privacy
Directly applies to you

The advertising cartel coming to your web browser

An incisive read on how the major browsers are converging on a shared advertising attribution API — sold as a privacy win, structured in a way that locks small publishers out and freezes the incumbents in place.

The argument is that Chrome, Safari, and now Firefox have each shipped their own version of in-browser attribution: a sealed pipeline that promises to count conversions without third-party cookies, while keeping the data inside the browser vendor. On paper it is a privacy story. In practice it is a cartel agreement where three companies, all of whom either sell ads or take a cut from the people who do, decide which signals advertisers are allowed to see and which they are not.

For a UK-based CISO this is also a regulator question. The ICO has been increasingly comfortable treating browser-mediated ad measurement as in-scope for PECR and UK GDPR, and the EDPB has been louder. The outcome that the post warns about — a permanent settlement where measurement happens only inside the browser, and only with the consent flows the browser dictates — is one your compliance team should be modelling now, not after the first enforcement action drops.

What to do

Brief your DPO and marketing lead this week on the Topics, Privacy Sandbox, and PAT trajectories. Confirm any vendor contracts that depend on third-party cookie measurement have a written fallback plan and a 2026 sunset date.

AI Tooling Release

MAI-Code-1-Flash

Microsoft ships MAI-Code-1-Flash, a small, fast coding model positioned as the inline-completion tier under their larger MAI-Code line — and the first product release that makes the in-house Microsoft AI lab look like a serious counterweight to OpenAI.

The launch post leans on latency numbers and on benchmark wins against Codestral and the smaller Qwen-Coder variants. The interesting bit is not the benchmarks, which everyone games. It is that Microsoft now ships an inline coding model that does not route through OpenAI infrastructure, paired with the GitHub Copilot App release that hit the front page the same week. The internal politics are visible from orbit.

If you write code with an assistant, the practical implication is choice. The dependency story for tools like Copilot has been a single-vendor one for two years. MAI-Code-1-Flash is the first credible alternative inside the Microsoft estate, and if it lands at the price tier Microsoft hinted at, expect the GitHub UI to start nudging you toward it by default. Whether the model is actually better is almost beside the point. Diversification on the substrate is overdue.

AI Research

AI outperforms law professors in Stanford Law study

Stanford Law published a controlled study comparing frontier LLMs to tenured law professors on a set of bar-grade legal reasoning tasks, and the models won, by a margin that the authors themselves describe as uncomfortable.

The methodology matters. The professors were asked to perform on the same closed-book conditions as the models, with the same time limits, on a set of fact patterns drawn from real cases that post-date the training cut-off of the model under test. The headline result is a meaningful accuracy delta, but the more interesting finding is that the models were better at the procedural steps the professors found tedious — citation hygiene, statute lookup, structuring an issue tree — and not noticeably better at the harder reasoning.

Read in context, this is consistent with what every knowledge-work field has been finding: the model is a force multiplier on the structured eighty per cent and still a junior on the messy twenty. For a security leader who is increasingly asked to engage with legal counsel on contracts, breach notifications, and DPIAs, the takeaway is not that lawyers are obsolete. It is that the lawyer who is fluent with an LLM will work three times as fast as the one who is not, and you should bias your panel that way.

Developer AI

GitHub Copilot App

GitHub previews the Copilot App, a standalone desktop client that decouples Copilot from the IDE — autonomous task execution, repo context, and a UI that looks more like Claude Code than the original sidebar plugin.

The product framing is that you should be able to hand Copilot a GitHub issue, walk away, and come back to a draft PR with the tests passing. The technical framing is that GitHub is shipping the same shape of agentic coding harness that the rest of the field has converged on over the last year. An orchestrator on top of a model, with tool calls to the filesystem, the shell, and the Git remote. The novel bit is the distribution: every paying Copilot seat already has a billing relationship.

The interesting question is what this does to the rest of the agentic-coding market. Cursor, Cline, Claude Code, Aider, and the rest each have a fingerhold; GitHub showing up with a free preview that is one click from any existing Copilot subscription changes the default. Worth installing the preview just to see how the prompts and tool schemas compare with what you already use. The deltas are usually where the engineering decisions are.

Open Source Legal

Adafruit receives demand letter from Fenwick legal counsel on behalf of Flux.ai

Adafruit, the open-hardware company, publishes a demand letter received from Fenwick & West on behalf of Flux.ai, and chooses to make the whole exchange public, with commentary, in a move that has the open-source hardware community paying attention.

The substance of the dispute is narrow: Flux alleges that Adafruit's reference to a specific design feature crosses into trademark territory, and demands changes. The substance of the response is broader: Adafruit, who have been doing open-hardware longer than most of the lawyers writing the letter have been licensed, picked the public-disclosure route on day one. That has historically been how this category of dispute ends, quietly, after one side notices the optics, and it is hard to imagine Flux's PR team welcomed the post.

There is a thread here that is worth pulling for any CISO who sponsors open source internally. The legal exposure of contributing to or shipping open hardware is increasingly being shaped not by patent law but by trademark and trade-dress claims from venture-backed entrants in the same space. The defensive posture has to include a counsel relationship that can read a demand letter the same day it arrives and a policy on which disclosures go public.

AI Infrastructure

Bringing Up DeepSeek-V4-Flash on AMD MI300X

An engineer documents the not-trivial process of bringing up DeepSeek-V4-Flash on AMD's MI300X, with kernel quirks, ROCm gotchas, and a working perf profile that puts the open-weight model into striking distance of an H100-class deployment at a noticeably lower per-token cost.

The write-up is technical and patient. It walks through the specific places where the official inference stack assumes CUDA semantics, the patches that get you past them, and the benchmarking discipline that makes the comparison fair. The headline number is competitive throughput on a 192GB MI300X, which is interesting on its own; the more interesting line is that the patches needed are getting smaller every release.

Strategically this is the supply-chain conversation that does not get enough oxygen. The default AI infrastructure story is single-vendor: Nvidia silicon, CUDA software, hyperscaler rental. AMD's MI300X line has been the closest thing to a credible second source for a year, and posts like this are the boring engineering work that turn that from a slide into a procurement option. If you are scoping a 2027 inference budget, you want at least one bid that lands on AMD.

AI Governance

Trump signs downsized AI order after weeks of reversals

After weeks of public reversals, Trump signs a substantially narrowed executive order on AI: most of the export-control teeth gone, the federal procurement language softened, and the framing reoriented around competition with China rather than risk management.

The Politico read on the timeline is the useful one. The original draft circulated by the White House would have set hard reporting thresholds for frontier training runs and explicit liability for foundational misuse. The signed text retains the threshold language but moves the enforcement mechanism from a regulator to a working group, which in Washington is the standard way of declaring that nothing will happen for at least a year. The lobbying campaign worked, exactly as you would expect.

For UK and EU organisations the immediate impact is on cross-border deployment. The signed order does not change the AI Act timeline, does not change UK regulator guidance, and does not change the GPAI obligations that come into force later this year. What it does change is the negotiating posture of US-headquartered vendors, who will now push harder against any contractual commitments that look like the original White House draft. Expect new model-card and red-team commitments to soften in your next renewal cycle.

AI Threats
Directly applies to you

U of T researchers demonstrate AI worm could target any online device

U of T researchers publish a working demonstration of an LLM-powered worm that can target effectively any networked device with a text interface, propagating through the conversation channel itself rather than through code execution.

The mechanism is grim and elegant. The worm payload is a prompt. When it reaches a device that exposes an LLM-mediated interface — and there are now many, from smart speakers to support chatbots to internal copilots — the prompt is parsed, the agent is convinced to take an outbound action, and the same payload is delivered onward. There is no buffer overflow to patch, no signature to write, no firmware update that closes the class of bug. The vulnerability is the agent's willingness to follow instructions.

For a CISO this is the threat-model conversation that the industry has been deferring for two years. Prompt injection has been treated as a content-moderation problem; this paper treats it as a propagation problem. The defensive surface is unfamiliar: input provenance, capability scoping, and the assumption that any text crossing a trust boundary into an agent should be treated the way you treat an attachment crossing a firewall.

What to do

Inventory every LLM-mediated surface in the estate this quarter — internal copilots, support bots, agentic tooling. For each, document the outbound tool scopes the agent has and define a trust boundary policy for untrusted input. Treat this as the new email-gateway problem.
