Model Theft & IP
Directly applies to you

Anthropic says Alibaba illicitly extracted Claude's capabilities

Anthropic alleges that Alibaba systematically queried Claude to distil its behaviour into a rival model — a claim that turns the abstract risk of "model extraction" into a named, headline-grade dispute between two of the largest AI shops on the planet.

Model distillation through the API is not exotic: you pour a frontier model thousands of carefully chosen prompts, harvest the outputs, and train a cheaper student model to mimic them. What is new is the willingness to say so out loud and point at a specific competitor. If the allegation holds, it means a vendor's terms of service and rate limits were the only thing standing between a multi-year training investment and a near-free copy — and that they failed. For anyone who treats a model API as a durable competitive moat, that is a sobering data point.

The CISO angle is not really about Anthropic versus Alibaba; it is about your own AI surface. Every organisation now exposes models — whether a customer-facing chatbot or an internal copilot — that can themselves be probed, extracted, or jailbroken at scale. The same techniques that allegedly copied Claude can lift a fine-tuned model trained on your proprietary data. Treat model endpoints as the high-value assets they are: monitored, rate-limited, and subject to abuse detection, not just an SDK call behind a key.

What to do

Inventory every model endpoint your organisation exposes and ask who can hit it and how fast. Add per-tenant rate limits, anomaly detection for high-volume systematic querying, and contractual anti-distillation clauses for any model you let partners call. If you fine-tune on sensitive data, assume the weights' behaviour is extractable and threat-model accordingly.

Surveillance & Privacy
Directly applies to you

Routing traffic out of the UK, on principle

A UK security writer describes quietly re-routing more of his own traffic through nodes outside Britain, not for speed but as a response to where the Online Safety regime is heading — a small, telling act of jurisdictional self-defence from someone who reads the legislation closely.

The piece is personal rather than corporate, but the instinct it documents is one every UK technology leader should recognise. As the Online Safety Act's duties bed in — age assurance, content scanning, the standing tension with end-to-end encryption — the writer's calculation is that the safest place for his data may simply be somewhere the regime does not reach. When competent, security-literate individuals start treating their own government's online-safety framework as a threat to design around, that is a signal about trust, not paranoia.

For a CISO based in Scotland, this is the policy weather you operate inside. The same forces that push an individual toward foreign exit nodes shape your decisions about where to host data, which messaging tools to sanction, and how to answer a board that wants to know whether UK regulation helps or hinders the security posture. The honest answer is increasingly "both" — and the gap between compliance and genuine privacy is widening enough that staff will route around it whether or not policy permits them to.

What to do

Map your organisation's exposure to the Online Safety Act and adjacent UK duties now, before enforcement detail forces a scramble. Decide deliberately where regulated data lives and which encrypted tools you will defend. And watch for shadow-IT routing — VPNs and offshore nodes adopted by privacy-conscious staff — as a usability signal worth heeding, not just a control to clamp down on.

Web App Exploitation
Directly applies to you

Walking through Johnson & Johnson's web apps

A researcher documents chaining flaws across several Johnson & Johnson web applications, reaching data and functionality that should have been firmly out of bounds — a reminder that brand size and security maturity are not the same thing.

The write-up is a tour of unglamorous failures: weak authorisation checks, exposed internal endpoints, and the kind of object-reference bugs that have topped the OWASP list for a decade. None of it required a zero-day; it required patience and a willingness to read responses carefully. That is precisely what makes it instructive. A pharmaceutical giant with the budget to do better still shipped public-facing apps where the boundary between "logged-in user" and "everyone's records" was thinner than anyone intended.

For a security leader, the lesson is to resist the comfort of assuming your own externally facing applications are fine because nobody has complained. The researcher's path — enumerate the app's real attack surface, test authorisation on every endpoint, look for the forgotten admin tool — is the same path a hostile actor walks, only quieter. Customer-facing web apps acquired through M&A or built by a fast-moving product team are the usual blind spots; they rarely get the scrutiny that the crown-jewel systems do.

What to do

Commission an authorisation-focused test of your public web apps — specifically IDOR and broken access control, not just a vuln-scanner sweep. Prioritise anything inherited via acquisition or built outside the core platform team. Confirm every API endpoint enforces object-level authorisation server-side, and stand up a vulnerability-disclosure channel so a friendly researcher reaches you before a hostile one does.

Identity & Access
Directly applies to you

Cloudflare ships self-managed OAuth for everyone

Cloudflare has opened up a self-managed OAuth capability to all customers, lowering the bar for teams to put standards-based authorisation in front of their own services and APIs without standing up a bespoke identity stack.

OAuth is one of those primitives that everyone depends on and almost nobody enjoys operating. Token issuance, scopes, rotation, and revocation are easy to get subtly wrong, and a misconfigured authorisation server is a direct route to account takeover. By folding self-managed OAuth into its edge platform, Cloudflare is betting that more teams will reach for a vetted implementation than will roll their own — a reasonable bet, given how many homegrown auth layers turn into liabilities the moment the engineer who wrote them leaves.

The strategic question for a CISO is concentration versus correctness. Pushing identity to a major edge provider buys you a hardened, maintained implementation and one less thing your own team can fumble. It also deepens a dependency on a single vendor sitting astride your authentication path — an availability and trust consideration, not just a security one. Neither answer is automatically right; what matters is making the trade deliberately rather than discovering the dependency during an incident.

What to do

If you already run on Cloudflare, evaluate whether self-managed OAuth can retire a hand-rolled auth layer you would rather not maintain. Pilot it on a non-critical service, validate token lifetimes, scope handling, and revocation, and document the new vendor dependency in your concentration-risk register before it becomes load-bearing across production.

AI & The Commons

PR spam now looks like email spam circa 2003

A maintainer-facing essay argues that AI-generated pull requests have reached the volume and low signal of early-2000s email spam — drowning open-source projects in plausible-looking contributions that cost far more to review than they were ever worth.

The analogy is sharper than it first appears. Email spam worked because sending was nearly free while filtering was expensive; the economics only tipped back once Bayesian filters and reputation systems raised the cost of getting through. AI-authored PRs follow the same curve. A model can generate a confident, well-formatted contribution in seconds, but a human maintainer still has to read it, reason about it, and decide whether it quietly breaks something. The asymmetry is brutal, and it is eroding the volunteer goodwill that open source runs on.

For anyone whose supply chain rests on open-source projects — which is everyone — this is not just a culture-war story about etiquette. Maintainer burnout and degraded review quality are security problems. A flood of low-effort contributions makes it easier for a genuinely malicious change to slip through in the noise, and it pushes exhausted maintainers toward either abandoning projects or rubber-stamping changes they no longer have time to scrutinise. The defences being floated — contribution rate limits, reputation gates, proof-of-effort — are the same reputation economics that tamed spam, now arriving for code.

Agentic AI

Gemini 3.5 Flash learns to use a computer

Google has added computer-use capabilities to its fast, cheap Gemini 3.5 Flash tier — letting the model click, type, and navigate interfaces directly, and pushing agentic control of real software from premium demos toward commodity pricing.

Computer use — an agent that drives a real browser or desktop rather than calling tidy APIs — has been the headline trick of the last year of model releases. Putting it in the Flash tier matters because cost is what decides whether a capability stays a demo or becomes infrastructure. When an agent can operate a UI for a fraction of a cent per step, the economics flip in favour of automating long-tail tasks that were never worth a bespoke integration: filling forms, reconciling dashboards, shepherding records between systems that don't talk to each other.

That same cheapness is the security story. An agent that can use any interface a human can use is, by definition, a credential-bearing automaton clicking around inside your environment — and a target for prompt injection delivered through whatever page it reads. For someone building AI-assisted workflows, the question is no longer whether to let agents touch real systems but how to scope what they can reach, log what they do, and contain the blast radius when a hostile web page tells the agent to do something its operator never asked for.

Supply Chain

When the package registry leans on GitHub

A pointed argument that crates.io's reliance on GitHub for authentication makes a single commercial platform a load-bearing dependency for the entire Rust package ecosystem — a fragility that supply-chain attackers and outages alike can exploit.

The critique is structural, not anti-GitHub. If publishing to a language's central registry requires authenticating through one company's identity system, then that company's outages, account-takeover bugs, and policy decisions all become the ecosystem's problem. A compromise of a maintainer's GitHub account becomes a direct path to poisoning a widely used crate; a GitHub authentication outage becomes a publishing outage for everyone. Centralising trust is efficient right up until the centre wobbles.

This is the supply-chain conversation that breaches keep forcing back onto the agenda, viewed from the plumbing rather than the headline. For a security leader, the takeaway is to know where the single points of trust sit in the toolchains your developers depend on — not only the packages you pull, but the registries, identity providers, and CI systems those packages flow through. Diversity of trust roots is unglamorous resilience work, and it is exactly the kind of dependency that is invisible until the day it isn't.

Practical Security

SSH tunnels, properly explained

A clear, hands-on guide to local and remote SSH port forwarding — the everyday plumbing that lets engineers reach internal services safely, and that turns into an exfiltration channel when nobody is watching how it is used.

SSH tunnels are one of those skills that separate people who fight their infrastructure from people who quietly route around its rough edges. Local forwarding pulls a remote service to your machine; remote forwarding exposes a local service outward; the guide walks both with the kind of concrete examples that finally make the direction of the arrows stick. For anyone who still copies tunnel commands from old notes without quite trusting them, it is worth the read on competence grounds alone.

The security leader reads the same guide with a second eye, because every capability here is dual-use. The remote forward that lets a developer demo a local app is the same mechanism an attacker uses to punch a reverse tunnel out through your firewall and keep a foothold alive. Understanding exactly how these tunnels work is the prerequisite for detecting the malicious ones — spotting the unexpected long-lived SSH session, the egress to an odd port, the jump host nobody authorised. You cannot monitor what you do not understand.

AI Infrastructure

OpenAI's first custom silicon, built by Broadcom

OpenAI has unveiled its first in-house AI chip, designed with Broadcom — a move to wean its enormous compute appetite off a single supplier and claw back margin, and a marker of how vertically integrated the AI giants now intend to become.

The strategic logic is plain. Training and serving frontier models is a capital problem as much as a research one, and being beholden to one dominant GPU vendor for both price and availability is a position no company that size wants to hold. Designing custom silicon — even with Broadcom doing the hard engineering — gives OpenAI leverage on cost, supply, and the ability to tune hardware to its own workloads. It is the same playbook the hyperscalers have run for a decade, now reaching the model labs themselves.

For a security leader the relevance is indirect but real. The infrastructure your AI vendors run on shapes their resilience, their pricing power, and ultimately their longevity — all things you inherit when you build on top of them. A market consolidating into a handful of vertically integrated giants with their own chips, data centres, and models is a market with fewer, larger points of failure. That is the supply-chain backdrop against which every "which AI vendor do we bet on" decision now gets made.

Research Integrity

The quiet flood of machine-made studies

Medical students, Science reports, are using a popular research tool to mass-produce misleading studies — churning out statistically dubious papers from shared datasets faster than the literature can absorb or correct them.

The mechanism is grimly simple. Open health datasets plus an automated analysis tool let anyone generate a publishable-looking association study with little understanding of the statistics underneath. The output is plausible, formatted, and wrong often enough to matter — p-hacked correlations dressed as findings, submitted in volume to journals that cannot keep pace. It is the research-integrity cousin of the AI-PR-spam story earlier in this issue: cheap generation overwhelming expensive review, with the commons paying the cost.

This belongs in a security-minded edition because trust in information is the substrate everything else rests on. When the medical literature fills with confidently produced noise, clinicians, policymakers, and the public lose the ability to tell signal from sludge — and the same dynamic is coming for every field where generation is cheaper than verification. Defending the integrity of the record, whether it is code, scientific papers, or security advisories, is becoming one of the quiet, central problems of the era, and there is no tidy technical fix waiting in the wings.
