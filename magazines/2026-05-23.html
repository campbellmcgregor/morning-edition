Lawmakers Demand Answers as CISA Tries to Contain Data Leak

Krebs reports that the U.S. Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency is scrambling to contain an internal data leak as members of Congress demand answers about what was exposed, who saw it, and how an agency built to defend critical infrastructure failed to defend itself.

The detail that should give every defender pause is the chain of trust. CISA is the agency that allied CISOs around the world rely on for threat intelligence sharing, indicators of compromise, and emergency directives. If sensitive internal data was exposed, it is not just a U.S. embarrassment. It is a question about whether the joint-cyber-defense pipeline that downstream CSIRTs and NCSC partners plug into is structurally trustworthy. UK and EU CISOs should treat the leak less as foreign news and more as a prompt to audit their own intel-sharing dependencies.

Krebs is also careful to point at the political reaction rather than the breach itself. Lawmakers are framing this as a governance failure rather than a technical one, which is the frame regulators tend to import into UK sectoral guidance afterwards. Expect more pressure on agencies and contractors to demonstrate not just that they were compromised, but that they could explain who, what, and when within hours. For boards, the lesson is small and clear: the speed of your post-incident answer is now part of your control posture, not separate from it.

What to do

Review your dependence on CISA advisories and KEV-feed automation. If your detection logic or vendor patches rely on the U.S. pipeline, log it in the risk register today and add an NCSC or ENISA fallback source so that a single foreign disclosure event does not blind your SOC.

Surveillance & Influence

TikTok Disproportionately Served Anti-Democratic Videos During the 2024 Election

A new academic study finds that TikTok's recommender system pushed disproportionately anti-Democratic political content during the 2024 U.S. election cycle, sharpening the long-running argument that algorithmic feeds are not neutral utilities but editorial actors operating at platform scale.

The methodology matters more than the headline. The researchers used controlled accounts with matched demographic and behavioural profiles, then measured what each account was actually served. That is the right design, because it isolates the recommender from the user. When matched accounts diverge in what they see, the explanation lives inside the platform, not the audience. For privacy professionals it is also a reminder that recommender systems are processing political-opinion data even when users have not knowingly disclosed it.

For UK and EU teams the regulatory teeth are sharper than in the U.S. The Digital Services Act treats VLOPs' recommender systems as auditable, and Ofcom's online safety duties already require risk assessments for politically sensitive content. Expect findings like this to be cited in the next round of DSA risk-assessment scrutiny. The interesting second-order question for security leaders is whether your own organisation's social listening or executive-protection feeds are being shaped by the same recommender bias, and whether your analysts know.

FBI Director's 'Based Apparel' Site Caught Hosting a ClickFix Malware Attack

PCMag confirms that a merchandise site associated with FBI Director Kash Patel was found serving a 'ClickFix' social-engineering attack, prompting visitors to paste a malicious PowerShell command into Run as part of a fake verification flow.

ClickFix is now the dominant initial-access pattern in opportunistic intrusions. The recipe is the same in every variant: a legitimate site is poisoned, a fake CAPTCHA or 'fix this issue' modal appears, and the victim copies an attacker-supplied command into Windows Run. There is no exploit, no patchable CVE, no software vulnerability to fix. It is a workflow attack, and the only durable mitigation is to break the workflow itself. That is hard for organisations that still let standard users open Run, paste from clipboard, and execute PowerShell unmonitored.

The political dressing of this incident will absorb most of the press attention. The technical signal underneath is more useful. Watering-hole compromises on niche merchandise and community sites are scaling, and your users will keep landing on them through search ads and social links. Detection needs to live at the endpoint, not the perimeter: clipboard-to-Run telemetry, command-line auditing, and AppLocker or WDAC policies that prevent unsigned scripts from executing in user context. Awareness training alone will not catch it, because the user believes they are following instructions from the site.

What to do

Push a ClickFix-specific advisory to your end-user comms this week with screenshots, and ask your EDR team to confirm they are alerting on powershell.exe launched from explorer.exe with clipboard-derived arguments. If you cannot answer that question in an hour, that is the gap to close.

Staged Publishing and New Install-Time Controls Arrive for npm

GitHub has landed staged publishing and install-time controls in the npm registry, giving maintainers a hold-and-verify window before a new version goes public and giving consumers the ability to block postinstall scripts and unsigned releases at the install step.

Staged publishing is the most significant change. For years, every npm publish was an irrevocable event the moment it hit the registry, which is why typosquats and compromised maintainer accounts cascaded into thousands of CI pipelines within minutes. Staging introduces a hold window where a release exists but is not yet resolvable by npm install, allowing maintainers and downstream security tools to scan, sign, and confirm. It is the missing control that the Log4Shell and event-stream eras kept asking for.

Install-time controls are the consumer-side half of the same idea. Organisations can now refuse to execute postinstall scripts, refuse unsigned packages, or refuse versions that have not been staged for a minimum period. That last lever is the one that matters operationally. If you configure your private registry to reject anything younger than, say, twenty-four hours of staging, you absorb the cost of waiting in exchange for an enormous reduction in malicious-package blast radius. The upgrade is not free, but the controls are finally there to be turned on.

What to do

Ask your platform team to enable install-time blocking of postinstall scripts and unsigned releases in your private npm registry this sprint, and to set a minimum staging age for direct dependencies. Document the change in your SBOM policy.

Applied Cryptography

A Blueprint for Formal Verification of Apple's corecrypto

Apple's security team has published a blueprint describing how it is using formal-verification tooling to mathematically prove properties of corecrypto, the cryptographic library that ships at the bottom of every iOS, macOS, and watchOS device.

This is a careful, technical piece and it is worth reading slowly. Apple is not announcing a verified library. It is describing the methodology and the tooling stack it is using to get there: HACL*, F*, and a domain-specific layer that maps Apple's optimised assembly implementations back to abstract cryptographic specifications. The interesting move is that they are verifying the optimised code, not a clean reference implementation. That is where bugs actually live, and it is the part of the supply chain that auditors usually cannot read.

The broader signal is what counts for non-Apple defenders. Formal verification of production cryptographic code is leaving research labs and becoming a vendor expectation. AWS, Google, and now Apple have all published variations on this story in the last two years, and procurement teams are starting to ask for evidence in security questionnaires. If your organisation ships software with embedded cryptography, expect 'where is the verification artefact?' to appear in customer due-diligence questionnaires within the next eighteen months. The right answer is no longer 'we test it'.

Anthropic Publishes the First Update on Project Glasswing

Anthropic has released the first public update from Project Glasswing, its internal effort to develop transparency and oversight tooling for production deployments of frontier models, and the writeup gives an unusually concrete look at how the company is thinking about audit trails and reasoning visibility.

The post pushes past the usual abstractions about 'interpretability' and into engineering. Glasswing is being framed as a layer that sits alongside a model in production and exposes structured artefacts the model would otherwise hide: which tools it considered, which it rejected, which retrievals influenced an answer, and where confidence dropped. For anyone deploying Claude into a business workflow, that is the missing instrumentation. Today the only honest audit answer for most agent deployments is 'we trust the model'. Glasswing aims to replace that with evidence.

For Euan and anyone building with Claude Code or Cowork, the practical takeaway is that the next twelve months of agent work will increasingly be judged not on what the agent did, but on whether you can prove what it did and why. Hooking your agents into Glasswing-style telemetry early will be cheaper than retrofitting it later. It is also the kind of artefact that buys you a much shorter conversation with internal audit and external assurance teams when they ask how decisions were made.

What to do

Sign up for early Glasswing access through your Anthropic contact and prototype the integration in a non-production Cowork workflow this month. Capture one example trace and use it in your next internal AI-governance review.

Microsoft Begins Cancelling Claude Code Licences Inside Its Developer Tools

The Verge reports that Microsoft has started cancelling Claude Code licences embedded in its own developer tooling, including Notepad's AI features, as the company consolidates around its in-house models and OpenAI partnership stack.

Strip away the consumer framing and this is a textbook vendor-lock story. Microsoft routed users through Anthropic for code-related features, then pulled the licence with limited notice once an internal alternative was ready. Customers who built workflows on top of those features now have to migrate, retrain, and re-procure. There is no security incident here, but there is a control failure: the third-party AI provider underneath your tooling can be swapped by your platform vendor without you. That risk used to live mainly with browser extensions and identity providers. It now sits inside the IDE.

For organisations that have standardised on Claude Code as a productivity tool, the read is sharper. The provenance of the Claude licence in your environment matters. A subscription you bought directly from Anthropic behaves very differently from a Claude entitlement bundled by a platform vendor. The former you control; the latter can disappear at a vendor's product-strategy meeting. Treat AI-coding assistants the way you already treat SSO providers, and put renewal and exit clauses in the same risk register entry as Okta and Microsoft Entra.

What to do

Audit which of your Claude entitlements are direct from Anthropic versus bundled through a third party, and document the exit cost for each in your AI vendor risk register. If you are paying Microsoft for an embedded Claude experience, switch to a direct Anthropic contract before renewal.

AI Pricing

DeepSeek Makes Its V4 Pro Price Cut Permanent

DeepSeek has converted what was a promotional discount on V4 Pro inference into a permanent price reduction, deepening the gap between the leading Chinese open-weight family and Western frontier APIs at the per-token level.

The price move is small in dollar terms and large in market terms. DeepSeek V4 Pro is now meaningfully cheaper per million tokens than any comparable Western frontier model, and the company has signalled that the pricing is structural rather than a campaign. That changes the calculus for anyone running large batch workloads, evaluations, or agentic pipelines where token cost dominates. It does not change the calculus for production workloads where data-residency, regulatory exposure, or auditable model provenance still matter more than unit cost.

For UK and EU teams the interesting question is governance rather than price. Using a Chinese-hosted frontier model for sensitive corporate data is not a procurement question, it is a compliance one, and the National Cyber Security Centre's guidance has been getting steadily firmer on supply-chain exposure to high-risk jurisdictions. The right move is usually to run DeepSeek through a Western inference partner that can give you a hosting and data-handling contract you can defend to your DPO. Cheap is only cheap if the bill of materials passes audit.

U.S. Researchers Face New Restrictions on Publishing With Foreign Collaborators

Science magazine reports that U.S. federal agencies are tightening rules around what American researchers can publish jointly with foreign collaborators, a shift that touches everyone from defence labs to civilian universities working with European and UK partners.

The new restrictions are framed as research-security guardrails, but the practical effect is to chill cross-border co-authorship. Joint U.S.-UK projects in cyber, AI safety, and biosecurity have been the connective tissue of a lot of the public threat-intelligence and standards work that defenders depend on. If U.S. researchers cannot publish freely with UK and EU partners, the shared evidence base shrinks. Standards bodies, ENISA working groups, and NIST joint papers all run on this kind of collaboration, and they will be slower and thinner as a result.

For UK organisations the immediate impact is procurement-shaped. If your research partners or technology vendors rely on U.S. federal funding, expect publication, data-sharing, and personnel-disclosure clauses to appear in contracts in the next funding cycle. CISOs and DPOs should read these contracts before they are signed rather than after. The clauses can reach into your own data-handling obligations in ways that are not obvious, particularly around personnel nationality and disclosure controls.

What to do

Ask your legal and research-partnerships teams whether any of your active U.S. collaborations involve federally funded counterparties. If so, request the most recent grant terms and check for new publication or disclosure clauses before your next deliverable lands.

Domain-Camouflaged Injection Attacks Evade Detection in Multi-Agent LLM Systems

A new arXiv preprint shows that prompt-injection attacks dressed in plausible domain language slip past current defences in multi-agent LLM pipelines, with the routing agent failing to flag the malicious instructions before they reach a downstream specialist.

The contribution is uncomfortable. The authors construct injections that read like legitimate domain content for the role the receiving agent expects to play. A finance-routing agent forwards what looks like a benign report; the downstream finance agent treats the embedded instructions as task context. Existing detectors, which are largely trained on adversarial-looking strings, miss the attack because nothing about the surface text looks adversarial. The success rate across the tested systems is high enough that 'we have prompt-injection defences' is not a sufficient answer for any multi-agent deployment.

For anyone building agent systems on Claude Code or Cowork, the read is direct. Subagent boundaries are the new privilege boundaries. The orchestrating agent must treat input from other agents the way an operating system treats input from a less-trusted process: validate the schema, refuse out-of-band instructions, and log every tool call with provenance back to its originating message. Treating subagent traffic as 'internal' is the multi-agent equivalent of trusting the LAN, and we know how that one ends.

What to do

Audit your current Cowork agent graph. For each subagent handoff, decide whether the receiving agent should accept free-form text or only a constrained schema, and downgrade the trust on any free-form path. Add a tool-call log review to your weekly agent-quality routine.
