Scammers are abusing an internal Microsoft account to send spam links

Researchers found scammers piggybacking on a legitimate internal Microsoft account to fire spam links into inboxes, the messages arriving fully authenticated and waved through by trust filters built to look for exactly the opposite.

The attack does not exploit a bug so much as a configuration: a misused internal sender that customers and detection stacks both treat as gospel. SPF, DKIM and DMARC all pass. Once the messages are inside, the spam-link payloads run with the implicit credibility of a Microsoft.com origin, which is precisely the property that defeats the secure email gateway sitting in front of most enterprises. Microsoft has been notified and is investigating, but as of this morning the route is still live.

For anyone running an M365 estate the exposure is binary. You either have controls that look beyond authentication results, or you do not. Detection-as-code rules that key on sender reputation alone will miss this entirely. The more interesting question is what other internal-account-as-relay scenarios are quietly being abused inside the tenant boundary, where the trust assumptions are even softer than at the perimeter.

What to do

Pull the IOCs from the TechCrunch piece into your SOC ticket queue this week. Validate that your email security stack alerts on links inside messages from internal Microsoft sender addresses, not just unknown ones. Brief the helpdesk so they stop telling users "if it is from microsoft.com it is safe".

Oura says it gets government demands for user data

Oura, the wearable maker whose ring quietly tracks sleep, heart rate and movement, has acknowledged for the first time that it receives government demands for user data, though it will not yet say how many.

The disclosure matters because wearables sit in a privacy grey zone most threat models still ignore. Heart rate variability and sleep patterns can imply pregnancy, illness, stress and travel, all of which are increasingly attractive to investigators and increasingly subpoenable from third parties under the third-party doctrine. Oura has not published a transparency report; the company says one is coming, but until then customers have no visibility into volume, jurisdiction, or compliance rate.

For organisations whose senior people wear these devices, this is the moment to ask the boring question. What data leaves the wrist, who holds it, under whose law, and what happens when a foreign government issues a request to a US-headquartered processor of EU residents data. The right answer is probably not "we will worry about it when it happens". The right answer is a written stance, communicated to the executive team, that treats consumer wearables as in scope.

What to do

Add wearable and quantified-self devices to your executive privacy briefing. Decide whether sensitive roles should disable cloud sync or use brands that publish transparency reports. Ask your DPO whether any current Oura processing involves UK or EU residents and how a US legal demand would be handled under the UK-US Data Access Agreement.

Biometric Surveillance

ICE Awards $25M Iris-Scanning Contract to Bi2 Technologies

US Immigration and Customs Enforcement has awarded a twenty-five million dollar contract to Bi2 Technologies for iris-scanning capability, normalising biometric identification at a scale and operational tempo that has not previously had federal funding.

Iris recognition is harder to fool than face and harder to revoke than a password. Once it is in a federal database, it is in there for life. The contract pushes the technology from prisons and jails, where Bi2 already operates, into immigration enforcement where the consent model is, generously, weak. Civil liberties advocates argue this is a textbook function-creep moment: the system stood up for one population gets repurposed for another, and the second population had no say in being enrolled.

The broader read for security leaders outside the US is that biometric procurement is moving faster than biometric governance everywhere. The Online Safety Act age-checks debate in the UK and the EU AI Act prohibitions on certain biometric uses are both still being interpreted in real time. Vendors are not waiting. Procurement decisions made now will outlive whoever signed them.

Open Source / History

Microsoft open-sources "the earliest DOS source code discovered to date"

Microsoft has released what is being described as the earliest discovered build of MS-DOS source code, a 1981 artefact that predates the version that went out with the original IBM PC, posted to GitHub under the MIT licence.

The release is mostly archaeological, but the choice of licence is the interesting part. MIT means the assembly listings can be excerpted, taught and forked without lawyer involvement, which matters for the small but serious community of retrocomputing maintainers. The diffs against later 1.x releases will fill in a few longstanding holes in the historical record around CP/M compatibility, BIOS boundary handling and the early Tim Paterson code paths.

For anyone who learned to program against later DOS, the listings read like a museum tour through a vanished set of constraints: eight kilobytes of resident code, hand-coded interrupt vectors, deliberate sacrifices for floppy speed. The educational value is real and the licensing decision is, in 2026, a reassuringly normal corporate move. Worth a slow Sunday read with a coffee.

Making deep learning go brrrr from first principles (2022)

Horace He’s 2022 essay on making deep learning go fast is back on the front page, and it is the rare piece that has aged into a near-canonical reference for anyone reasoning about why a model trains at the speed it does.

The argument is simple and the implications are not. Deep learning performance is governed by three regimes — compute-bound, memory-bound, and overhead-bound — and intuitions developed in one regime do badly when transplanted to another. He walks through the arithmetic intensity calculations that tell you which regime you are in for a given operation, and the rules of thumb that follow: when fusion helps, when it does not, when chasing FLOPS is wasted effort because you are bandwidth-starved.

The essay is worth re-reading now because the regimes have shifted under our feet. The 2022 era was dominated by attention kernels on A100s. In 2026 the H200 and B100 generations, longer-context architectures, and the rise of inference-time compute have moved many workloads decisively into bandwidth-bound territory. The mental model still holds. The constants do not. That is a reasonable summary of most useful engineering writing.

Directly applies to you

-​-dangerously-skip-reading-code

A new essay arguing against the now-common Claude Code flag that skips reading existing code before editing it has hit the front page, and the disagreement it has provoked maps directly onto the divide between agentic AI optimists and the engineers cleaning up after them.

The author’s thesis is straightforward. Disabling the read-before-write step in a coding agent is a local time saving paid for with global correctness debt: changes get made against a model of the code rather than the code itself, regressions slip past tests, and the diff is harder to review because the agent cannot explain choices it never grounded. The piece names names and shows diffs, which is what gives it teeth.

For anyone running Claude Code or Cowork inside an engineering workflow this is not abstract. The pattern the author critiques is exactly the one that turns a useful collaborator into a confident vandal in larger repos. Worth circulating to the team along with a working agreement on when, if ever, the flag is allowed. The default answer should be: never on shared code, sometimes on a throwaway script, only with an explicit user override.

What to do

Audit your team’s Claude Code and Cowork configurations for any use of dangerously-skip flags or equivalents. Add a written norm to your engineering handbook that agents must read affected files before editing them, with a documented exception process. Bring this up at your next AI tooling sync.

Civil Liberties

Texas woman arrested for Facebook post about town water quality

A Texas woman was arrested after a Facebook post questioning the quality of her town’s drinking water, an enforcement action that has prompted lawyers and free-speech groups to ask what statute, precisely, the police believe she violated.

The reported facts are these: a resident posted publicly about discolouration and taste in tap water and was charged under a state misinformation statute. The legal hook appears to be a provision originally written for terrorism hoaxes, now being stretched to cover what looks a lot like ordinary citizen complaint. The story is being amplified because it lands at a moment when several US states are testing the edges of what speech online can be criminalised for, and because the chilling effect on legitimate public-health reporting is obvious.

For practitioners in the UK and EU it is a useful reminder that the same legislative pattern — broadly drafted online harms statutes being applied beyond their original scope — is not a uniquely American phenomenon. The Online Safety Act, the Digital Services Act and equivalent regimes elsewhere all contain provisions whose first contested enforcement cases will set important precedents. Worth tracking even if you don’t draft policy yourself.

Developer Safety

Improving C# Memory Safety

Microsoft’s .NET team has published the design notes for the memory-safety improvements landing in upcoming C# releases, a quiet but consequential shift in how the language handles spans, lifetimes and the unsafe surface.

The headline change is a tighter, statically checked model for ref-struct lifetimes that closes several long-standing footguns around stack-allocated buffers escaping their scope. It is not Rust, and it does not claim to be: the compromise is that the existing C# idioms keep working, but the compiler now refuses the small subset that used to silently produce undefined behaviour. The proposal also lays the groundwork for a path away from the unsafe keyword for common interop scenarios.

For enterprise teams the practical effect is that some classes of vulnerability — use-after-free in pooled buffers, span-based parsing bugs — should become harder to write and easier to spot. That matters because .NET continues to be a dominant runtime in finance, government and healthcare, exactly the sectors where memory-safety issues have the most expensive blast radius. The .NET 11 preview cycle is the right time to read the spec and start planning migrations.

Spanish court declines to fine NordVPN over LaLiga piracy blocking order

A Spanish court has declined to fine NordVPN for refusing to enforce LaLiga’s sweeping piracy-blocking order against its own users, the first significant judicial check on a regime that had previously been treated as functionally automatic.

The blocking order, originally issued at LaLiga’s request, required ISPs and adjacent providers to cut access to a moving target list of IP ranges. The collateral damage has been steady: Cloudflare-fronted unrelated sites going dark during matches, payment processors briefly unreachable, and the predictable VPN workaround becoming so popular it inverted the original purpose of the order. The court’s reasoning, summarised in the filing, leans heavily on proportionality and on NordVPN’s lack of a local establishment.

The ruling is narrow but the read-across is broad. EU member states have been testing whether private rights-holder injunctions can be exported into the infrastructure layer, and several UK enforcement bodies have been watching closely. A judicial brake from any one jurisdiction matters; a Spanish brake on a sports-rights case matters more, because the LaLiga regime had become the working template.

What to do

Add this judgement to your legal-watch queue and flag it for the General Counsel. If your organisation operates VPN, CDN or DNS services into Spain, ask outside counsel whether the reasoning extends to your category. UK readers: track whether the Premier League replicates the LaLiga playbook in the autumn season and what your statutory obligations would be if it does.

Accountability

Air France and Airbus found guilty of manslaughter over 2009 plane crash

A French appeal court has found Air France and Airbus guilty of manslaughter over the 2009 crash of Flight 447, reversing earlier acquittals and ending a seventeen-year legal arc with a finding that corporate decisions, not just pilot error, were at fault.

The crash, which killed 228 people after pitot tubes iced over and the autopilot disengaged at altitude, has been studied in safety circles for a generation. The new ruling explicitly attributes part of the causal chain to known deficiencies in the pitot tube model, to insufficient pilot training for high-altitude stall recovery, and to a corporate posture that treated both as acceptable risks. It is the first time the French criminal system has held a major airframer and a flag carrier jointly responsible at this level.

The judgement will be cited far beyond aviation. Anyone whose work touches safety-critical software or hardware should read the reasoning, because it lays out a workable doctrine for corporate manslaughter that turns on documented internal awareness of risk plus a decision not to act. That doctrine has obvious applications to algorithmic harms, to autonomous systems, and to any vendor whose telemetry shows failure modes that were not communicated to the buyer.
