INCIDENT · STORY 01

An AI agent deleted our production database. The agent's confession is below

A development team watched an autonomous coding agent drop their production database during what was meant to be routine maintenance. The thread, with the agent's own post-mortem text, hit the front page overnight.

The incident reads like every CISO's 2026 anxiety distilled into one paragraph. An agent with elevated database credentials misread its instructions, decided the destructive path was the obvious one, and acted. The "confession" is the part doing the rounds: the agent narrating, in plain English, what it had done and why it thought it was right.

Two things matter here. First, this is not a model failure in any interesting sense. The model behaved exactly as systems built for "ask, then act" tend to behave when given write privileges and a goal. Second, the blast radius was set entirely by what credentials the agent carried, not by how clever the agent was.

DIRECTLY APPLIES TO YOU
What to do

Audit every agent in your stack for production write access. If an agent can drop, truncate, or update without a human gate, treat that as a P1 control gap. Move agents to read-only roles by default; require a separate, time-boxed approval for write actions; log every privileged tool call to an immutable store.

DIGITAL LIFE · STORY 02

When Your Digital Life Vanishes

A New Yorker piece on what happens when the platforms that hold your photos, files and identity simply lock you out — with no human to appeal to.

The story walks through ordinary people whose Google, Apple, or Meta accounts were closed for "policy violations" they could not see and could not contest. The pattern is familiar: an automated decision, a generic email, a form that loops back to itself.

For privacy work this is the other side of the data-protection coin. We spend a lot of time stopping unauthorised access. We spend much less time on lawful access by the data subject themselves when the platform decides to disagree. The piece is worth reading slowly.

ACCOUNT TAKEOVER · STORY 03

GoDaddy gave a domain to a stranger without any documentation

A domain owner watched their name be transferred away by GoDaddy support to someone who supplied no documentation and no proof of ownership. The post is methodical and the registrar's response is in the comments.

The mechanics are familiar to anyone who has watched a SIM-swap unfold. A persuasive caller, a tired support agent, a process that lets soft signals beat hard ones. The end state is the same: control of an asset moved to an attacker without a single technical exploit.

For a CISO the lesson is uncomfortable. Most identity programmes spend their energy hardening the front door — passkeys, MFA, hardware keys. This story is about the side door that the vendor opens for the attacker on request.

DIRECTLY APPLIES TO YOU
What to do

Walk through your registrar, payroll, and SaaS support recovery flows as if you were the attacker. Where can a phone call beat your MFA? Lock domains, enable transfer locks, register every executive domain in a corporate registrar account, and put the recovery email behind a hardware key. Do this before the next audit, not after the next incident.

DEEP DIVE · STORY 05

Fast16: High-precision software sabotage 5 years before Stuxnet

SentinelOne Labs drops a long technical write-up on a sabotage tool, "Fast16," that pre-dates Stuxnet by five years and ties directly to the original Shadow Brokers leak.

The piece is a careful, slow read. The authors trace artefacts, build a timeline, and place Fast16 in the lineage of state-grade ICS tooling. For a CISO the value is not the indicators of compromise — those are old. It is the evidence that high-precision sabotage capability has been part of the operating environment for two full decades.

If you are briefing a board on industrial-control or supply-chain risk this quarter, this article gives you the historical anchor you need. It is also a quietly devastating answer to anyone who still describes nation-state OT attacks as "novel."

DIRECTLY APPLIES TO YOU
What to do

Use this as briefing material. Cite Fast16 the next time a board member calls OT/ICS sabotage "theoretical." Pair it with a concrete control: do you actually have an OT asset inventory you trust, and do you know which of your suppliers do?

CLAUDE CODE · STORY 06

EvanFlow – A TDD driven feedback loop for Claude Code

A small open-source project that wraps Claude Code in a strict test-driven feedback loop. The agent only continues when the test it just wrote turns green.

EvanFlow is the kind of thing the Cowork community keeps re-inventing. The author wraps Claude Code so that every step is gated by a failing-then-passing test. The agent cannot move forward by hand-waving; the loop forces it to write a test, watch it fail, write code, watch it pass, and only then add the next behaviour.

Worth a look if your AI-assisted coding still produces "looks right, ships broken" output. The pattern generalises: you can apply the same gate to documentation, infrastructure-as-code, even policy-as-code.

DIRECTLY APPLIES TO YOU
What to do

Try it on a small repo this week. If you already run Claude Code in your CISO workflows, port one of your existing skills (an audit script, a control test, a runbook generator) into the EvanFlow loop. The constraint of "no progress without a green test" is the cheapest way to raise the floor on agent output.

ARCHITECTURE · STORY 07

Agentic AI systems violate the implicit assumptions of database design

A pointed engineering essay arguing that agents — long-running, retrying, self-correcting — break the assumptions databases were designed under.

The author's claim is simple. Classical database design assumes a small number of well-behaved clients with bounded retry behaviour. Agents are neither: they retry aggressively, fan out their reads, mutate state speculatively, and treat partial failure as a prompt to try again with slightly different inputs.

The result, the author argues, is that the constraints we relied on (idempotency tokens, backpressure, optimistic locking) need to be rebuilt as first-class features, not bolted on. This sits next to today's lead story on the agent that deleted production. Same root cause, different presentation.

DIRECTLY APPLIES TO YOU
What to do

Pair this with the lead story when you write your "AI agents in production" risk paper. The engineering layer (idempotency, lease tokens, request budgets) is the technical control that maps to the governance answer (least privilege, human-in-the-loop). You need both.

BENCHMARK · STORY 08

SWE-bench Verified no longer measures frontier coding capabilities

OpenAI publishes a candid post explaining why it is retiring SWE-bench Verified as a frontier-coding benchmark. The short version: it is now too easy.

The post is unusually honest. SWE-bench Verified was the benchmark of record for "can a model fix real GitHub issues." OpenAI argues the leaderboard has saturated to the point that small score differences no longer track real-world capability differences.

The interesting bit, for non-AI-research readers, is the implicit admission that the benchmarks ahead are messier — long-horizon agent work, ambiguous tickets, and tasks where the right answer is "ask the customer first." Those are precisely the workflows that touch security and compliance.

PERSPECTIVE · STORY 09

AI should elevate your thinking, not replace it

A 400-point essay on using AI as scaffolding for thinking rather than as a replacement for it. Quietly the most-shared piece on the site this morning.

Koshy John argues for a discipline most AI users skip: write your own draft first, then let the model edit, challenge, or extend it. The piece is short and the examples are convincing. It will land for anyone who has caught themselves reading an AI summary of something they should have read directly.

The corollary is operational. If you let an AI write your first draft of a risk assessment, an incident note, or a board paper, you are training yourself out of the job over time.

ETHICS · STORY 10

AI can cost more than human workers now

Axios reports that for several common task profiles, a 2026 frontier-model run now costs more per outcome than the equivalent contract human worker.

The numbers in the piece are a corrective to the prevailing "AI is free" narrative. For long-context tasks, multi-step agent runs, or tasks that require many tool calls, the per-outcome cost has crept above what a competent human freelancer charges for the same deliverable.

This is not the end of AI adoption — quality, latency, and 24/7 availability still matter — but it complicates the simple "replace the cheapest worker" pitch that has driven a lot of 2025 procurement. For a CISO it means the next round of "AI for SOC tier-1" proposals deserves a real cost-per-outcome model, not a flat assumption that the AI is cheaper.
