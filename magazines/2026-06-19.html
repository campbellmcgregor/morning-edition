10,000 GitHub Repositories Caught Distributing Trojan Malware

A single researcher mapped roughly ten thousand GitHub repositories quietly seeded with trojanised code — fake tools, cracked software, and game cheats engineered to look legitimate in search results and pull unsuspecting developers into running malware.

The campaign is industrial in scale and depressingly mundane in technique: automated accounts publish plausible-looking projects — password crackers, free game cheats, pirated apps — with polished READMEs and inflated star counts to game both GitHub search and Google. Click through, run the build, and you have invited an infostealer onto your machine. What makes it dangerous is not novelty but volume; ten thousand repos means a long tail that survives takedowns, and the social-engineering layer sits exactly where developers are most trusting: inside their own tooling ecosystem.

For anyone running an engineering org, this is the supply chain attack that does not need a compromised npm package to work. It only needs one curious developer searching for a shortcut. The lesson from earlier waves — typosquatted packages, poisoned VS Code extensions, fake Stack Overflow answers — is that the perimeter is now the search bar. Reputation signals like stars and forks are cheap to fake and should carry near-zero weight in a sourcing decision.

What to do

Remind engineers that GitHub stars and fork counts are trivially botted and are not a trust signal. Steer dependency sourcing to vetted, pinned, and hash-verified packages; treat any "free crack / cheat / unlocker" repo as hostile by default. If you run endpoint detection, confirm infostealer signatures are current, and make sure developer laptops are inside the same monitoring you apply to production.

Surveillance & Privacy

Are You in the Weights?

A new site lets you check whether your own writing, name, or personal details appear to have been memorised inside large language models — turning the abstract anxiety about training data into a concrete, searchable question about your own digital footprint.

The premise is simple and unsettling: models trained on the open web do not just learn patterns, they sometimes memorise specifics — quotes, biographies, contact details — and will regurgitate them under the right prompt. "Are You in the Weights?" gives ordinary people a way to interrogate that, and the answer for anyone who has published online for years is usually yes. It reframes the training-data debate away from copyright lawsuits and toward something more personal: the model may already know you, and you were never asked.

For a security leader this is less about novelty than about exposure mapping. Staff who have posted on forums, mailing lists, or social media under their real names are, in effect, indexed inside systems no one controls. That has implications for social-engineering risk, executive-impersonation attacks, and the slow erosion of the assumption that "delete" means gone. The web has always remembered; now it can also synthesise.

Forced Consent Was Unlawful — and It Cost Elkjøp €1.8M

A privacy advocate who warned electronics retailer Elkjøp five years ago that bundling marketing consent into a purchase was unlawful has been vindicated: regulators landed a €1.8M fine for exactly the "forced consent" practice he flagged.

The case is a clean illustration of a principle GDPR has stated since 2018 but enforcement has been slow to bite: consent must be freely given, which means you cannot make it a condition of a sale or a service. Elkjøp's pattern — collect marketing opt-in as part of completing a transaction — is one that countless retailers and SaaS products still quietly rely on. The five-year gap between warning and penalty is the real story; the rule was never ambiguous, only under-enforced.

That changes the risk calculus. Regulators across the EEA are increasingly willing to pursue dark-pattern consent flows, and a €1.8M penalty against a regional retailer signals the floor is rising. For any UK or EU organisation, "everyone does it" is no longer a defence, and consent strings buried in checkout flows are now an audit liability rather than a growth tactic.

What to do

Audit every place your organisation collects consent — checkout, signup, account creation — and confirm none of them gate the core service on marketing opt-in. If consent is bundled, unbundle it now. Brief marketing and product that "freely given" is a hard legal test, not a UX preference, and document the lawful basis for each processing purpose before a regulator asks.

Zero-Touch OAuth Comes to MCP

The Model Context Protocol team has published an enterprise-managed authentication design that lets organisations broker OAuth for MCP servers centrally — so AI agents and their tools authenticate through existing identity providers without users wiring up tokens by hand.

As MCP becomes the connective tissue between AI assistants and real systems, its weakest seam has been auth: every server inventing its own token dance, every user pasting credentials into config files. "Zero-touch" enterprise OAuth pushes that into the identity provider, where SSO, conditional access, and revocation already live. The agent gets scoped, auditable access; the admin gets a single place to grant and pull it. For anyone running Claude Code or Cowork against internal tooling, this is the difference between a demo and something a security team will actually sign off on.

The significance is governance, not plumbing. The moment agents can act on systems, the questions become the old IAM ones — who authorised this, what scope, can we revoke it, is it logged — and answering them through a federated identity provider rather than per-server secrets is the only model that scales. This is MCP growing up to enterprise expectations.

What to do

If you are deploying MCP servers internally, plan to front them with your existing IdP rather than per-server tokens, and require scoped, time-bounded, revocable grants. Make sure agent actions land in the same audit trail as human ones. Treat this spec as the reference design when you evaluate any MCP integration touching sensitive systems.

AI & Vulnerability Research

Greg Kroah-Hartman: AI Tools Are Now Finding Real Kernel Bugs

Linux's stable-kernel maintainer says the era of AI-generated "slop" bug reports is giving way to something more useful — AI tooling is now surfacing genuine, reproducible defects in kernel code that maintainers can act on.

For two years the open-source world has groaned under low-quality, machine-generated vulnerability reports — confident, plausible, and wrong — that waste maintainer time. Kroah-Hartman, one of the most exposed maintainers alive, marking a shift is notable precisely because he has every reason to be sceptical. The claim is not that AI replaces review but that, pointed at a codebase this large, it now finds real bugs at a rate worth the triage cost. That is a meaningful inflection for a discipline where signal-to-noise is everything.

The flip side sits with defenders. If AI can reliably find exploitable defects in the kernel, so can attackers running the same tooling at scale against everything else. The asymmetry that has long favoured well-resourced bug hunters is collapsing, and the practical takeaway is to assume your own dependencies are about to be probed harder than ever — and to get your patch cadence ahead of it.

Applied AI

Midjourney Turns Its Image Models on Medicine

The image-generation company best known for dreamlike art has announced a move into medical imaging — applying its generative models to a domain where accuracy, provenance, and regulatory scrutiny matter far more than aesthetics.

Midjourney built its reputation on visual flair, so a pivot toward medicine is a deliberate bid for legitimacy in a higher-stakes arena. Generative imaging in healthcare has real uses — augmenting sparse training datasets, simulating rare presentations, anonymising patient scans — but each carries a sharp edge. A synthetic image that looks clinically convincing but is subtly wrong is not a fun artefact; it is a diagnostic hazard. The top-of-front-page score reflects how loaded the combination of "generative AI" and "medicine" has become.

For security and governance people, the interesting questions are about data and provenance. Where does the training data come from, how is patient information protected, and can a generated image ever be mistaken for a real one in a record? As generative tools push into regulated verticals, the watermarking, audit, and consent problems that the consumer side has shrugged off become non-negotiable compliance requirements.

EU Set to Name AWS and Azure as Gatekeepers

Reports indicate Brussels is preparing to designate Amazon Web Services and Microsoft Azure as "gatekeepers," bringing the two dominant cloud platforms under the EU's strictest tier of digital-market obligations for the first time.

Gatekeeper status — the heavy-duty designation in the EU's digital-markets regime — has so far targeted consumer platforms like app stores and messengers. Extending it to hyperscale cloud is a different order of intervention, because so much of European public and private infrastructure now runs on AWS and Azure. The obligations that follow tend toward interoperability, data portability, and limits on self-preferencing — all aimed at loosening the lock-in that makes leaving a cloud provider so painful.

For organisations that have standardised on one hyperscaler, this is worth watching closely. In the near term it promises better exit ramps and fairer terms; in practice it may also bring new compliance overhead, contractual churn, and uncertainty about which services change. The wider signal — echoed in this week's separate digital-sovereignty debate — is that Europe is done treating dependence on a handful of US clouds as an acceptable default.

What to do

Add cloud-gatekeeper designation to your regulatory horizon scan and ask your AWS/Azure account teams what changes they anticipate. Use the moment to revisit exit and portability clauses in your contracts, and to honestly assess how reversible your lock-in really is. Even if you stay put, knowing the cost of leaving is now a governance question, not a hypothetical.

Infrastructure

Ubiquiti Builds an Enterprise NAS on ZFS

Ubiquiti has introduced an enterprise network-attached storage line built on ZFS, extending its prosumer-friendly UniFi ecosystem into the kind of resilient, checksummed storage that has traditionally meant TrueNAS or a hand-rolled server.

ZFS is the quiet hero of trustworthy storage: end-to-end checksumming, snapshots, and self-healing that catch the silent data corruption cheaper filesystems miss. Ubiquiti packaging it behind its familiar UniFi management is a bid to bring enterprise-grade integrity to the mid-market — the small businesses and home labs already running its networking gear. The appeal is integration: storage, network, and access control under one console, with snapshots that double as a ransomware safety net.

The caution is the same one that follows every vendor consolidation. A single management plane is convenient until it becomes a single point of compromise, and Ubiquiti has had its share of cloud-account and disclosure controversies. Snapshots are a powerful recovery tool against ransomware, but only if they are immutable and the management interface itself is locked down. Convenient infrastructure is still infrastructure that needs a threat model.

The Korean Telecom at the Centre of Anthropic's Mythos Controversy

Wired traces how SK Telecom became the focal point of a dispute over export controls and Anthropic's "Mythos" effort — a window into how AI models, the chips that train them, and national-security policy have become tangled together.

The piece pulls apart a knot that increasingly defines frontier AI: compute, export controls, and partnerships that cross geopolitically sensitive borders. A Korean telecom giant sitting at the centre of an Anthropic-linked controversy shows how decisions made in Washington about chip access ripple straight into the commercial and research plans of AI labs and their international partners. For anyone whose stack depends on a particular model provider, the supply chain behind that model is now a political object, not just a technical one.

For a security leader building on Claude or any frontier model, the lesson is dependency awareness. Model availability, pricing, and even which regions can be served are increasingly subject to forces no vendor fully controls. That argues for understanding your provider's exposure and keeping an honest sense of how portable your AI workflows would be if the geopolitical weather changed.

What to do

Map which of your workflows depend on a single AI provider and how hard they would be to move. Keep a light watching brief on export-control developments that affect model and chip availability, and factor provider geopolitical exposure into vendor risk assessments — the same way you would for any critical supplier with concentrated geographic risk.

Digital Sovereignty

The Theater of European Digital Sovereignty

An essay on "W Social" and the parade of public institutions migrating to European platforms argues that much of the continent's digital-sovereignty drive is performance — gestures that signal independence while the underlying dependence on US infrastructure quietly persists.

The author's charge is uncomfortable: European institutions love to announce sovereign alternatives — a public social network here, a national cloud there — while the servers, chips, and core services beneath them remain firmly American. The result is theatre, where the symbolism of independence substitutes for the slow, expensive work of actually building it. It is a sharp counterpoint to this morning's gatekeeper story: regulation and rhetoric both aim at the same dependence, and neither has yet dislodged it.

For a UK reader watching from outside the EU, the essay is a useful corrective against assuming any of this is simple. Genuine sovereignty means owning the stack down to layers most organisations have never had to think about, and the gap between a press release and a migration is measured in years and budgets few are willing to commit. The honest question is whether sovereignty is a goal or a brand — and the answer, mostly, is still being decided.
