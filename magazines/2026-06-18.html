Supply Chain & National Security
Directly applies to you

US holds off blacklisting DeepSeek; 100+ firms deemed security risks

Washington has paused a formal blacklisting of DeepSeek even as it flags more than a hundred firms as national-security risks, leaving Western enterprises to weigh their Chinese-AI exposure without the cover of a clear regulatory line.

The US has stopped short of adding DeepSeek to its formal entity list, even as officials circulate a roster of more than a hundred Chinese firms judged to pose national-security concerns. The restraint is pragmatic — an outright ban would be near-impossible to enforce against an open-weights model already mirrored across the internet — but it leaves a vacuum. Enterprises get the signal that DeepSeek is risky without the legal clarity of a designation that would force the issue with procurement, legal, and cyber-insurance teams.

For security leaders that ambiguity is the hard part. Shadow adoption of DeepSeek's cheap, capable models is almost certainly already happening inside most organisations — via API wrappers, IDE plugins, and self-hosted weights that never touch a vendor contract. Waiting for Washington to decide is not a control. The defensible position is to treat Chinese-origin AI the way you treat any high-risk processor: know where it runs, what data it sees, and whether a future designation would force an abrupt rip-and-replace.

What to do

Run a discovery sweep for DeepSeek usage across API gateways, code assistants, and self-hosted inference. Classify what data each touches, document a fallback model, and brief procurement and legal now so a future blacklisting is a switch-flip, not a fire drill.

Surveillance & Lockdown

Volkswagen starts blocking GrapheneOS users from its app

Volkswagen's companion app now refuses to run on GrapheneOS, the hardened Android fork, after the carmaker tightened its device-integrity checks — the latest case of attestation quietly excluding privacy-focused users from everyday services they already paid for.

Volkswagen's companion app has begun refusing to launch on GrapheneOS, the security-hardened Android fork, after the company tightened its device-integrity requirements. GrapheneOS passes the hardware-backed attestation that exists precisely for this purpose, so the block looks less like a genuine security control and more like a blunt allowlist of Google-blessed configurations. Owners who chose a more private phone now find a car they paid for partially bricked at the app layer, with no security rationale that holds up.

This is attestation creep, and it should worry anyone responsible for device strategy. The same Play Integrity mechanisms that lock out GrapheneOS can equally lock out rooted test devices, custom enterprise builds, and the privacy-preserving configurations some regulated sectors prefer. When a manufacturer can unilaterally decide which operating systems may speak to its product, the line between security and control gets thin. Expect more of it as integrity APIs spread from banking apps into cars, appliances, and beyond.

Hardware Security Regression
Directly applies to you

AMD silently removes memory encryption from consumer Ryzen CPUs

AMD has quietly stripped transparent memory encryption from recent consumer Ryzen parts, removing a defence against cold-boot and physical DMA attacks on laptops and desktops — with no announcement and only a missing capability bit to give it away.

AMD appears to have quietly dropped Secure Memory Encryption from several recent consumer Ryzen processors, with no announcement and only sharp-eyed reviewers noticing the absent capability. SME transparently encrypts system RAM, blunting cold-boot attacks and physical DMA snooping against data in memory — exactly the threats that matter when a laptop is lost, stolen, or seized at a border. Its silent removal narrows the hardware baseline that endpoint defences had quietly assumed was present.

Most fleets never explicitly enabled SME, so the regression is easy to miss — which is what makes it dangerous. Confidential-computing roadmaps, full-memory-encryption assumptions, and some disk-encryption threat models all lean on this primitive. If your standard build targets consumer Ryzen hardware, a protection you documented may simply no longer exist on this year's machines, and nobody told you. This is the kind of change that invalidates a control without ever surfacing in a CVE feed.

What to do

Audit which endpoint models in your fleet relied on AMD SME and confirm whether their replacements still expose it. Where memory encryption sits in your lost-device threat model, lean on OS-level disk encryption and consider business-grade Ryzen Pro SKUs that retain the feature.

Secure Tooling

GrapheneOS has been ported to Android 17

The hardened, de-Googled Android distribution has landed on Android 17 within weeks of release, carrying its memory-safety hardening and sandboxed Google Play onto the newest platform without forcing users to trade current patches for privacy.

GrapheneOS has been ported to Android 17 within weeks of the platform's release, carrying its memory-safety hardening, hardened malloc, and tighter sandboxing onto the latest base. The project continues to ship sandboxed Google Play as an ordinary, unprivileged app, giving users most of the app ecosystem without granting Google the system-level reach it normally enjoys. Rapid rebasing matters: it means privacy-conscious users never have to choose between current security patches and a de-Googled stack.

For security teams, GrapheneOS remains the most credible high-assurance mobile option for staff who handle sensitive material — executives, incident responders, journalists, anyone in an elevated threat model. The Android 17 port keeps it viable as a managed-device choice rather than a hobbyist curiosity. The catch is the one above: as more services adopt aggressive integrity attestation, even a more secure OS can be locked out of mundane apps, so deployment has to account for compatibility, not just hardening.

Engineering Practice

AI demands more engineering discipline, not less

Charity Majors argues that coding assistants amplify whatever rigor a team already has: without tests, observability, and review, AI-generated code simply ships defects faster and in greater volume than humans ever could alone.

Charity Majors makes a contrarian-sounding case that lands as obvious once stated: AI coding assistants do not reduce the need for engineering discipline, they raise it. A model that writes plausible code three times faster also writes plausible bugs three times faster, and review, tests, observability, and clear ownership are what stop that volume from compounding into fragility. The teams that benefit are the ones that already had the guardrails; the ones that didn't just accumulate liabilities more quickly.

The security parallel is direct. Generated code inherits whatever insecure patterns the prompt and the training data suggest, and it does so at scale, without the friction that once made developers think twice. If your SDLC controls — SAST, dependency scanning, mandatory review, secrets hygiene — were already shaky, AI-assisted development will find the gaps and widen them. The remedy is not to ban the tools but to insist the discipline scales with the throughput they unlock.

Running local models is good now

Vicki Boykis benchmarks today's open-weight models on a single laptop and finds the gap to frontier APIs has narrowed enough that local inference is now genuinely useful — and a practical way to keep sensitive data off third-party servers.

Vicki Boykis revisits local inference and finds the story has changed. Open-weight models now run usefully on a single laptop, and the quality gap to hosted frontier APIs has shrunk from embarrassing to merely noticeable for many everyday tasks — summarisation, drafting, classification, code help. The tooling has matured too, with quantised builds and runners that make a capable model a one-command download rather than a weekend project.

The security upside is the headline. Local models let sensitive data stay on the device, sidestepping the data-residency, retention, and training-on-your-inputs questions that make legal teams nervous about cloud AI. For regulated work, or for anything you simply don't want leaving the building, a local model is now a realistic control rather than a compromise. It also caps a quietly growing third-party dependency: the more workflows that run offline, the smaller your exposure when an AI vendor changes terms, prices, or availability.

What to do

Pilot a local open-weight model for use cases involving regulated or confidential data that currently can't touch cloud AI. Fold the option into your AI-use policy so staff have a sanctioned, private alternative rather than pasting secrets into a hosted chatbot.

Data Hostage

Want your images back? That'll be $5

A developer recounts how a cloud image service quietly turned its free tier into a paywall, holding years of uploaded assets hostage behind a sudden charge — a small cautionary tale about depending on opaque third-party storage with no clean way out.

A developer's write-up details how a cloud image-hosting service abruptly converted its previously free tier into a paywall, then withheld access to years of already-uploaded assets until a charge was paid. Whatever the legality, the effect is a soft form of ransom: data you depended on, fenced behind a sudden fee, with no graceful export. It is a small story that rhymes with a much bigger risk pattern.

Third-party services that hold your content can change the deal at any time, and 'free' tiers carry no obligation to let you leave cleanly. The defensive posture is unglamorous but reliable — own a copy of anything that matters, prefer providers with real export paths, and treat any single-vendor store of business data as a continuity risk. The same logic that drives off-site backups applies to every SaaS bucket your teams have quietly come to rely on.

GLM-5.2 is the new leading open-weights model

Zhipu's GLM-5.2 tops independent open-weights leaderboards, edging ahead of recent Qwen and Llama releases on reasoning and coding while remaining downloadable and self-hostable — capability an organisation can own rather than rent.

Independent benchmarking now places Zhipu's GLM-5.2 at the top of the open-weights field, ahead of the latest Qwen and Llama releases on reasoning and coding evaluations. Crucially the weights are downloadable, so the capability is something an organisation can host itself rather than rent through an API. The steady cadence of strong open models is reshaping expectations of what 'self-hostable' can deliver.

For security leaders the rise of capable open weights is double-edged. It makes private, on-premise AI more attainable — a genuine win for data control — while also handing the same power to adversaries, who can fine-tune uncensored models for phishing, malware, and social engineering without API guardrails. The takeaway is to plan for both: exploit open models where they reduce your data exposure, and assume attackers are using them to raise the quality and volume of what they send at you.

Vendor Risk & Governance
Directly applies to you

Tesco moves 40k server workloads off VMware amid Broadcom's conduct

Tesco is migrating roughly forty thousand workloads off VMware, citing Broadcom's aggressive post-acquisition licensing — a high-profile UK example of concentration risk forcing an expensive, security-sensitive platform exit on someone else's timetable.

Tesco is moving roughly forty thousand server workloads off VMware, an unusually public rebuke of Broadcom's post-acquisition licensing, which has driven steep cost increases and bundling that many large customers describe as coercive. A migration on this scale is not a quick swap; it touches networking, storage, security tooling, and operational runbooks built up over a decade — and it is being undertaken anyway, because staying put now looks like the bigger risk.

This is concentration risk made concrete, and it is squarely a CISO concern as much as a CIO one. Forced platform exits compress timelines, and rushed migrations are where misconfigurations, lapsed controls, and security regressions creep in. For UK organisations watching Broadcom's pricing, Tesco is a useful stress test: it shows the exit is feasible but costly, and that the moment to model your own VMware dependency — and the security cost of leaving it — is before the next licensing letter arrives.

What to do

Map your VMware/Broadcom exposure now: which security and operational controls assume that platform, and what would a forced exit cost in money and in risk? Build the migration threat model before commercial pressure dictates the timeline.

AI Safety

ChatGPT's image generator can be manipulated into harmful content

Security researchers at Mindgard show OpenAI's image generator can be coaxed past its guardrails into producing violent and sexual imagery, underscoring how brittle content safety remains the moment a determined user starts prompting adversarially.

Researchers at Mindgard demonstrate that OpenAI's image generator can be steered past its safety filters into producing violent and sexual content through fairly ordinary adversarial prompting. The findings are less about one model than about a persistent truth: content-safety guardrails are statistical, not absolute, and a determined user with patience and iteration can usually find the seam. As image and video generation become commodity product features, that seam is everywhere.

The ethical and operational stakes are rising together. Organisations embedding generative media into products inherit the abuse surface — brand-damaging output, harassment material, synthetic content used for fraud — and 'the vendor handles safety' is not a defence that survives contact with a motivated abuser. Treat generative features like any other untrusted path: assume the guardrails can be bypassed, log and monitor outputs, and decide deliberately what your product should refuse to make, rather than inheriting a model provider's choices by default.
