Hardware Attack
Directly applies to you

Your speakers can be turned into a BadUSB device — no physical access required

Researchers at NNS demonstrated that the audio bus on many consumer PCs can be coerced into injecting USB HID traffic, letting attackers run keystrokes and ship payloads through nothing more than a played sound file.

The trick exploits a class of audio codec chips that share signalling lines with USB controllers. A carefully constructed waveform played through the speakers — or through a malicious web page that autoplays — induces voltage transitions that the host interprets as a connected HID device. Once the PC believes a keyboard is attached, the rest is conventional BadUSB territory: scripted keystrokes, PowerShell, and remote payloads. No driver install, no permissions prompt, no physical proximity.

What makes this dangerous in a workplace context is that all the existing speaker hygiene controls are useless. USB-block policies don't help because the device is virtual. Endpoint detection sees a legitimate HID enumeration. And every meeting room with always-on hardware is now a candidate attack surface. The mitigation path is firmware-level — vendors need to seal the codec-to-HID bridge — but in the meantime, organisations should be auditing which speaker-equipped systems run unattended and watching for unexpected HID enumeration events.

What to do

Pull a list of every conference room, kiosk, and unattended workstation with internal speakers, then push EDR rules that alert on unsolicited HID device additions outside business hours. Treat the codec firmware question as a vendor inquiry this week, not next quarter. Share the NNS write-up with your SOC so the detection logic gets into next sprint.

Workplace Surveillance
Directly applies to you

Meta lets staff disappear from monitoring for 30 minutes a day

An internal policy change at Meta gives employees the option to opt out of workplace activity tracking for half an hour per day — a small concession that nonetheless sets a precedent other surveilled employers will be asked to match.

The carve-out is narrow. Tracking continues by default; the opt-out is per-day, non-cumulative, and applies only to a limited set of monitoring signals. Meta has framed it as employee wellbeing, but the backstory is regulatory pressure: EU works councils have been pushing back against blanket activity monitoring under GDPR Article 88 and the EU Platform Workers Directive, and Meta would rather offer a 30-minute valve than litigate the principle.

For CISOs and people leaders in the UK and EU, this is a small story with a large shadow. Once the largest American employer in Europe offers a no-monitoring window, the question every works council will ask is why your company can't. The right move is not to wait for the regulator or the staff petition — it is to surface the existing monitoring surface to legal and HR now and decide proactively which signals can be paused, which need DPIAs, and which were never properly disclosed in the first place.

What to do

This week, ask your IT and HR leads for a one-page inventory of every employee monitoring signal currently in use, what its lawful basis is, and where it appears in your privacy notice. If the answer takes more than a week to assemble, that is itself the finding.

Post-Quantum Crypto
Directly applies to you

Let's Encrypt publishes the first post-quantum certificate path for the open web

The free CA that issues most of the web's TLS certificates has begun shipping experimental ML-DSA-65 issuance, marking the first time a high-volume public CA has put a NIST PQC signature scheme into a production certificate chain.

The new chain is opt-in and not yet a default — clients have to specifically request a post-quantum certificate, and only a handful of browsers and TLS libraries can validate it. But Let's Encrypt issues over four million certificates a day, and once the experimental flag flips off, the migration becomes everyone's migration. The CA/B Forum has signalled that hybrid certificates (classical + PQ) will be allowed by default within twelve months.

For security teams, the practical implication is inventory. Your TLS estate includes load balancers, API gateways, mTLS service meshes, IoT bootstraps, code-signing intermediaries, and the half-forgotten reverse proxy that someone set up in 2019. Every one of those eventually needs to validate a chain it has never seen before. The teams that audit their cert handling now — particularly anything pinned, anything cached, anything that parses ASN.1 by hand — will spend Q3 making minor adjustments. The teams that don't will discover the problem when something breaks in production.

What to do

Get an inventory of every system in your environment that validates a TLS chain on something other than a current OpenSSL/BoringSSL. Pinned certs, custom truststores, hardware appliances, and embedded devices are the highest risk. Tag anything older than three years as the early-warning population.

Language Release

Elixir 1.20 ships gradual typing — and the BEAM ecosystem changes shape

The release brings set-theoretic types to a language that has lived without static checking for fifteen years, with a migration path that lets existing codebases stay untyped while new code gets compile-time guarantees.

The implementation is unusual. Rather than bolting on a Hindley-Milner system, Elixir uses set-theoretic types — the same family Python's typing draft and TypeScript's structural types come from — and infers them at function boundaries. Untyped code keeps working. Typed code gets compile-time errors. Mixed codebases are first-class. José Valim has been telegraphing this for two years; the actual landing in 1.20 makes it real.

For teams already running Phoenix or LiveBook in production, the upgrade itself is uneventful. The interesting question is what gradual typing does to the hiring market. Elixir's developer-experience advantage has always been its small but unusually disciplined community; introducing static checking makes it more legible to the wider Python and Go talent pool, which could pull the language out of its current niche or dilute the thing that made it good. Both outcomes are worth watching over the next two release cycles.

AI Red-Team
Directly applies to you

What $1,500 of LLM agent attacks against a vulnerable web app actually finds

A security researcher pointed several frontier models at a deliberately broken application, let them spend real budget, and documented exactly which classes of vulnerability the agents found, which they missed, and where they hallucinated their way past defences.

The findings are more nuanced than either the AI-bull or AI-bear camp would want. Agents reliably find IDOR, basic SQLi, and obvious auth bugs — the things a junior pentester would catch in an afternoon. They are unreliable at chained attacks requiring multi-step reasoning across pages, and they routinely fabricate exploits that look plausible in the report but don't reproduce. Total spend per useful finding was in the low triple digits, which is well above a junior consultant's day rate but well below a senior offensive engagement.

The piece is worth reading not because the conclusion is novel — agentic pentesting is roughly where automated scanners were in 2010 — but because the cost numbers are concrete. For internal security teams, that translates into a usable threshold: if your bug-bounty payouts for a finding are below the cost of an LLM agent finding it, your programme is probably leaving cheap defensive coverage on the table. The right framing is not 'replace the pentester' but 'add a continuous low-cost agent run between engagements'.

What to do

Run a one-week experiment: point Claude or GPT at your staging environment with a fixed budget cap (£500), record every finding the agent surfaces, and triage them alongside your most recent pentest report. The cost-per-true-positive number is the only one that matters; everything else is vibes.

Model Release

Google's Gemma 4 12B arrives as an encoder-free multimodal open model

The new release folds vision, audio, and text into a single transformer with no separate encoder head, and Google has shipped weights under a permissive licence that allows commercial use up to a usage threshold.

The architectural claim is the interesting part. Most open multimodal models still pair a vision encoder (often a CLIP variant) with the language model; Gemma 4 collapses that into one tokeniser-and-transformer stack, which Google's team argues simplifies fine-tuning and reduces inference latency. Early benchmark numbers put it competitive with Llama 4 70B on text reasoning and slightly ahead on image-grounded tasks, at roughly a sixth the parameter count.

For practitioners, the operational implication is real. A 12B model with strong multimodal capability fits comfortably on a single H100 or two consumer GPUs, which makes on-premises deployment financially defensible for organisations that have been waiting for an open model that doesn't require a cluster. The licence is the catch worth reading carefully — the commercial threshold is generous but not unlimited, and there is a clause about high-risk uses (biometric ID, health diagnosis) that will matter for some deployments.

AI in Education

Berkeley CS reports failing grades climbing as AI use rises and core math skills erode

Faculty in Berkeley's introductory CS sequence are reporting a sharp jump in failing exam grades, attributing it to students who can produce working code with an assistant but cannot reason through the underlying mathematics under timed exam conditions.

The piece is careful not to claim causation, but the correlation is striking. Homework completion rates are at historical highs. Exam pass rates are at historical lows. The gap is widest in courses where the assessment requires algorithmic reasoning without an interpreter — discrete math, algorithms, and the linear algebra prerequisite for systems courses. Instructors are responding with oral exams, in-class problem sets, and explicit AI-free homework, none of which scales well at Berkeley's enrolment numbers.

There is a CISO-adjacent angle worth noting. The hiring pipeline for entry-level security engineers runs through these same programmes, and the skills most degraded by always-on AI assistance — sustained reasoning about adversarial systems, comfort with formal models, the ability to hold a complex invariant in your head — are precisely the skills that mid-career security work depends on. The reasonable response is not to panic about the next graduate cohort but to invest more deliberately in apprenticeship and internal training for the next three to five years.

AI Containment
Directly applies to you

Anthropic publishes the engineering details of how it contains Claude across products

A new engineering post walks through the sandboxing, network egress controls, prompt-injection defences, and capability isolation that Anthropic applies to Claude when it is wired into tools, computers, and the browser.

The interesting parts are not the high-level principles — least privilege, defence in depth, the usual — but the concrete trade-offs. The post is unusually specific about the egress firewall policy for code execution, the kill-switch hierarchy for agentic actions, and the way capabilities are negotiated per-tool rather than per-session. For anyone building agentic workflows on top of Claude, this is the closest thing to a reference architecture the platform has published.

For Euan specifically — building on Claude Code and Cowork — the document is essentially the model for what a defensible internal Claude deployment should look like. The principles map cleanly onto an enterprise rollout: tool allow-lists, network-egress restrictions for agent sandboxes, audit trails on capability grants, and human-in-the-loop gates on irreversible actions. The places it doesn't directly answer are around multi-user audit and on-premises deployment, both of which remain open questions for regulated industries.

What to do

Read the post end-to-end and map each containment control Anthropic describes to whether you have an equivalent in your own AI tooling rollout. Anywhere you don't, that is either a deliberate risk acceptance (document it) or a gap (fix it). The egress-policy section is the highest-value 15-minute read.

AI Cost Signals
Directly applies to you

Uber capping AI tool usage at $1,500 per month per engineer reveals the real cost curve

Simon Willison's analysis of Uber's new internal cap on AI tool spend per engineer points out that the number is a useful market signal: it is where a large, well-resourced engineering organisation has decided the productivity return stops compounding.

The argument is straightforward. Uber has thousands of engineers, sees the per-seat usage data, and would not voluntarily impose a $1,500/month ceiling if engineers above that line were producing proportionally more value. The cap therefore is the company's revealed estimate of where diminishing returns set in. That number is roughly three times the most expensive consumer plan and roughly half what unbounded usage of frontier models can run to, which puts it in an interesting middle band.

For organisations that haven't set internal limits, the operational implication is that $1,500 is now the implicit benchmark. Going lower is a defensible cost-control position; going higher needs justification. The deeper question — whether per-seat pricing is even the right model for AI tools, given how variable usage is across roles — is the one budget owners will be wrestling with through the rest of the year. Allocations per project or per shippable output may end up making more sense than per-engineer caps.

What to do

Pull your current AI tool spend by user for the last 90 days. If the distribution is heavily skewed (top decile spending 5x the median), the per-seat licence model is already losing you money. Bring a usage-based proposal to finance before the next renewal cycle.

AI Philosophy

Ted Chiang on why the consciousness question is the wrong one to ask about AI

The novelist and longtime AI sceptic argues in The Atlantic that the public fixation on whether large models are conscious obscures the more useful questions about agency, responsibility, and what we actually want these systems to do for us.

Chiang's case is not the usual sceptic line. He grants that current systems are impressive, useful, and likely to keep improving. His objection is to the framing: consciousness is a question we cannot answer about other humans, let alone about systems that have no shared evolutionary history with us, and treating it as the central question lets the debate drift into theology while the practical questions about deployment go unaddressed. He prefers the framing of capability and accountability — what can the system do, who is responsible when it does it.

The essay is worth reading slowly. It is the rare piece of AI commentary that neither boosts nor doomscrolls; it asks the reader to think about the kinds of work AI systems will plausibly do in five years and what social arrangements need to exist around them. For a security leader, the practical residue is a reminder that the policy questions worth fighting for — disclosure, traceability, the right to a human reviewer — are answerable independently of whether the system has an inner life.
