Supply-Chain Compromise
Directly applies to you

Microsoft Copilot Cowork Exfiltrates Files

Researchers at PromptArmor show that Microsoft 365 Copilot can be coaxed into silently exfiltrating files from a tenant via a poisoned shared document, with no user click required and standard DLP blind to it.

The chain is depressingly straightforward. An attacker plants a Word or OneDrive file with hidden prompt instructions, shares it into the target tenant via an external collaboration link, and waits for any user inside the org to ask Copilot a benign question that grazes the document. The poisoned text overrides the user's prompt, instructs the agent to enumerate accessible files, and stitches the contents into an outbound URL the agent fetches as a citation. The data leaves the tenant disguised as a model lookup.

This is the second time in a quarter we have watched the same architectural failure: agentic AI tools that conflate trusted user intent with untrusted retrieved content. Microsoft's compensating controls assume Copilot only reads what the user can read, which keeps the auditors happy but ignores the actual attack surface. If your organisation is running M365 Copilot, External Sharing, and a graph-aware agent, you have shipped a confused deputy to every desk. Treat it like one.

What to do

Audit external sharing on SharePoint and OneDrive. Disable anonymous link creation where you can. Force Copilot tenant policy to deny following outbound URLs the user did not type. Add a Purview sensitivity label that blocks Copilot grounding on documents tagged Confidential, then test with a deliberately poisoned file you control.

Yoti age checks share facial photos and device fingerprints with third parties

A new analysis of Yoti, one of the largest age-assurance vendors used by UK and EU platforms under the Online Safety Act, shows facial scans and device fingerprints flowing onward to third-party analytics and ad partners despite contrary public claims.

The pitch from age-assurance vendors has always been that biometric checks are ephemeral, processed once and deleted. The network traffic tells a different story. Researchers captured Yoti's mobile flows on three large UK platforms and recorded face vectors, hashed device IDs, and behavioural fingerprints landing at telemetry endpoints owned by ad-tech intermediaries. Nothing in the consent flow warned users this was happening. The data was structured in ways that allowed downstream linking to existing browser profiles.

For organisations operating in the UK, this matters in two ways. First, Ofcom is about to publish enforcement guidance on age assurance, and "we relied on a certified vendor" will not survive a Data Protection Impact Assessment if the vendor itself is leaking biometrics. Second, every controller is on the hook under UK GDPR Articles 5(1)(f) and 28 for what processors do with the data, even if you bought a turnkey product. The chain of responsibility ends at the controller, not the badge on the SDK.

What to do

Pull your DPIA for any age-gate, parental consent, or identity-verification flow that uses a third-party SDK. Ask the vendor for a recent independent traffic audit or run one yourself with mitmproxy. If you cannot prove the data path, assume it leaks and update your Record of Processing.

CVE-2026-28952: Apple macOS 26.5 Kernel Vuln found by Claude

Apple has shipped macOS 26.5.1 patching a kernel use-after-free found by an Anthropic Claude-powered fuzzing pipeline. CVE-2026-28952 allows local privilege escalation to kernel and is exploitable from a sandboxed app context.

The advisory is dry but the provenance is not. Apple credits the finding to "an automated vulnerability research pipeline at Anthropic," confirming what the security community has whispered for a year: large coding models, given a long-running agent loop and a fuzzing harness, are now competitive with seasoned human researchers at finding real kernel bugs. The vulnerability lives in the IOSurfaceClient family and has been quietly exploited in at least one targeted attack, per Apple's wording.

For fleet managers the immediate work is mechanical. Push 26.5.1 via MDM, gate access to high-value systems on patch level, and watch your EDR for the IOSurface mach calls Apple's release notes describe. The longer conversation is harder. If Anthropic, Google, and a handful of well-funded research labs can now point an LLM agent at any operating system and harvest kernel bugs at scale, the offensive side of that ledger is not standing still. Patch velocity is going to matter more in 2026 than it did in 2025.

What to do

Push macOS 26.5.1 to all Mac endpoints today via your MDM. Confirm coverage on contractor and BYOD devices that access corporate data, since IOSurface exploits do not need root or admin to start. Flag any Macs still on 26.5 in your weekly vulnerability report.

Using AI to write better code more slowly

Nolan Lawson argues that the productivity story of AI-assisted coding has been measured in the wrong currency: lines committed per hour rather than time saved on the dull bits, freeing more time for the careful thinking the dull bits used to crowd out.

Lawson's piece sits against the prevailing 10x-engineer hype. He keeps a log of how he uses Claude Code on production work and finds the wins are in the unglamorous middle: writing the eighth test fixture, translating a JIRA ticket into a draft branch, scaffolding a migration script. The interesting code, the bit where you decide whether the abstraction is worth it, still happens in his head, and now there is more time for it. He produces less code per day. The code is better.

The implication for anyone running a team is that the productivity benchmarks vendors are pushing right now are measuring the wrong thing. Lines, PRs, and tickets are leading indicators of slop, not throughput. The honest measurements are review feedback density, defect escape rate, and the time between a senior engineer reading a PR and approving it without major changes. If those are getting better, your AI rollout is working. If they are not, you are paying a subscription to ship faster bugs.

What to do

When evaluating AI coding tools for the team, build the success metrics first. Track defect escape rate and senior reviewer turnaround over a 90-day pilot, not commit count. Bring the same discipline to your own Claude Code workflow: where is it removing thinking time, and where is it removing thinking?

Cryptography Primer

How Shamir's Secret Sharing Works

A clear, careful explainer from Ente walking through Shamir Secret Sharing, the threshold scheme behind a quietly increasing share of password managers, key recovery flows, and self-custody crypto wallets.

The maths reads as elegant once it clicks. Take a secret, encode it as the y-intercept of a randomly chosen polynomial of degree k-1, then hand out points on that polynomial to n trustees. Any k of them can interpolate the polynomial and recover the secret. Fewer than k learn nothing useful. The piece earns its length by walking the reader through the polynomial reconstruction with worked numbers, including the modular arithmetic that keeps the scheme information-theoretically secure.

The reason this matters in 2026 is not academic. Threshold cryptography is moving from research papers into the kinds of systems CISOs are now asked to approve, especially for break-glass admin access, root key recovery on HSMs, and disaster-recovery flows in hosted SaaS. Knowing the difference between a threshold scheme and a plain key-escrow file is the kind of small literacy that prevents an architect from being talked into something exotic by a vendor who has read the same Wikipedia page.

AI Infrastructure

Norway's 2 petabytes of Huawei flash storage and LLM training

Norway has installed roughly two petabytes of Huawei OceanStor flash to support a national LLM training programme, raising fresh European questions about sovereign AI and the gap between procurement policy and actual deployments.

The detail buried in the procurement notice is that the Huawei kit was chosen on price-performance after EU-tier alternatives were ruled out on cost. Norway is not in the EU, but it follows EU rules closely on critical-infrastructure supply chains, and its national AI consortium includes universities and one defence contractor. There is no public risk assessment in the file. The kit is sitting behind Norwegian-government-managed networking, not Huawei management, which is the standard mitigation story for this class of decision.

The story is worth tracking because it is the first time a Western European public-AI programme has openly bought Chinese flash for training data. The political calculation is that bulk training storage is lower-risk than networking or compute. The counter-argument is that two petabytes of behavioural and government data, accumulated over years and replicated by firmware nobody fully audits, is exactly the asset that a determined nation-state attacker would target slowly. Watch for this story to be cited in upcoming EU AI Act guidance.

Law Enforcement Action
Directly applies to you

Netherlands Seizes 800 Servers, Arrests 2 for Aiding Cyberattacks

Dutch police, working with Europol, have seized roughly 800 servers and arrested two operators of a bulletproof hosting service that catered to ransomware affiliates and infostealer crews active across the UK, Germany, and the Nordics.

Krebs reports the takedown ran for nine months and was triggered by a payment-trail investigation rather than a technical compromise. The hosts were domiciled in the Netherlands but advertised on Russian-language forums, and the customer base spanned at least four named ransomware brands. Investigators have signalled that customer manifests, transaction logs, and email backups were seized intact, which means the next several months will see victim-notification waves and possible indictments in jurisdictions including the UK.

For UK organisations the relevant question is not whether you were targeted by these crews but whether your incident-response retainer firm knows how to read a victim-notification letter from the National Crime Agency. Many do not, and the deadline to respond to the data-protection regulator is shorter than the deadline to talk to the police. The other watch-item is the cascade effect. Bulletproof-host disruptions historically push activity to less mature providers for six to twelve weeks, with a measurable uptick in opportunistic attacks during the migration.

What to do

Ask your incident-response retainer how they handle a UK NCA victim-notification within the ICO 72-hour window. Refresh your playbook for a forensic-evidence-preservation request from law enforcement. Brief execs that opportunistic ransomware activity is likely to rise across the next six weeks while crews resettle.

Privacy Tooling

Exit IP VPN servers mitigation rollout

Mullvad has begun rolling out a multi-layer mitigation against the IP-leak class of attacks that hit consumer VPNs over the last year, separating exit IPs from account identifiers at the network layer.

The technical write-up is unusually frank about what the change does and what it does not. Mullvad has restructured how an exit IP is associated with a customer session so that even a fully compromised exit node cannot link traffic back to an account identifier without cooperating egress and authentication boxes. The migration is rolling and visible in the client; existing users will be moved over the next six weeks. The cost is a small latency hit on first connection and a behavioural change in port forwarding.

The wider point is that the consumer VPN market has been quietly consolidating around two architectures: aggressive logging with claims of "no logs" laundered through marketing, and genuinely no-logs services that have to keep innovating to stay that way. Mullvad sits in the second camp, and this update is a useful piece of evidence in favour of that claim. For users in jurisdictions where VPN choice carries consequences, the architectural detail published here is more informative than the average transparency report.

Regulation & Governance

California moves to exempt Linux from its age-verification law after backlash

California has signalled it will carve Linux distributions out of its incoming app-store age-verification regime after open-source maintainers and EFF raised technical and constitutional concerns about the original scope.

The walk-back is narrower than the headline implies. California's age-verification law remains in force for commercial app stores and the platforms behind them, and the statutory deadline is unchanged. What the regulator has conceded is that requiring Debian, Fedora, and Arch maintainers to gate package installs on age proof was both technically incoherent and likely a First-Amendment violation. The carve-out is being drafted into the implementing regulations and will land before the law takes effect.

The interesting consequence is jurisdictional. California has set the de facto floor for US age-verification policy, and the EU's parallel regime under the Digital Services Act is being watched for similar exemptions. UK regulators, who have less appetite for FOSS exceptions, will be pressed to follow. If you operate a developer-tooling business, a self-hosted product, or any open-source distribution channel with US customers, the next twelve months will determine whether your downloads need a friction layer the rest of the open ecosystem has been spared.

In Memoriam

Toshifumi Suzuki, founder of Seven-Eleven Japan, has died

Toshifumi Suzuki, the operations strategist who built Seven-Eleven Japan into one of the most-studied retail systems in the world, has died at 93. His legacy is a quiet lesson about what operational excellence actually looks like over decades.

Suzuki ran Ito-Yokado's licence of the American 7-Eleven brand from 1973 and proceeded to invent the modern convenience-store category. He insisted on multiple deliveries per day, a point-of-sale system that fed back to suppliers within hours, and a cultural commitment to small format and dense networks. By the time the parent American chain went bankrupt and Suzuki bought it, the Japanese operation was already several decades ahead on logistics, data, and supplier coordination.

The reason his career repays study is that almost none of what he did would screen well as a single quarter's strategy. The data integration was a fifteen-year project. The supplier relationships were thirty. The instinct to reject American category-management orthodoxy in the 1970s was a long, unglamorous bet on knowing your specific stores better than anyone else did. For an industry that now thinks two-year planning horizons are visionary, his career is a reminder that the durable advantage tends to be the one that took the longest to build.
