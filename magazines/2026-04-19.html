Binary Dependencies: The Hidden Packages We All Depend On

⚠ Directly Applies to You

New research maps the invisible web of binary dependencies that underpin software supply chains, revealing packages that millions of projects silently rely on without declaring them.

The concept of supply chain risk has expanded well beyond direct package.json or requirements.txt entries. This analysis digs into the binaries that get compiled, linked and shipped as part of builds but rarely appear in any SBOM. Think native crypto libraries, image decoders and compression tools that exist only as compiled artefacts in CI pipelines. The research identifies specific packages that sit in critical paths across thousands of projects but receive minimal security scrutiny or funding.

For security teams, this is the gap between what your dependency scanner sees and what actually runs in production. The xz-utils incident in 2024 showed what happens when one of these invisible load-bearing packages gets compromised. This work systematically catalogues where the next one could be hiding.

What to do

Audit your CI/CD pipelines for binary dependencies that don't appear in your SBOM. Cross-reference this research with your own build artefacts. Consider whether your current dependency scanning covers compiled binaries, not just declared packages. Flag this for your next supply chain risk review.

EU Age Verification App: "Worry-Free Package" Ships with Security Vulnerabilities

⚠ Directly Applies to You

The European Union's official age verification app, designed to protect minors online, has been found to contain security vulnerabilities that could expose the very users it aims to protect.

Heise reports that the EU's age verification solution, marketed as a "worry-free package" for parents and platforms, contains flaws that could allow data leakage or impersonation. This is a particularly damaging finding because age verification systems handle some of the most sensitive identity data possible: proof of a person's age often requires government ID documents, biometric data or both.

The irony is thick. A system built to protect children's privacy contains the kind of vulnerabilities that could expose their identity data. This feeds directly into the ongoing debate about whether mandatory age verification creates more risk than it mitigates. For organisations operating in the EU/UK that were considering integration with this system, this is a red flag.

What to do

If your organisation processes age verification data or was evaluating the EU's solution, pause integration until the vulnerabilities are patched and independently verified. Review your own age-gating mechanisms for similar issues. This also strengthens the case for privacy-preserving age estimation over document-based verification. Brief your DPO.

Keep Pushing: 10 More Days to Reform Section 702

⚠ Directly Applies to You

The EFF reports a critical 10-day window has opened to push for meaningful reform of Section 702 of FISA, the US surveillance authority that enables warrantless collection of communications data, including from non-US persons.

Section 702 is the legal basis for some of the most expansive surveillance programmes operated by the NSA and FBI. While it technically targets non-US persons, it inevitably sweeps up communications from US citizens and, critically for UK and EU organisations, captures data flowing through US-based services. Every time your organisation's data touches a US cloud provider, Section 702 is the authority under which it can be accessed without a warrant.

The reform window matters because the current reauthorisation included minimal privacy protections. The EFF and allied organisations are pushing for warrant requirements for US person queries, limits on "backdoor searches" and greater transparency. Whether these reforms succeed affects the legal risk calculus for any organisation storing data with US providers.

What to do

Monitor Section 702 reform outcomes for impact on your data sovereignty posture. If your organisation relies on US cloud services, this directly affects your GDPR adequacy arguments and UK data protection compliance. Consider whether your current data residency controls adequately address 702 collection risks. Factor reform outcomes into upcoming vendor risk assessments.

SPEAKE(a)R: Turn Speakers to Microphones for Fun and Profit

> Researchers demonstrate that speakers in PCs and smart devices can be silently repurposed as microphones, enabling covert audio surveillance even when no microphone is present.

This 2017 paper from USENIX WOOT resurfaced on HN today and remains deeply relevant. The researchers from Ben-Gurion University showed that speakers and headphones can be used as makeshift microphones by exploiting the reversibility of audio transducers. The RealTek audio codec chip, found in most PC motherboards, allows software to silently reassign the headphone jack from output to input, turning connected speakers into listening devices.

The attack works without any hardware modification. A piece of malware can flip the codec configuration, record audio through the speakers and exfiltrate it. This is particularly concerning for air-gapped environments where microphones may be physically removed or disabled as a security measure, since speakers remain connected. The research demonstrated intelligible speech capture from several metres away using standard desktop speakers.

Zero-Copy GPU Inference from WebAssembly on Apple Silicon

A technical deep-dive into running ML inference directly from WebAssembly with zero-copy GPU memory access on Apple Silicon, eliminating expensive data transfers between CPU and GPU.

This is a genuinely interesting technical achievement. The author demonstrates how to run neural network inference from within a WebAssembly sandbox while maintaining zero-copy access to Apple's unified memory architecture. In practical terms, this means WASM-sandboxed ML models can achieve near-native performance on M-series chips without the overhead of copying tensors between memory spaces.

The implications are significant for edge AI deployment. WebAssembly provides a security sandbox and portability layer, and eliminating the GPU memory copy bottleneck makes it viable for real-time inference workloads. This could enable a new class of browser-based or edge-deployed ML applications that previously required native code for acceptable performance.

Michael Rabin Has Died

Michael O. Rabin, Turing Award winner and one of the foundational figures of theoretical computer science and cryptography, has passed away.

Rabin's contributions to the field are hard to overstate. His work with Dana Scott on nondeterministic finite automata earned them the 1976 Turing Award. But for the security community specifically, his contributions to cryptography are what matter most. The Rabin cryptosystem, published in 1979, was the first public-key encryption scheme proven to be as hard to break as integer factorisation. His work on randomised algorithms laid the groundwork for the probabilistic methods that underpin modern cryptographic protocols.

He also made pioneering contributions to information-theoretically secure communication and oblivious transfer, concepts that are now central to secure multi-party computation. Every time you use a system that relies on the computational hardness of factoring, Rabin's work is part of the foundation it stands on. A giant of the field.
