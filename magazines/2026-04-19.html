EU Age Verification App Ships with Security Vulnerabilities

Directly Applies to You

The EU's official age verification app, marketed as a "worry-free package" for protecting minors online, has been found to contain significant security vulnerabilities. The irony is as thick as the flaws are deep.

Heise reports that security researchers have discovered vulnerabilities in the EU's age verification application, a tool designed to help platforms comply with the Digital Services Act and upcoming age-gating requirements. The app handles sensitive biometric and identity data for minors and adults alike, making these flaws particularly concerning.

The vulnerabilities reportedly include issues with data handling and authentication that could expose personal information. For a tool whose entire purpose is to protect vulnerable users, shipping with security holes undermines the credibility of the regulatory framework it supports. It also raises familiar questions about government-mandated technology being held to lower security standards than the private sector it aims to regulate.

What to do

If your organisation operates in the EU or serves EU users, monitor this closely. Any integration with EU age verification infrastructure should be paused pending a full security assessment. Review your own age-gating mechanisms and ensure they do not depend on this app's APIs or data flows. Brief your DPO on the implications for GDPR compliance if this tool is part of your data processing chain.

EFF: 10 More Days to Reform Section 702 Surveillance

The Electronic Frontier Foundation is pushing for reform of Section 702 of FISA, the US government's most powerful mass surveillance authority. A 10-day window has opened for meaningful change.

Section 702 authorises the US government to collect communications of non-US persons located outside the United States, but in practice it sweeps up enormous volumes of communications involving Americans and, critically, anyone communicating with US-based services. That includes most of the world's business communications.

The EFF is calling for reforms including requiring warrants for searches of US person data, closing the "backdoor search" loophole, and adding meaningful oversight mechanisms. For organisations outside the US, Section 702 is one of the primary legal mechanisms that makes US cloud services a data protection risk under GDPR. Any reform (or lack thereof) directly impacts the legal basis for transatlantic data transfers.

The 10-day extension is both an opportunity and a signal that there is political will to act. Whether that will translates to meaningful reform remains uncertain.

Binary Dependencies: The Hidden Packages We All Depend On

Directly Applies to You

A deep investigation into binary dependencies reveals the invisible packages underpinning modern software, many of which receive minimal security scrutiny despite being critical infrastructure.

Most software supply chain analysis focuses on source-level dependencies: npm packages, PyPI libraries, Go modules. But beneath that layer sit binary dependencies, compiled packages distributed by operating systems and package managers that are harder to audit, harder to track and often more deeply embedded in production systems.

The research identifies packages that appear in nearly every production environment but receive disproportionately little attention from security teams. These are the invisible foundations: compression libraries, certificate handling utilities, DNS resolvers. When one of these fails (think Heartbleed, or the XZ Utils backdoor attempt), the blast radius is enormous precisely because nobody was paying attention.

This work provides a framework for identifying which binary dependencies carry the most risk and deserve the most scrutiny from security teams.

What to do

Add binary dependency scanning to your SBOM process. Most organisations track source dependencies but have a blind spot at the OS package level. Review your container base images and identify which binary packages are included. Cross-reference against this research to prioritise which deserve active monitoring. Raise this with your engineering teams as a gap in current supply chain risk management.

SPEAKE(a)R: Turning Speakers into Covert Microphones

A 2017 USENIX paper resurfaces on HN, demonstrating how speakers can be repurposed as microphones to eavesdrop on conversations, bypassing traditional audio security controls.

The research, conducted by Mordechai Guri and colleagues at Ben-Gurion University, demonstrates a technique called SPEAKE(a)R that exploits the fact that speakers and microphones are physically similar transducers. By using a computer's audio codec to silently reconfigure speaker jacks as input channels, an attacker can turn passive speakers into active listening devices.

This matters for air-gapped environments and facilities where microphone access is physically restricted. If your threat model includes nation-state actors or insider threats in sensitive environments, the assumption that "we removed the microphones" may not be sufficient. The attack works on standard PC audio hardware without any hardware modification.

The paper is nine years old but its resurgence on HN suggests the security community is re-evaluating physical security assumptions in an era of increasingly sophisticated surveillance. For organisations handling classified or highly sensitive discussions, this is a reminder that audio security requires more than removing obvious recording devices.

College Instructor Turns to Typewriters to Curb AI-Written Work

A Colorado college instructor has resorted to typewriters in the classroom, requiring students to compose on mechanical keyboards as a response to the proliferation of AI-generated submissions.

With 329 points and 325 comments, this story has sparked a vigorous debate about the appropriate response to AI in education. The instructor's approach is deliberately analogue: by removing the digital layer entirely, they eliminate the possibility of AI assistance during composition.

The HN discussion is more nuanced than the headline suggests. Many commenters note that this approach teaches valuable skills (thinking before writing, working without a delete key, composing complete thoughts) while others argue it is a retreat from reality. The real question is not whether typewriters are a good pedagogical tool, but what it means when institutions resort to eliminating technology rather than adapting to it.

For security and technology leaders, this is a governance parable. Every organisation is facing some version of this question: do you ban AI tools, restrict them, or embrace them with guardrails? The typewriter approach is the educational equivalent of blocking ChatGPT at the firewall. It works in the short term but does not prepare anyone for the world they are entering.

Towards Trust in Emacs: Rethinking Software Integrity

A proposal for improving trust and integrity in the Emacs package ecosystem tackles fundamental questions about how we verify that the software we run is the software we intended to run.

Eshel Yaron's post examines the trust model of Emacs packages, where users routinely execute arbitrary Lisp code from community repositories with minimal verification. The proposal outlines a path toward cryptographic signing, reproducible builds and transparent dependency tracking for the Emacs ecosystem.

While this is specifically about Emacs, the underlying problem is universal. Every package ecosystem, from npm to PyPI to MELPA, faces the same fundamental challenge: how do you establish trust in code written by strangers? The XZ Utils incident demonstrated that even widely-used, seemingly trustworthy packages can be compromised through patient social engineering.

The Emacs community's approach is worth watching because it starts from first principles rather than bolting security onto an existing system. The discussion around reproducible builds, signature verification and trust anchors mirrors conversations happening in every serious software supply chain security effort. Small ecosystems can move faster than large ones, making Emacs a potential proving ground for ideas that could propagate to larger package managers.
