Supply Chain Breach

Vercel Says Internal Systems Hit in Breach

Directly applies to you

Vercel confirms unauthorised access to internal systems, raising supply-chain exposure for every team whose deploys touch the platform. Scope still being investigated.

Vercel said on Friday that attackers reached internal systems and are cooperating with law enforcement while the investigation continues. The company is a dependency for a very large slice of modern web deploys, edge functions and previews, so even a narrow breach casts a long supply-chain shadow.

Initial reporting points to credential compromise rather than a product vulnerability, but Vercel has not yet published the affected systems, the dwell time, or whether customer build artefacts were touched. Expect more detail as forensics complete. Treat every access token, environment variable and deploy hook that ever touched a Vercel account as potentially handled by an attacker until proven otherwise.

This one matters because the blast radius is not Vercel's own infrastructure, it is every app that was built, signed, or deployed through it.

What to do

  • Rotate Vercel API tokens, deploy hooks, and any env-var secrets stored in project settings.
  • Audit GitHub/GitLab integrations linked to Vercel and revoke stale OAuth grants.
  • Pull Vercel audit logs for the last 30 days, focusing on team member adds, role changes, and integration installs.
  • If you sign artefacts or publish packages from Vercel builds, assume the signing material is suspect and reissue.

Surveillance

Airline worker arrested after sharing photos of bomb damage in WhatsApp group

Dubai police seized a private WhatsApp group and arrested an airline worker for sharing photos of bomb damage. The chat log was the evidence.

The Insider reports that Dubai authorities obtained messages from a WhatsApp group used by airline staff, leading to the arrest of a worker who had shared images of bomb damage. The group was private and end-to-end encrypted.

Officials have not said how the messages were obtained. Possibilities range from a compromised handset, to a cooperating group member, to a commercial mobile-device forensics tool. Each reading carries a different lesson, but the one constant is that 'encrypted' never means 'unreachable.' Endpoint compromise, cloud backups, and insider access all remain paths into chats that users assume are sealed.

For anyone advising staff on what is safe to discuss on a personal device, this is another reminder that the threat model is the device, not the app.

Data Protection Alert

Notion leaks email addresses of all editors of any public page

Directly applies to you

A researcher shows that any public Notion page reveals the email addresses of every editor who ever touched it. No authentication required.

The disclosure, shared on Twitter by researcher weezerOSINT, demonstrates that the Notion API surface returns editor email addresses for any publicly shared page. That means every customer-facing doc, public wiki, or 'share with anyone' page that a team has ever published is effectively a staff directory for anyone who cares to scrape it.

The vector is trivial, the impact is quietly enormous. Phishing campaigns thrive on verified corporate emails, and the editor list for a public roadmap or hiring page is often a clean map of the team. Notion has not yet issued a public statement at the time of writing.

Under UK GDPR and the EU's equivalent regime, employee email addresses are personal data. Leaking them through a public API surface is a textbook Article 32 control failure and likely a notifiable incident depending on volume and downstream use.

What to do

  • Inventory every Notion page your organisation has set to 'Share to web.' The setting is per-page and surprises most teams.
  • Assume editor emails on those pages are already harvested. Warn affected staff about targeted phishing.
  • Add Notion public-share events to your DLP and SIEM monitoring if the plan tier supports it.
  • Document the decision and timing in your incident log; a UK/EU supervisory authority will ask.

Infrastructure

Migrating from DigitalOcean to Hetzner

A detailed walkthrough of moving production workloads from DigitalOcean to Hetzner, with real numbers on cost and performance. The top story on HN today with 847 points.

The post walks through a full migration: why the move, how the network was cutover without downtime, and what the bill looks like on the other side. The short version is that Hetzner's price-per-core remains the outlier it has been for years, and for steady-state workloads the savings are dramatic.

The more interesting sections cover the stuff that usually gets glossed over in cost-comparison posts: DNS TTL discipline, database replication across providers, object storage egress bills during the cutover, and the limits of each provider's managed services.

Worth a read for anyone re-evaluating cloud spend in 2026. The post is candid about Hetzner's weaker edges too, including support response times and a more Spartan console.

Investigation

Russia's doping program is run by the same FSB team that poisoned Navalny

The Insider's open-source analysis links Russia's state doping program to the same FSB unit that poisoned Alexei Navalny. A study in how adversary units pivot between missions.

The Insider and Bellingcat's joint investigation traces travel records, phone metadata, and unit affiliations to show that the FSB's Criminalistics Institute, long linked to the Navalny poisoning, also ran the logistics behind Russia's Olympic doping scheme.

For threat intel readers, the methodology is the lesson. Small-scale open-source tools, consistently applied over years, can deanonymise state-sponsored operators with the precision that used to require classified collection. The same techniques of call-detail record correlation, airline manifests and facial recognition on leaked databases now underpin some of the most detailed public attribution work being done.

It also underlines a point that CISOs tracking nation-state activity already know: the units that run kinetic operations are not siloed from the ones that run cyber. Personnel, infrastructure, and tradecraft are shared.

What makes this useful rather than yet-another-benchmark is the lens. Chambers is measuring the cost of real agentic patterns, not just single-turn Q&A. That means the findings translate reasonably well to the kind of workloads Claude Code and Cowork run at scale.

If you are picking a default model for a production agent loop this week, the data here is worth twenty minutes.

Critical CVE

Critical flaw in Protobuf library enables JavaScript code execution

Directly applies to you

A critical flaw in the Protobuf library allows attackers to execute arbitrary JavaScript code through crafted messages. Bleeping Computer has the breakdown.

The vulnerability affects the JavaScript implementation of Google's Protobuf library, widely used across backend services, SDKs and browser clients. A specially crafted protobuf message can cause the parser to execute attacker-controlled JavaScript on the host, turning a serialisation library into a remote code execution primitive.

Patched versions are available now. The maintainers recommend immediate upgrade; there is no known workaround that fully mitigates the issue without the patch.

The class of issue, RCE in a serialiser that everyone imports transitively, is exactly the supply-chain profile that has caused the worst incidents of the last two years. Most organisations do not know where protobuf-javascript ends up in their dependency tree, which is the problem.

What to do

  • Run an SBOM scan for protobufjs and google-protobuf in every JavaScript and Node service. Include front-end bundles.
  • Prioritise patching internet-facing services and any service that parses protobuf from untrusted sources.
  • Check for exploitation attempts in WAF logs; payloads will look like unusually structured POST bodies or gRPC-Web traffic.
  • Brief the on-call on-call engineer; this is the kind of CVE that will see exploitation within days.

Directly applies to you

Sam Henri Gold, until recently at Anthropic, publishes a long-form reflection on the design system behind Claude and the trade-offs of designing for a non-deterministic product.

The essay is a rare inside look at how a design team shapes the surface of a model. Gold covers why Claude's interface leans as plain as it does, how tone and restraint translate into visual decisions, and the quiet work of deciding what the model should not look like.

For anyone building on Claude, the piece reads less like a retrospective and more like a specification. The patterns Gold describes are the ones you end up reimplementing anyway, badly, if you ship your own Claude-backed product without them.

What to do

  • Note which patterns Gold calls out as load-bearing and audit your own agent UX against them.

Policy & Governance

Keep Pushing: We Get 10 More Days to Reform Section 702

The EFF has ten more days to push reforms to Section 702 surveillance authorities. What the fight looks like and why it still matters for non-US organisations.

Section 702 is the US authority that permits warrantless collection of communications between US persons and foreign targets. Every meaningful reform cycle comes down to a handful of procedural windows, and the EFF's post maps the current one: what amendments are still in play, which senators are movable, and what a weak reauthorisation looks like in practice.

For UK and EU organisations the relevance is indirect but real. Section 702 is the authority that sits underneath the adequacy decisions governing transatlantic data flows. A weak reauthorisation, or worse an expansion, tips another round of Schrems-style litigation back into the queue and the compliance implications for data transfer will land on European privacy programmes within months.

AI & Education

College instructor turns to typewriters to curb AI-written work

A college instructor moves his writing class to typewriters, a quiet protest against AI-assisted work and a broader argument about what education is for.

The instructor's argument is not nostalgia. It is that writing is the thinking, and outsourcing the writing means outsourcing the thinking, and after two years of trying to design AI-resistant assignments the only honest option left was to remove the machine from the room.

The sentinel that AI-in-education discourse has settled into is mostly economic: how much does the degree still cost, how much value remains. The quieter question is what kind of minds the next decade is training, and what is lost when the friction of composing a sentence is removed from the formative years.

Reasonable people will disagree about whether typewriters are the answer. The underlying concern, that the tool is reshaping the student faster than the curriculum can adapt, is harder to wave away.
